To do so, we will compare two different ways of making the execution time of an algorithm faster, one is having a faster computer. The other is designing an algorithm with a running time with a slower growth, which one has a bigger impact a faster computer or a faster algorithm. Let's say you have designed one algorithm to solve a problem and execution time of this algorithm has a quadratic growth. That is, the running time grows with N to the square where N is the input data size. Let's assume that in your current computer every time unit is equal to one nanosecond. In this table you can see the estimated execution times of your algorithm in your current computer. Remember we are interested in the running time of the algorithm for big input sizes. What about a computer ten times faster? Let's assume a computer this fast is available in the market. So before buying it you do your calculations. You can see the results in the third column of the table. Well, these results are not very encouraging. Although you have decreased you're running times by ten times for 100 million elements of input data you still have to wait for almost 12 days to get your results. Of course it's an improvement, but it's not enough. Let's stretch things further and estimate the execution time with a computer that is 100 times faster than your current computer. Well in this case again you decrease the execution time significantly, but a weight of one point two days for your results might still be unacceptable. Besides a computer 100 times faster is not going to be available in the market anytime soon. Faced with this situation you decide to approach the problem a fresh and try to design a second version of your algorithm in such a way that the running time grows slower than N the square. Imagine you have managed to design a second version with a linear growth. That is the running time grows linearly with the input data size, you do the calculations are realized that having a a faster algorithm immediately solves your problem. In this table you can see that for an input data size of 100 million elements the second version of the algorithm solves the problem in a much shorter time, just a fraction of a second. As this is not an exact value we cannot say that the new algorithm is going to take exactly 0.1 seconds. But we can say with certainty that the linear algorithm is going to provide a much faster solution than buying a computer that it's 100 times faster than our current computer. On this plot you can see how the execution time of the algorithm with a quadratic growth only moves down with a faster computer, but its growth does not change. On the other hand the linear algorithm grows much lower and that makes all the difference. So the key message of this lecture, is that investing time in a faster algorithm is always better than investing money on a faster computer. Of course, there are some problems that can't be solved by designing an algorithm with a linear growth or even slower. That is why it's so important for you to know about the algorithms that exist for some classical problems in computer science and the best growth you can expect from them. Usually very big instances of problems can only be solved with algorithms who's running times grow with NlogN and and slower. If you cannot find an algorithm with a growth this is slow, then you must be aware that you will only be able to solve small instances of your problem.

To introduce you to the topic, let's start with a bit of history. If you look at Europe using Google Maps, you will find that between Poland and Lithuania, there's a Russian exclave called Kaliningrad. If you zoom in, you will eventually see a long river, the Pregel River. At some point, this river encloses a piece of land, forming an island. In the 18th century, Kaliningrad was not called Kaliningrad, but Konigsberg, and Konigsberg had seven bridges around the river in that part of the city. Here, I have circled the seven bridges of Konigsberg so you can see them clearly. At that time, there was a mathematical problem known as the Seven Bridges of Konigsberg. The problem consisted in answering the following question: can you walk the city crossing every bridge exactly once? The question had been around for a while and then in 1735, Leonhard Euler showed that this was not possible. For his demonstration, Euler used an abstraction of the river, the land pieces, and the bridges. He named the different land parts, A, B, C, and D, then he depicted how they were connected by the different bridges using this abstraction. In this abstract representation of the bridges, every circle is a piece of land and every line connecting a pair of circles is a bridge. This visual representation of an interconnected system using circles and lines is called a graph. In graph terminology, every circle is called a node or vertex, and every line is called a link or edge. Nowadays, graphs are powerful tools used to analyze and solve problems in a wide range of interconnected systems. Let's look at some examples. This is a graph representation of the book Les Miserables. Every node is a character from the book, and every line signals the existence of an interaction between two characters. This representation is one of the examples of graphs that come with the free software, Gephi, developed to analyze graph properties. The first time a character in the book interacts with another character, a line is drawn between these two to signal the existence of an interaction. Every time the same pair of characters interacts, the line becomes thicker to signal the intensity of the interaction. So you not only have information about which characters interact with each other, but also the importance of that interaction. Without even reading the book, just by looking at the graph, you know that Valjean and Cosette have a strong relationship. Additionally, every interaction enlarges the circle representing the characters so you can also observe that Valjean must be the protagonist of the story if his circle is the largest in the graph. With the same idea, movies have also been represented as graphs, as shown here for The Imitation Game. The same idea of interaction between characters is used here to represent the plot of the movie as a graph. In a completely different area, graphs are used to represent computer networks. The nodes of the graph represent the routers, and the edges of the graph represent the communication links, for example, optical fiber links. In this picture, you can see a graph representation of the NSFNET as it was in 1991, super-imposed onto a map of the USA. Social networks can also be represented using graphs. In this case, similarly to graphs representing books or movies, every node is a person, and if two people have a relationship, they are connected using a line. This image is a very famous example in network science. It shows the social network of a character group, where two clear groups of people can be identified because their members are mainly connected to members of the same group. For example, you can see that node 24 is only connected to members of the Red team, and that the same situation happens with most members of the Red and White teams. There are very useful algorithms that help you to find out how many and what communities or groups exist in a social network by analyzing its graph. By now, I hope you have an idea of how useful graphs are to analyze many different interconnected systems. I have just shown you a few examples, but if you look on the Internet for more, you will find graphs being used in a wide range of disciplines. Now, let's look at two ways of classifying graphs. The first way is as directed or undirected. In a directed graph, the lines connecting the nodes are arrows, signaling the direction of the relationship between two nodes. For example, if we are going to use a graph to represent a set of roads, we can use a directed graph to explicitly represent the direction of the different streets. In this example, you can see the streets around Goldsmiths, University of London. We can choose to represent each junction as a node in the graph, and the streets are the links of the graphs. As cars cannot traverse the street in any direction, we represent this using arrows. Another example of a directed graph is Twitter. On Twitter, you might follow someone who is not following you back. In that case, the relationship between you and the person you follow is signaled by an arrow starting at the node representing you and ending at the node representing the person you follow. Let's move now to undirected graphs. In an undirected graph, the relationship between nodes is symmetrical. For example, two friends on Facebook. If you are friends with someone on Facebook, that person is also a friend of yours. So unlike Twitter, you are connected by two arrows, one in each direction. To avoid drawing so many arrows in that case, we represent the symmetrical relationship with a line. So every time you see a graph with lines instead of arrows, remember this is an undirected graph and that the line represents two arrows, one in each direction. Now, let's look at the second dimension we will use to classify graphs, the weight associated with the links. The two graphs already shown here are unweighted graphs. That means that the relationship between any pair of nodes has the same importance across the graph. For example, on Facebook, you are friends with someone or not. There are not categories of friendships that will make some links more important than others. However, we might want to assign a given importance, priority, or metric to different links in a graph. For example, in the case of roads, we might assign to each link a weight that represents the length of the street in meters or the level of congestion, three being a high level of congestion and one being no congestion at all. In these two examples, we have directed and weighted graphs because the links have directions and different values associated with them. The last type of graph we are going to reveal is a graph that is undirected and weighted, as in the graph we saw for the book Les Miserables. In this case, we assign a weight to each relationship to signal its intensity. So in this example, if this were a social network, the links with weight 3 may represent people that exchange a high number of likes, while the links with weight 1 may represent people with a low frequency of interactions. In summary, visiting this figure clockwise, we have weighted directed graphs, weighted undirected graphs, unweighted undirected graphs, and unweighted directed graphs. Finally, I would like to mention that graphs can have very different topologies. Here are some of the most common. The bus topology, where nodes form a line, the ring topology where the last node of a line is connected to the first node of the line, the tree topology following the shape of a tree data structure, the regular Manhattan typology, and the arbitrary mesh topology. With this, we finish our introduction to the graph data structure.

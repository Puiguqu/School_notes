In this lecture, we will study the pseudocode of Kruskal's algorithm. Just to refresh your mind, Kruskal's algorithm starts by having as many trees as nodes in the graph, and then begins merging the trees by selecting the next lowest cost edge that connects two different trees. The algorithm finishes when all trees are merged into a single one. Now, let's look at the three main steps of this algorithm. The first step is the initialization of the tree with as many 1-node trees as there are nodes in the graph. The second step is sorting the edges of the graph in ascending order of weight. The third and final step is selecting the next edge to add to the spanning tree. When adding that edge, two trees are merged. Now, before looking at how these steps can be transformed into pseudocode, we need to briefly visit the operations of a data structure called a disjoint set. We will not study the details of the disjoint set data structure, we will only review three of its operations, as we will use them in implementing Kruskal's algorithm. A disjoint-set data structure is a collection of sets that do not have elements in common. In the example shown here, we can see that the disjoint-set data structure F is made of nine sets of one element each. The three main operations of the disjoint-set data structure are MAKE-SET, this function creates a new set made of a single element.

In this lecture, we are going to learn about an alternative way of describing the time or memory requirements of an algorithm. It's known as the asymptotic analysis. Before diving into the details of asymptotic analysis, let's summarize what we have learned so far and discover how it relates to asymptotic analysis. We have seen that the computational requirements of an algorithm can depend only on the input data size, or on the input data size and the input data content. In the first case, that means, that we will only get one expression for the computational requirements of an algorithm. For example, after counting up the number of operations, we can determine that the running time is proportional to the input data size. Therefore, we can say that the algorithm grows linearly with the input data size, regardless of the content of the input data. In the second case, we might get several different expressions for the computational requirements, depending on the content of the input data. This produces worst, best and average cases. We might get a different expression for the running time of each case. In both types of algorithms, after obtaining the expression for the running time or the memory requirements, we realized that the constant and the lower order terms are not significant for large inputs. Thus, we ignored them to establish the growth of the running time in terms of the dominating term. This is what we have been doing so far in terms of algorithm analysis, comparing algorithms in terms of their growth of the running time functions. What we are going to learn now, is an alternative way of comparing algorithms. This alternative way, also starts with the function describing the computational requirements of an algorithm. But instead of just dropping constants and lower order terms, we will define things a bit more formally. This alternative way was originally devised by mathematicians to describe Functions. By a function, I mean any function, not necessarily related to computational requirements. Just to give you a bit of historical context, the first proposal was called the Big-O Notation and was published by Paul Bachmann in 1894, well before the invention of the modern computer architecture we now today, Big-O was a mathematical notation to describe the behavior of a function, when its argument tends to infinity. It remained in the mathematical domain for another 76 years after its creation. Then in 1914, the Omega notation was introduced by Godfrey Hardy and Joan Littlewood. Again, this was proposed in the area of math to analyze Functions. Finally, in 1970, Donald Knuth, imported the Big-O and Omega notations into the field of computer science, to analyze functions describing the computational requirements of algorithms. In that year, Theta notation was created especially for algorithm analysis. As these three notations study the behavior of a function, when the argument tends to infinity. Their use in analyzing algorithms when the input data is large, is called asymptotic analysis.

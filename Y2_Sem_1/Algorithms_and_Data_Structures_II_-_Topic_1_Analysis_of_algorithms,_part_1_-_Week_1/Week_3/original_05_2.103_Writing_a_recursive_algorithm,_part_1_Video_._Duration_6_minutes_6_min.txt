We have already traced several recursive programs to do. So, every time a recursive call is made we live the current execution on a standby and proceeded to execute the recursive call. That is each call to a recursive function can be seen as opening a new box and looking into detail into it to execute every part of it, this way of looking at recursive algorithms is very useful for tracing algorithms, but it's not good for writing them. Writing recursive algorithms requires a point of view that is different from the one you used to understand them. A successful approach to ideating a recursive algorithm is ignoring the details of the recursive processing, that is using the recursive call as a black box for which we only know it's result. Let's illustrate this idea with the following problem. Imagine you have a big bag full of marbles and you are asked to determine exactly how many there are in this bag. You don't feel like doing all that work on your own so you decide to do a small part of the job and then delegate the rest. Think of the small part of the job as the processing that the recursive algorithm does, and think of the delegated part as the recursive call. When you delegate a task you really don't care about how the task is going to be solved. You just want a result back. When writing a recursive call we will take the same approach. So you do your small part of the job by taking one marble out of the bag and registering that information one. You have one marble, and then you delegate by giving the bag to someone else asking them to give you the number of marbles left in the bag. You don't care about how this person is going to solve the problem. You just know that when the person gives you back the resulting number you are going to sum one to this number to get the final answer, in doing so you will have solved the original problem without worrying about the details of how the other person solved the problem. Now let's enter the trace mode. If we trace the entire procedure we can imagine that the person you gave the bag to executed the same task. Upon receiving the bag they took one marble out of it and handed it back with the remaining marbles to another person who will have to solve the problem of determining the number of marbles in the bag. If every person on the chain keeps on repeating the same procedure, we will get to the point where a person receives a bag with only one marble. At that point the task of counting the number of marbles has become trivial. This is our base case, the person then simply answers back there's one marble in the bag. The person receiving this answer sums this answer one to his counting one and gives inturn an answer back. There are two marbles in the bag this procedure repeats until the person you gave the bag comes back to you with an answer. Let's say 47 marbles. Now, you can solve your problem. You know that the back had 48 marbles in it, and to get the answer you simply had to take one marble out of the bag and delegate the rest. This idea of doing just a small part of the job and delegating the rest without caring about the details of how the delegated task is going to be executed is key to being able to write a recursive algorithm. If we had to write the pseudocode of the recursive algorithm that solves the marble counting problem, it would look like this. The input argument is a bag with marbles. The small part of the job is taking one marble out. We wait for someone else to solve the problem given them the bag with the remaining marbles and return one plus that answer. Now this recursive algorithm is missing a base case, the base case happens when the person receiving the bag is left with an empty bag. In that case the person simply returns the answer one. If add the base case, we have the final algorithm to determine the number of marbles in the bag with this idea of doing just small part of the job and delegating the rest. Please review the recursive algorithms we have studied so far. Identify what is the small job the algorithm does by itself.

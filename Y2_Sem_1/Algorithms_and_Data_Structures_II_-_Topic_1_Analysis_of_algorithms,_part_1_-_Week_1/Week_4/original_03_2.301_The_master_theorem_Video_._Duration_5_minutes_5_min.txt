In the last lecture, we learned how to find the recurrence equation that describes the running time of a recursive algorithm and how to solve that recurrence equation. The method to solve the recurrence equation, consisted of two steps. Finding a known value for the running time, by analyzing the best-case, expanding the right side of the recurrence equation until the known value found in the previous step, could be replaced in it. This way of solving recurrence equations is effective, but time consuming. On some cases a much quicker and easier method can be applied. The method is known as the master theorem. The master theorem makes the asymptotic analysis of recursive functions much easier. However, it can only be applied, if the recurrence equation has this specific structure. You can think of the master theorem as a recipe, that solves recurrences where the coefficient a, is equal to or greater than one and the coefficient b must be strictly greater than one. That is the argument of the running time function in the right side of the recurrence equation, must be a fraction of n. Thus, for example, the master theorem can be applied to analyze this recurrence equation, because a is equal to one and b is higher than one. In this other example, by contrast, the master theorem cannot be applied, because although a is equal to or greater than one, b is not higher than one. Now, that you know what recurrence equations can be solved, with the master theorem, let's see how to use it. The master theorem classifies the recurrence equation in one of three cases, depending on whether the function f of n is lower than or equal to or higher than n to the log of base b of a. Thus, if f of n is lower than n to the log base b of a, the recurrence equation belongs to case 1. If f of n is equal to n, to the log B of a, the recurrence equation belongs to case 2. Finally, if f of n is greater than n to the log base b of a, the recurrence belongs to case 3. Case 3 has an extra requirement. You can only apply it, if this condition is also met. That is, if a times f of n over b is equal to or lower than c times f of n for c less than one and n large. Once you have determined, which case you're recurrence equation belongs to, you can use the master theorem to find out the Theta notation for the running time of your algorithm as follows. For case 1, the running time t of n is Theta of n to the log base b of a. For case 2, the running time is Theta of n to the log base b of a multiplied by log n. Finally, for case 3, the running time is Theta of f of n. Before finishing, let's see an example of how to apply the master theorem. In the left part of the screen, you can see the structure of the recurrence equations that can be solved by applying the master theorem and the three cases. In the right part of the screen, you can now see the recurrence equation we are going to analyze. The first step is identifying the values of a and b, and checking they meet the condition for which the master theorem can be applied. In this case, a is equal to two, which is greater than one, and b is also equal to two, which is strictly higher than one. Thus the master theorem can be applied. The next step is identifying which of the three cases this recurrence equation belongs to. To do so, we first calculate log base b of a, which is log base two of two, which is equal to one. Now, compare f of n to n to the one. If this was case 1, f of n should be strictly lower than n, as n is not strictly lower than n. This is not case 1. If this was case 2, f of n should be equal to n. That is exactly the case. So this recurrence equation belongs to case 2. Thus, the running time of the recursive algorithm described by this recurrence equation is Theta of n times log n. So from now on, every time you need to analyze a recursive algorithm. Please check first of all, whether the recurrence equation describing the running time is such that the master theorem can be applied.

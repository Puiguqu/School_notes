Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Hello and welcome to Topic 3. In this topic, we will study a classical problem in Computer Science, the problem of sorting. That is, the problem of arranging data in ascending or descending order. Many algorithms have been proposed to solve the problem of sorting. They can be classified in two main categories, comparison and non comparison sorts. The most known algorithms belong to the first category. You might have heard of them already. Bubble sort, Insertion sort, Selection sort, Quick sort, and Merge sort are all examples of comparison sorts. The main characteristic of comparison sorts is that they compare pairs of elements to determine the sorting order. For example, bubble sort compares two consecutive elements in an array and swaps them if the element on the left is higher than the element on the right and the sorting is ascendant. Non comparison sorts instead do not make comparisons. In this module, we will study three of them: Counting sort, Radix sort, and Bucket sort. By now you might be asking why do we need to learn even more sorting algorithms? Are the comparison sorts I already know not enough? Well, the main point here is that comparison sorts have a limit to the running time. In the worst case, they cannot be faster than n times log n. We will see why in another lecture. Here you can see a summary of the worst and best case time complexity for the most known comparison sorts. Notice that I'm using the Theta notation, the notation created specifically for analysis of algorithms. In the worst-case, simple algorithms like bubble, insertion, and selection exhibit a quadratic running time which makes them unsuitable to sort big volumes of data. Recursive Quicksort does not improve on the worst case, and Merge sort gets a significant improvement by having a running time that grows with n times log n, which makes it the fastest algorithm of these five for the worst case. Of course, this list is not exhaustive, and you might wonder whether there exists a comparison sort that can run faster than n times log n in the worst case. Maybe we did not include it in the list, or maybe it has not been invented yet. Well, there isn't one. There is no comparison sort that can run faster than n times log n in the worst case. We will see later that because comparison sorts must compare pairs of elements, they cannot run faster than n times log n. That leads us to the reason why we will study non comparison sorts. Sorting algorithms that do not make comparisons can beat the lower bound of n times log n of comparison sorts.

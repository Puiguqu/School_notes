1. The lecture explains the concept of merge sort, a sorting algorithm that successively divides an array into halves until only one element remains.
2. Merge sort works by dividing the array into smaller subarrays, sorting each subarray recursively, and then merging the sorted subarrays back together.

3. The example given is a five-element array with unsorted numbers: 31, 12, three, 23, and 17.

4. The algorithm starts by dividing the array into two halves, resulting in two sub-arrays of three elements each.

5. The process continues until only one element remains in each half of the original array.

6. When merging two sorted sub-arrays, comparison is performed to ensure that the elements are in the correct order.

7. For instance, comparing 31 and 12 results in 12 being placed before 31 in the sorted sequence.

8. This process continues recursively until only one element remains in each half of the array.

9. The final step is merging these two halves together to form a fully sorted array.

10. The algorithm repeats this process for every sub-array, ensuring that all elements are correctly ordered.

11. Merge sort has an average-case time complexity of O(n log n), making it one of the most efficient sorting algorithms available.

12. This means that the number of steps required to sort an array grows logarithmically with respect to its size.

13. Additionally, merge sort is a stable sorting algorithm, meaning that equal elements are not swapped during the sorting process.

The course has reached its new major topic group, which focuses on assertions and parameter checking.

The main learning objectives for Week 9 are to define what an assertion is in a computer program, differentiate between assertions and regular control flow, and understand how assertions fit into the software development lifecycle.

Assertions are used to control how a program works, whereas regular control flow statements like if-else statements serve different purposes.

Assertions can be used to test code before it's executed and can help prevent errors or unexpected behavior.

The course will cover practical capabilities for writing assertion code in different programming languages.

A key concept is that assertions should be enabled when a program is tested and disabled when the program is running, as they may slow down execution time.

This topic group builds upon previous modules on module cohesion, coupling, unit testing, and test-driven development.

Week 9 introduces defensive coding, debugging, exception handling, and the use of assertions in software development.

The course will explore case studies and real-world examples to illustrate the importance of assertions.

One such example is the Ariane 5 rocket failure, where asserting certain conditions may have prevented the error.

The course materials for Week 9 include a video on introduction to assertions, practice assignments, readings, and a practical demonstration.

Students are encouraged to write their own assertions in different programming languages as part of an assignment.

The final task is to find an example of a famous/public software failure and discuss its implications for using assertions.

There will also be a discussion prompt to consider whether assertions should be left in or not.

Overall, the course aims to provide students with practical knowledge and skills on using assertions effectively in software development.

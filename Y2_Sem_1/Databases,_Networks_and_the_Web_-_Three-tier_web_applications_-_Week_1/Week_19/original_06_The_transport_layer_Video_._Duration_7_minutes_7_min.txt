This model is actually named in part after the most famous protocol of the transport layer, TCP, or transmission control protocol. Protocols such as FTP, HTTP, and SMTP in the application layer rely on TCP in transport layer. TCP is connection-oriented. It takes place between a client and server when initiating or terminating a TCP connection. TCP being connection-oriented means that first a reliable connection must be established and acknowledged, and then data can be transmitted. So the advantage of TCP is that it is reliable. It also does lots of error checking. For example, TCP has a checksum in its header, which is used to verify that end-to-end data has not been corrupted. It also involves the three-way handshake process. Let me elaborate this more. The three-way handshake process occurs between a client and server when initiating or terminating a TCP connection. As its name implies, it's a three-step process which mirrors the process of meeting and talking to someone, which is why it's called a handshake. Imagine you want to talk to a colleague or friend about something. You would initiate that by saying hello or excuse me, or ask them if they are free for a chat. If they reply yes, then you can start the conversation properly. This is the three-way handshake process in computing. In step one, the client sends a TCP SYN or synchronization segment to server. Remember from our earlier lectures that packets are called segments in transport layer. In this step, no data is sent yet. So it's a bit like the first approach of asking someone to talk. In step two, the server receives the synchronization segment and replies with a SYNACK, which is synchronization acknowledgement segment. Now, in step three, the client receives their SYNACK and replies with an ACK or acknowledgement segment, which now may contain data. This would be the substance of the conversation in our real world example. All this back and forth communication and deliverability is good for reliability, but it is not good in terms of speed. UDP is another protocol at the transport layer. UDP stands for user datagram protocol. A datagram is the same thing as a packet of information. The UDP protocol works similarly to TCP, but it leaves all the error checking out. There is no hand shaking before sending a segment in UDP. That means UDP is suitable for application in which speed is desirable and error correction is not necessary. This would be things like live broadcasting, online games, or telephone and video conferencing over the Internet. TCP, on the other hand, is suitable for application in which error correction is necessary, like file transfer and email. Ports and sockets are two other important concepts in the transport layer. The port number is a bit like a telephone extension number in a big organization. Let me explain. As we know, the IP address will identify your machine on the Internet so that data can be sent to it. This is a bit like someone calling the switchboard. They will get through to the right organization this way, but not to the person who needs the message. Once connection is established, you need to know the extension number to know how to talk across that connection. So you need one more mechanism to identify which application on your machine is the destination of each message. When your computer receives data from outside, that message needs to be directed to the correct application process. This is where ports and sockets come into play. Each application runs on a specific port number on your machine, just as each person in an organization has their own extension number. The IP address plus the port number is called a socket, which creates a complete addressing mechanism. Remember developing your dynamic web application in lab exercises in previous topics. When you were writing a web server software or index.js, you assigned a value to your port number. Example port numbers were 8080 or 8081 and so on. This leads us to two more concepts that happen in the transport layer, multiplexing and demultiplexing. Multiplexing is at the sender side. There may be many processes that want to send their packets, and multiplexing is the process of passing these segments to the network layer. TCP at the transport layer accepts and collects the data it receives from all the different sockets and adds transport headers which add data related to that layer before sending them on in a single line. So multiplexing is a many-to-one process. Demultiplexing, on the other hand, is the reverse process happening at the receiver side. It is one-to-many process. Packets arrive one after the other in one line, and then they are redirected to different ports. Demultiplexing checks the port numbers and delivers the datagrams to the correct applications. So demultiplexing is the process whereby the transport layer delivers data to the correct socket. Now that we have covered two layers in the TCP/IP model, namely the application and transport layers.

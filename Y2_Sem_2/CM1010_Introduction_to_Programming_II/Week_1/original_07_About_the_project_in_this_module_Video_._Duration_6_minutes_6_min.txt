Having run this module for a couple of years now, we have decided to make a few changes to the way it's run, to make sure you get the most support. We've spent time reflecting on what students have told us about their learning experience and taken this feedback into account when redesigning the module. Here, I'm going to outline how this module will work to give you a better expectation of how it's going to run. This module will feel quite different to Introduction to Programming Part 1. In Introduction to Programming Part 1, we gave you quite a lot of guidance, quite a lot of support. In this module, we're going to take away some of that stability. Previously, we supported you through your initial experiences with learning to code. We've taught you a lot about syntax and we've taught you the logical constructs that sit behind the coding process. But what we haven't really done is got you to think about building and writing your own code from scratch. While we're going to take away some of those stabilizers, we're still going to make sure there's plenty of support and help for you to get through this next stage of learning to program, which is potentially the most difficult. Although it is difficult, once you do learn how to code independently, it is the most rewarding part of programming because you'll be able to think about problems and work towards solutions. In this module, we're going to be teaching you how to come up with your own code and your own code structures. And we're going to assess that through a larger project. This will be a testing ground for the kinds of advanced projects you're going to do later on in your degree, where you will work more independently. With the project you completed in Intro to Programming Part 1, we were telling you exactly what you needed to do. Here, we're going to be asking you to think within certain parameters about the kinds of code that you might want to write and how you're going to go about doing that. As well as getting you to synthesize your own programs, we're also going to be thinking more about the full development cycle. Software development is a multifaceted challenge. It doesn't just begin and end with the code editor. Looking at problems, looking at the kinds of code that you might want to write, the kinds of software that you might want to create. We will then be working through various iterations of design. Think about how you organize and design your code. Finally, when you've done your coding, we're going to be thinking about how you're going to evaluate it. To be an effective programmer, you must be able to fully understand the full expanse of the software development cycle, not just be able to write good code. Therefore, this is primarily a project-based module. You are going to be assessed a little bit on your coding, but you're also going to be assessed on how effectively you brainstorm your ideas. Similarly, you're going to be assessed on how well you produce designs. Have you iterated your designs? Have you shown us that you've gone through an effective process? This doesn't mean to say that we're looking for a design aesthetic. This is still a computer science module after all. But what it does mean is that we want to see that you've thought about the problem critically and have worked towards a solution, and you've written effective and organized code to do that. Alongside the structure you apply to your code, we also want you to think about the structure you apply to your time. How organized are you? How do you know what thing it is you're going to be working towards next? And then finally, how effectively are you going to be able to critique your work during and after that development period? I've got one final thing that I want to cover, and that is how you are going to be marked in this module. So we are all aware of this. I'm going to briefly sketch out exactly what the marks that you will be given in this module mean in terms of UK grade classifications. 40%, as hopefully most of you are aware, is the pass line for an undergraduate qualification. To pass this module, you need to have scored 40%, and you need to have done it across both the midterm and the final submission. If you score over 40%, that is what we refer to as a pass, or being satisfactory. You may have heard this referred to as a third class degree. Over 50% is what we consider to be good. This classification shows that you have achieved everything that we've shown you, and you've understood most of that quite well. We might refer to this as a 2-2, or a 2 lower class division. Beyond that, at 60%, this we would classify as very good. It may be referred to as a 2-1, or a second class upper. And then finally, first class is over 70%, and that is what we consider to be excellent. Realistically, if you do everything we tell you, this is the top mark that you should be able to achieve. Getting much beyond 70%, 80%, or even into the 90s is very unusual when we're talking about the UK grading scale. This means that the very, very top grades shouldn't really be achievable at this stage, so we protect those quite carefully. In this module, we've tried to design to really reflect how you will be graded in later parts of your degree, particularly in project modules.

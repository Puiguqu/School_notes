Music visualization has evolved over almost five decades, initially appearing with historical examples like the Atari Video Music system in 1977. This early hardware connected stereos and TVs to create sound property visualizations, though its output was often considered unsuccessful. The popularity of synched display technology grew alongside the shift from physical media to digital storage and the rise of MP3 formats. By the late 1990s and early 2000s, major playback software such as Winamp, Windows Media Player, and iTunes incorporated built-in visualization features. These concepts are complemented by the art form of VJing, where performers create arresting visual outputs for live audiences. Furthermore, the "demo scene" has historically pushed computing platforms, like the Commodore Amiga, to generate complex graphical outputs. This case study aims to build upon a basic music visualization program to reflect music's tone, energy, and time signature. The modern application is designed to be highly responsive, featuring full-screen and window mode functionalities. Upon activation, the app displays the first visualization, an old-fashioned spectrum representing the diverse amplitudes of the sound. A menu system allows users to switch between various visualizations, including a standard wave pattern. This wave pattern responds directly to waves derived from the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) and displays sampled sound output. The final, most complex visualization is called "needles," which tracks the amplitude of four specific frequency bands: bass, low-mids, high-mids, and treble. The developers noted that the application has inherent limitations, such as the need for perfect scaling improvement.

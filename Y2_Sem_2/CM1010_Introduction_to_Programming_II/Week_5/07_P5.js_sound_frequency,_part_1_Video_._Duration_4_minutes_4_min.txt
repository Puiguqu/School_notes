Sound quality involves more than just amplitude; another essential quality is frequency, which we commonly refer to as pitch. Pitch defines the perceived highness or lowness of a sound, illustrated by examples such as an opera singer having a high pitch, measured around 440 hertz. Conversely, a Buddhist monk chanting might have a lower frequency, such as around 160 hertz, while the low notes of a piano register near 27.5 hertz. Human hearing is capable of detecting frequencies across a range extending from 20 hertz to 20,000 hertz.

However, determining a single precise frequency for instruments like triangles and kettledrums is challenging because these sounds are complex. The fundamental concept is that all sounds are actually composed of many constituent frequencies, each possessing its own unique amplitude. When multiple notes are played together, forming a chord, the sound requires the combined description of several distinct frequencies. In contrast to simple musical notes, analyzing complex sounds necessitates understanding both the constituent frequencies and their varying amplitudes.

This leads to the definition of a frequency spectrum, which is generated by theoretically measuring the amplitude of every possible frequency from 20 hertz to 20,000 hertz. Due to the sheer number of frequencies—specifically 18,980—analyzing the full spectrum is impractical. Therefore, these frequencies are typically grouped into manageable segments called frequency bands, analogous to the display found on graphic equalizers. Because sounds are dynamic, most changes occur over time, requiring continuous capture of new spectra to maintain accuracy.

In terms of technical implementation, the required tool is the FFT object available within the p5.sound library, which allows for obtaining a "rolling frequency spectrum" for a music track.

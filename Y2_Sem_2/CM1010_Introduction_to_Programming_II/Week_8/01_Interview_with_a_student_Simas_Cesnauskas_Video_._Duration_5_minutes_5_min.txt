# Interview with a student: Simas Cesnauskas

Simas Cesnauskas, a computer science student at Goldsmiths, presented an expanded data visualization application developed for a programming project. He began by demonstrating extensions added to existing data sketches, such as the original pie chart. Specifically, he converted the pie chart into a donut chart by adding a complex overlay feature. This overlay allows users, upon hovering, to view the percentage contribution of a specific race within a company or corresponding group. Another developed sketch focused on Internet usage across the UK. This visualization tracks the percentage of internet usage, segmented by age and sex, for two distinct years. A visual cue in this sketch is that the magnitude of the circle represents the overall size of the population within that specific group.

A third major addition detailed the immunization status of UK boroughs. This data set tracks vaccinations for MMR and Meningitis C. A key technical detail of this sketch is that overlapping data points display a unique, distinguishing color. Simas also significantly expanded the core mapping functions of the visualization app. Conceptually, his most refined work was the Internet usage visualization, which he considered his most significant achievement.

This success was largely attributed to mastering the use of a programming concept called the constructor. The constructor acts as a template, allowing the program to automatically generate content, such as repeating circles, without the need for writing repetitive code. Simas highlighted that he started with no prior programming experience, making his acquisition of JavaScript, HTML, and CSS particularly notable.

His design inspiration was fundamentally to challenge himself by successfully implementing the constructor concept. For the Internet usage sketch, he purposefully selected two contrasting years—2012 and 2017—to compare male and female usage percentages across four different groups. He designed the visualization to be more engaging, incorporating movement that enhanced, rather than altered, the data representation.

Regarding implementation challenges, he faced difficulties learning to use new third-party libraries, some of which proved unreliable. However, he managed to make improvements where overlapping data points change color to prevent redundancy. Furthermore, he integrated mapping functionality using the Leaflet library.

Looking forward, he identified several areas for future project improvement. First, he intended to dedicate more effort to perfecting the map functionality. Second, he aspired to creating a 2D density plot, although he opted not to use the DS.js library as it was deemed too simple for his current challenge. Finally, he initially envisioned making the speed of the circles proportional to their size in the Internet usage plot. Overall, the project showcased a strong technical progression from a beginner status to utilizing advanced OOP and specialized visualization libraries.

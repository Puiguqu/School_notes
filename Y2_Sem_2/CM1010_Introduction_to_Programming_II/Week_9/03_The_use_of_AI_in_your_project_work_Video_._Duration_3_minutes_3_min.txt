The video transcript discusses recent advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI) and their profound impact on student projects. Major computing developments involve the increased use of large language models (LLMs). These sophisticated LLMs, such as ChatGPT, can generate documents from natural language prompts and also support code generation and code completion. Regarding academic policy, students must never submit any code, writing, or material generated by an AI without clearly labeling it as not being their own original work. Failure to label AI contributions is strictly prohibited and constitutes a breach of the contract with the University of London, which is classified as academic misconduct. The institution has established methods to effectively detect the use of AI in submitted coursework. Academically, relying too heavily on AI inhibits the development of crucial learning skills and problem-solving abilities. Asking an AI to write code or academic work is explicitly likened to soliciting help from a fellow student in the class. While professional software developers do utilize these tools, they succeed because they already possess deep understanding, and they know how to inspect, validate, and interpret the generated code. The purpose of the coursework is specifically to ensure students learn and demonstrate these core technical skills. However, AI can be utilized responsibly to assist with the project ideation process. Students may use AI to brainstorm different concepts and generate practice exercises in areas where they struggle. Furthermore, AI can help with abstract issues by suggesting conceptual pointers, similar to discussing the project with a peer. This assistance must be framed as seeking suggestions, not asking the model to complete the code. Crucially, upon submission, students must include documentation detailing both the prompts given to the LLMs and the subsequent responses generated by the model. The curriculum is structured across 21 weeks, guiding students through project development and advanced testing. Initial case studies focus on three distinct applications: a drawing app, a music visualiser, and a data visualisation tool. Development extends into advanced topics like "Extending the apps, part 1," focusing on modules such as the stamp tool. Later modules build upon this by covering specialized topics like "Callbacks" and detailed testing procedures. The final phase emphasizes "Testing for stability" and ultimately "Testing with users," which are critical for project completion. Overall, AI is permitted only as a sophisticated, non-substantive aid to thought and conceptual development. The core finding is that while AI is powerful, the primary objective remains the development and demonstration of the student's own foundational coding skills.

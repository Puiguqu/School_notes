# Brainstorming and scoping

The core challenge in any software project, whether large or academic, is the major limiting factor of time. Consequently, project scope and scalability are entirely dictated by what can be achieved between the start date and the final deadline. To mitigate the inherent unpredictability of software, such as getting stuck on a bug, careful planning is essential. The midterm assignment requires students to produce a comprehensive report detailing their project plan and initial coding structure. A critical preliminary step involves brainstorming a wide array of potential extensions and refinement ideas for the chosen application. Students should draw inspiration from similar real-world applications, such as examining vintage drawing tools or scanning newspaper websites for unique visualizations. The project methodology requires going through this exhaustive process for each template application to inform the final selection. The instructors will guide students through a series of potential extensions, and the project may incorporate variations of two of these suggested modules.

Once ideas are gathered, the next stage involves ranking the concepts by measured difficulty. This ranking serves as a yardstick for complexity, contrasting an easier feature like a basic rectangle tool with a more challenging one, such as a bucket fill algorithm. For highly complex extensions, like basic layer support in a drawing app, planning must incorporate both the code structure and the necessary user interaction design. In cases like the bucket fill, students must research the standard algorithms required and identify the specific P5 objects needed for implementation. Other advanced features may not have single algorithmic solutions, necessitating a deep consideration of the problem's complexity.

After creating an ordered list of extensions, students must select and justify their final choices. Decisions can be guided by criteria beyond difficulty, such as the extension's potential usefulness or its creative value. Furthermore, selecting extensions requires checking the project’s specific mark scheme to ensure the chosen features will satisfy all grading requirements. The submitted midterm report must provide concrete evidence of the entire planning process, not just the final code. This evidence must include descriptions and diagrams illustrating the design's evolution from initial conception to the establishment of the code structure.

Crucially, the report must also demonstrate careful consideration for evaluation and testing. While full test plans are not mandatory for this module, students must detail how they plan to test the successful implementation of their extensions. This includes planning for edge case scenarios or utilizing real-world feedback by testing the app with peers. Finally, the report requires a robust plan for time management, most commonly visualized through a Gantt chart. This chart must break down all project tasks and fit them into the available timeline, especially reserving time for necessary contingency. Students are encouraged to include all planning evidence, including "scrappy diagrams, sketches or scans of handwritten notes," to showcase the entire intellectual process.

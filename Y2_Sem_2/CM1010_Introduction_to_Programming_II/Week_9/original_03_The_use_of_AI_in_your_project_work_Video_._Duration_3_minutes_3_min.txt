Since we originally designed this module, a major change in computing has been the increase in the use of large language models. They have become increasingly more sophisticated and have been adopted more widely. Applications such as ChatGPT have enabled the LLMs to support features such as document generation from natural language prompts and supporting code generation and code completion. In terms of what you're allowed to do in your projects, you must not submit code, writing or other material that has been generated by an AI for your coursework, without clearly labelling it as not being your work. So you may be questioning, why not? Why shouldn't I use these tools that are available? And quite simply, it is a breach of your contract with the University of London and an example of academic misconduct. Asking an AI to write your work, to write your code, is no different from asking a fellow member of class. It's also important to note that we have methods that will be able to detect this use. Most importantly of all, you won't be able to learn as much as you would compared to taking the time to do the project by yourself and solving coding problems as they arise. You might be thinking that professional software developers use these tools, so why can't you? And of course they do. However, they're in a position where they already understand code. They know how to inspect it, how to understand it, and how to validate when it's correct. You've got to learn and demonstrate these skills to be successful in this field. This is likely to remain the case for quite some time yet. However, there are some opportunities for you to use AI in your work. It might help you to ideate, to come up with different ideas and different concepts for your project on the extensions. You might also use it to generate exercises for yourself in areas that you find hard. Then you can practice to try out different techniques and give yourself the solutions to those exercises. It might also help you with issues in the abstract. If you're stuck with a problem in your code, you could maybe ask it how you might go about solving the problem, but in an abstracted way. Imagine you're talking to a friend or fellow student about your project. You're not actually asking them to write it or to complete the code for you. You're just asking them to give you suggestions and pointers as to where you might move on next.

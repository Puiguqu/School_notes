This educational module addresses cyber threats, defining them as any attempt to gain unauthorized access to a system, network, or digital device with the intent to cause harm. Cyber threats affect all aspects of life, requiring vigilance for individuals, businesses, and governments alike. The most common types include malware, phishing, ransomware, social engineering, information manipulation, and supply chain attacks. Malware, or malicious software, functions like a digital virus, capable of stealing data, spying on activity, or destroying files. Phishing is a highly successful threat where attackers use seemingly legitimate emails to trick victims into revealing confidential information, such as passwords or credit card details. Ransomware locks a user's files and demands payment, presenting a growing and costly threat. Social engineering is a non-technical attack that relies on psychological manipulation, such as a caller pretending to be tech support. Information manipulation and interference involves spreading false data to confuse or influence decisions, impacting everything from elections to financial markets. A critical threat is the supply chain attack, which targets less secure elements, such as third-party vendors, to gain access to a major organization’s system. The SolarWinds attack exemplifies this, where malware was injected into a legitimate software update to infiltrate major government institutions undetected for months. Historical examples highlight the severe impact of these threats, notably the 2017 WannaCry ransomware attack, which infected over 200,000 computers globally and forced hospitals to cancel surgeries. Furthermore, the Equifax data breach in 2017 involved the theft of personal data, including social security numbers, from nearly 150 million people due to a vulnerability in update software. Cybersecurity is crucial because threats affect personal data control and, for businesses, can damage reputations and cause massive financial losses. The academic curriculum spans 22 weeks, beginning in Week 1 with the identification and classification of cyber threats. Subsequent weeks advance the knowledge base, covering topics like cybersecurity frameworks and standards in Week 3. Weeks 5 and 6 focus on specialized Network and Operating System security principles. Week 7 introduces Application security, covering principles of testing and debugging. The curriculum then shifts to Weeks 9 and 10, providing detailed instruction on Cryptography in practice, including PKI and digital signatures. Week 11 delves into Blockchain for cybersecurity, a topic followed by Week 13, which covers Incident Response and forensics. Weeks 15 and 16 address Emerging trends, which include AI, ML, quantum computing, and IoT security. The course culminates in practical application in Weeks 19 and 20, focusing on real-world case studies to design and test security solutions.

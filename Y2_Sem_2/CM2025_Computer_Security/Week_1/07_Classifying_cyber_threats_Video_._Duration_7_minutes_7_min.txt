# Classifying cyber threats

This session provides a deep dive into the classification of cyber threats, enabling the categorization of threats based on their origin, operational vector, and resulting damage. Threat classification considers three main dimensions: who is responsible, how the attack operates, and what type of damage is inflicted. Threat origins are divided into insider threats and external threats. Insider threats stem from within the organization, involving current or former employees, contractors, or third-party partners. These can be classified as malicious, when acted upon deliberately for personal gain or revenge, or negligent, arising from accidental actions like losing a device or falling for phishing. Conversely, external threats originate outside the organization and include cybercriminals, hacker groups, state-sponsored actors, and hacktivists, exemplified by the 2017 WannaCry attack on the NHS.

Attack vectors are categorized into network-based threats and endpoint threats. Network-based threats target the digital infrastructure, such as servers, routers, or cloud systems, without needing access to a specific device. Key network attacks include Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks, which overwhelm servers with traffic, and Man-in-the-Middle attacks, where communication is secretly intercepted. Another network risk is port scanning, used to identify exploitable weaknesses across systems. Endpoint threats target the devices users actively utilize, including laptops, tablets, and smartphones. These can be delivered via infected USBs or phishing emails, or via keyloggers that steal credentials by recording every keystroke.

The impact of cyber attacks is assessed through financial and reputational damage. Financial impact can be immediate, such as paying a ransom (e.g., the Colonial Pipeline incident), or long term, involving legal fees and system downtime. Reputational damage occurs when sensitive data is exposed or systems are publicly compromised, eroding public trust. The infamous 2014 Sony Picture Hack exemplifies how data leakage can cause severe, lasting brand damage. To summarize, analysis requires considering the actor (insider vs. outsider), the vector (network vs. endpoint), and the impact (financial vs. reputational).

The course structure is comprehensive, spanning 22 weeks to cover advanced cybersecurity topics. Week 1 introduces foundational concepts, including the syllabus, general cyber threat landscapes, and basic classification. Week 2 continues the identification and classification of threats, forming part 2. Later modules include Weeks 3 and 4, which focus on cybersecurity frameworks, standards, and legal considerations. Subsequent weeks cover practical technical domains, such as Week 5/6 (Network and OS security) and Week 7/8 (Application security principles). Key technical depths are addressed in Weeks 9/10 (Cryptography in practice, covering PKI and digital signatures) and Week 11/12 (Blockchain for cybersecurity).

Incident response is covered in Weeks 13/14 (Incident response and forensics), while Week 15/16 tackle emerging trends, including AI, ML, quantum computing, and IoT security challenges. The final learning segments address advanced theory and application, encompassing Weeks 17/18 (EDI principles) and Weeks 19/20 (Real-world cybersecurity case studies). The curriculum culminates in Exam Preparation Week (Week 21) and the final Exam Week (Week 22).

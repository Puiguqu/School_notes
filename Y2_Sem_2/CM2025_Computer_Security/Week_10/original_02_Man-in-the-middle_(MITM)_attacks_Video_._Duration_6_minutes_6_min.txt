In this video, you will learn what man-in-the-middle attacks are, how they work, and how to defend against them. We will cover the different types of man-in-the-middle attacks, real-world examples, and the cryptography protection that help prevent them. By the end of this session, you will understand how attackers intercept and alter communication and how to spot and stop these attacks. A man-in-the-middle attack happens when an attacker secretly intercepts and possibly alters communication between two parties. The victim believes they are communicating directly with the trusted party, but the attacker is actually in the middle. This allows the attacker to eavesdrop on sensitive information or inject malicious content without the victim knowing it. Man-in-the-middle attacks typically begin with the attacker placing themselves between the sender and the receiver. They may use tools to spoof a network, intercept traffic, or exploit unsecured connections. Once in position, they can capture data like login credentials, banking information, or private messages. The victim's device continues to communicate unaware that their traffic is being watched or changed. There are several forms of man-in-the-middle attacks. These include Wi-Fi eavesdropping where attackers set up rogue hotspots, leaked hashes where attackers impersonate a trusted IP address, DNS spoofing where users are directed to fake websites, HTTPS stripping where secure connections are downgraded to encrypted ones. Each type uses different techniques to intercept or manipulate communication and leads to password reuse on other sites. Attackers use various tools to launch man-in-the-middle attacks. Some common tools include Ethercap which enables packet sniffing and spoofing, Wireshark which captures network traffic in real-time, SSLShip which removes encryption from HTTPS. These tools help attackers monitor and alter traffic without altering the user. Let's now look at some real-world examples. In one case, a public Wi-Fi network was set up in a coffee shop. Unsuspecting users connected and browsed the internet. The attacker in control of the network captured emails, passwords, and even banking details. In another example, DNS spoofing led users to fake banking websites where their login credentials were harvested. This example shows how easily man-in-the-middle attacks can occur without visible signs. Cryptographic encryption is one of the most effective defenses against man-in-the-middle attacks. HTTPS protects web traffic using transport layer security TLS which encrypts the connection between the browser and the server. Other email protocols like TLS and Pretty Good Privacy which stand for PGP protect messages in transit. Even if a hacker intercepts the encrypted traffic, they cannot read or alter it without the private keys. Digital certificates confirm the identity of websites and servers. Private authorities issue trusted certificates using secure browsing. Browsers show warnings if certificates are invalid or fake. Users should always verify the padlock icon and avoid entering credentials on unsecure or suspicious websites. Multi-factor authentication also adds protection even if data is intercepted. To ensure that the network is secure, avoid using open or public Wi-Fi without a virtual private network. Use VPNs to encrypt traffic even on insecure networks. Keep operating systems and software updates to patch non-vulnerabilities. Educate users about phishing and social engineering which attackers often use to gain access or fool victims into connecting to malicious networks. To summarize, man-in-the-middle attacks occur when attackers secretly intercept or alter communication. They use tools and network manipulation to steal data or inject malicious content. Strong encryption, authentication, and safe network habits can defend against these threats. In our next session, we will explore side-channel attacks which extract data through indirect signals like timing and power consumption.

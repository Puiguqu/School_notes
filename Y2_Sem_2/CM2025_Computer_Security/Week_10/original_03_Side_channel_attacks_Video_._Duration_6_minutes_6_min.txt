In this video, we will learn what side-channel attacks are, how they work, and how they can be prevented. We will explore real-world examples, discuss different types of side-channels, and examine how attackers exploit indirect information like power, timing, and sound. By the end of this session, you will be able to explain why physical and behavioral signals matter in security, and how a system can be protected against such attacks. Side-channel attacks do not rely on traditional software vulnerabilities, instead, they exploit information leaked through physical or indirect channels during computation. This might include power consumption, electromagnetic radiation, timing information, or even sound produced by a device. Even strong cryptographic systems can be compromised if side-channels are not properly secured. There are several types of side-channel attacks that attackers can exploit. Timing analysis uses variations in processing time to infer secret values. Power analysis looks at changes in energy usage during computation. Electromagnetic attacks capture data from emitted signals. Acoustic analysis uses sound patterns, such as keyboard clicks, to deduce information. Each method reveals sensitive data without directly breaking encryption. Most side-channel attacks require physical access or close proximity to the target device. Attackers measure power usage, timing, or emission during sensitive operations like decryption. They then use statistical analysis and modeling to extract private keys or passwords. Some attacks can even be carried out using compromised smartphones, microphones, or cameras placed nearby. Timing attacks measure how long it takes a device to complete certain operations. For example, if password validation stops at the first incorrect character, attackers can guess one letter at a time. Similarly, cryptographic algorithms that take variable time depending on input can leak bits of a private key. This type of attack has been successfully used against poorly implemented RSA and Advanced Encryption Standard, AES, algorithms. Power analysis attacks monitor the electrical power used by a device. Simple power analysis looks for a pattern that reveals instructions of data. Differential power analysis uses a statistical method to compare many power traces and recover secret keys. Smartcards and embedded devices are especially vulnerable to power analysis if not protected by special hardware. Electromagnetic attacks use antennas or probes to capture signals emitted by processor or memory components. These emissions can carry traces of processed data. Acoustic attacks record sounds from keyboard or CPU fans to extract type text or processing information. In one study, researchers extracted RSA keys from a laptop using a nearby phone's microphone. One well-known side-channel attack targeted a cryptographic library running on a smart card. Researchers measured power traces and extracted secret keys in just a minute. Another case involved a computer placed near a wall attacking three cryptographic keys by analyzing electromagnetic leaks through the wall. These cases highlight how side-channels can be exploited even in secure environments. Defending against side-channel attacks requires both software and hardware strategies. Use constant-time complexity algorithms that execute in the same runtime regardless of input. Add noise to power usage or timing to make analysis harder. Shield hardware to reduce electromagnetic emissions. Use secure enclaves or coprocessor to isolate sensitive computations. Use good physical security and monitoring to also reduce exposure to distress. To summarize, side-channel attacks bypass traditional defenses by exploiting physical signals and behavior. They can compromise secure systems by measuring power, time, sound, and radiation. Defense includes constant-time programming, hardware, shielding, and noise injection.

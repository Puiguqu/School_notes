Smart contracts are defined as self-executing programs where the terms are coded directly into software. They operate on a blockchain, automatically executing when predefined conditions are met. This process ensures that the contract is transparent, tamper-proof, and operates without human intervention. Crucially, once deployed, these smart contracts cannot be altered or changed. They are coded using specific languages like Solidity and are triggered by transactions or inputs on blockchain platforms, such as Ethereum. This mechanism makes them highly effective for automating actions and enforcing established rules. Their adoption significantly reduces operational costs by eliminating the need for traditional intermediaries or manual validation. Furthermore, they automate processes, ensuring improved speed, increased accuracy, and building trust through their inherent transparency and tamper-proof nature. In finance, smart contracts manage both automated payments and insurance claims. Within healthcare, they are utilized to manage medical data sharing and consent. Supply chains benefit by using smart contracts to track goods and verify product origins. Real estate transactions are becoming entirely digital, and the contracts are also employed in school services and digital legal agreements. Despite their benefits, several significant risks and limitations exist that must be noted. If there is a bug in the code, the contract may behave in an unpredictable manner. The code is final, meaning that errors cannot be easily reversed, and network scalability remains a limitation. Legal enforcement varies regionally, and industry regulations are still rapidly evolving. The principle of automated execution was demonstrated using a flight delay insurance contract, where the system checked a flight status via an API to pay compensation automatically, bypassing the claim process entirely. Overall, smart contracts represent a major advancement in secure, automated transactions and will redefine digital trust.

We will explore how blockchain can mitigate modern security risks and help build trust in digital systems. By the end of this session, you will identify major cybersecurity challenges, understand how blockchain addresses these, review real-world blockchain use cases, and consider key benefits and risks. Cybersecurity today is challenged by centralized systems that are vulnerable to attack. As data breaches become more common, user trust continues to diminish. Blockchain offers strong solutions. Decentralization prevents central point of failure. Data on change is cryptographically secure and immutable ledger ensures traceability. Smart contracts even automate responses to specific threats. In identity management, blockchain supports self-sovereign identity models. This means users own and control their credentials, reducing risk of identity theft and unauthorized access. IoT devices are vulnerable because of weak authentication. Blockchain allows these devices to securely verify themselves. It provides a tamper-proof audit trail of their activity and eliminates their reliance on centralized controllers. Real-world implementations are already happening. Banks use blockchain to prevent fraud. Hospitals use it to protect patient data. And governments test secure digital IDs and voting systems. Despite its promise, blockchain isn't perfect. It still faces challenges like scalability and network latency, energy usage for some consensus models, and integration with existing IT environments. Legal and regularity clarity is also evolving. In conclusion, blockchain is a powerful ally for cybersecurity by enhancing it with decentralized, verifiable systems and offering greater resilience and transparency.

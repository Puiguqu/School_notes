As blockchain continues to expand, it raises a number of important ethical questions around privacy, power and social responsibility. By the end of this session, you will understand the ethical dimensions of blockchain applications, explore privacy, accountability and governance challenges, evaluate the social impact of decentralized systems, reflect on responsible use of blockchain technology. One of the most debated ethical topics is data privacy and ownership. On a public blockchain, anyone can access transaction records, even with pseudonyms. This raises concerns over data control and ownership. This raises concerns about the ethical implications of permanent data storage. While transparency is a strength of blockchain, not all information should be openly visible. Ethical design must strike a balance between transparency and confidentiality, possibly through selective disclosure or private chains. Decentralized systems often lack clear accountability. Who is responsible for blockchain-based decisions? These systems challenge autonomous smart contracts and are legally and morally ambiguous. Blockchain's environmental footprint, especially from mining, is a serious concern. Ethical development calls for more energy-efficient alternatives like proof-of-work or hybrid models and a push towards sustainable consensus mechanisms. Blockchain has the potential to increase inclusion and worsen inequality. Developers must consider accessibility, affordability and digital literacy in their designs to ensure equity. With few global standards, regulation lags behind technology and there are often conflicts between decentralization and regulation. The ethical use of blockchain requires careful collaboration between developers, lawmakers and civil society to avoid harm and enable good. To conclude, blockchain can drive significant change but we must confront its ethical implications directly. Balancing innovation with accountability is key.

Digital Forensic Analysis (DFA) is defined as the process of examining digital data to find and interpret legal or investigative evidence. This methodology is broadly applied across law enforcement, cybersecurity, Human Resources, and regulatory audits. Digital evidence sources are diverse, including hard drives, SSDs, volatile RAM, networks, mobile devices, cloud systems, and system logs. Evidence can take many forms, such as user-created documents, chats, application logs, browser history, and crucial metadata like timestamps. Investigators must adopt a methodical approach starting with identifying the data relevant to the case. The process involves three main stages: evidence preservation, extraction, and detailed examination. Proper evidence collection is critical to prevent tampering or loss, followed by sound methods for data extraction. A fundamental principle in forensic integrity is hashing, which ensures that evidence remains unaltered throughout the investigation. Tools generate a hash both before imaging and after examination to verify the data's integrity. Common algorithms used for forensic validation include MD5, SHA-1, and SHA-256. Advanced techniques include timeline analysis, which reconstructs digital events using timestamps to link system behavior with user actions. Keyword search and filters are powerful tools used to narrow vast datasets, particularly in cases involving fraud or child protection. Popular commercial forensic tools include EnCase and FTK, while academic or open-source options are Autopsy and Volatility. Network forensics utilizes specialized tools like NetworkMiner and Wireshark for analyzing traffic captures. For formal reporting, findings must be clear, logical, legally sound, and factually defensible. Reports must systematically include timelines, screenshots, hash verification, source information, and detailed logs.

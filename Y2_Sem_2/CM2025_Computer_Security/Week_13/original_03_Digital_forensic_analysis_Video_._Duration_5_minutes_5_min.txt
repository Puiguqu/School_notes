Today we'll explore how investigators examine digital evidence to support legal, civil or internal cases. By the end of this session, you'll be able to define digital forensic analysis, identify types and sources of digital evidence, apply forensic methodologies to evidence handling, explore tools used in modern forensic investigations, prepare clear and admissible reports. Digital forensic analysis is the process of examining digital data to find legal or investigative evidence and interpret electronic evidence. It's used in law enforcement, cybersecurity, HR and regulatory audits. Evidence can come from many digital sources. These include hard drives and SSDs, RAM and volatile memory, networks, mobile devices, e-mails and cloud systems, and system logs and registry data. Investigators need to know where to look. Digital evidence comes in many forms such as user-created content, documents, chats, system data such as logs, timestamps, application data such as browser history, app logs, and metadata such as file properties and timestamps. Analysts must understand which data types are most useful for their case. A methodical approach is essential. Start with identifying what data is relevant. Preserve evidence to prevent tampering or loss. with proper collection, extracting data using sound methods. Then move to examination for an initial look for suspicious content. A deeper analysis will establish evidence and finally structured reporting with clear, factual, and structured documentation. Hashing ensures that evidence has not been altered. Tools generate a hash before imaging and after examination to verify data integrity. Common algorithms used in forensic validation are MD5, SHA-1, and SHA-256. Timeline analysis allows investigators to reconstruct digital events using timestamps. It helps link system behaviour with user actions and uncover when and how an incident unfolded. Keyword search and filters help investigators narrow down vast data sets by identifying relevant documents, names, and email addresses. They are especially useful in cases involving child protection, fraud, and IP theft. Digital forensic professionals rely on powerful tools to collect and analyse data. EnCase and FTK are popular commercial options, while Autopsy and Volatility are widely used in academia and open-source investigations. Other popular tools are Sleuth Kit and X-Ways Forensics and NetworkMiner and Wireshark for network forensics. When presenting evidence, it's crucial that reports are clear, logical, and legally sound. Reports must include timelines, screenshots, hash verification, source information, and logs. Findings must remain factual and legally defensible. To conclude, digital forensic analysis is the foundation of credible investigations and turns digital traces into evidence that tells a story.

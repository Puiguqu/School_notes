Digital Forensics Basics involves exploring the foundational concepts, tools, and practices required to analyze and present digital evidence. The scope of digital forensics is vast, covering everything from cybercrime investigations to intellectual property theft and regulatory compliance. Digital evidence can originate from diverse sources, including hard drives, USB/SSDs, emails, instant messages, and cloud accounts. Furthermore, critical data points include browsing history, cache, log files, and physical records. The successful investigation hinges on knowing precisely where to look and how to access the data without compromising its integrity. To ensure evidence admissibility, the digital forensic process involves seven mandatory phases. These phases are: identification, preservation, collection, examination, analysis, documentation, and presentation. Crucially, every single phase must be thoroughly documented for use in legal settings, audits, or internal reviews. Practically, in a phishing incident, forensic teams examine headers, attachments, and logs to uncover malicious redirections. Similarly, investigating suspicious data movement requires USB log analysis and endpoint forensics to prove unauthorized file transfers. Key tools essential to the field include FTK Imager and NCase, used for imaging and initial analysis. Volatility is a specialized tool designed specifically for memory analysis, a complex technical area. For monitoring network traffic and traces, Wireshark is the indispensable utility. To maintain the legal integrity of all findings, several best practices must be strictly followed. These practices include using write blockers and imaging tools to preserve the original data state. Maintaining a detailed chain of custody is absolutely mandatory when handling digital evidence. Analysts must also observe every action taken, respecting both user privacy and established legal boundaries.

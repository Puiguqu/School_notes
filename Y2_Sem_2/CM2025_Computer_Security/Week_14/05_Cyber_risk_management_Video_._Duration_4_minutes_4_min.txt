# Cyber risk management

1. Cyber Risk Management is a structured process designed to help organizations understand and manage potential harm resulting from cyber threats.
2. Cyber risk is defined as the possibility of loss, damage, or disruption stemming from cyber threats.
3. These threats can cause operational disruptions, financial losses, legal action, or reputational harm.
4. Cyber risks originate from vulnerabilities and threats within systems and processes, acting upon assets at risk.
5. Common cyber risks include malware and ransomware infections, phishing attacks, and social engineering schemes.
6. Other sources of risk involve weak third-party security, unpatched systems, API abuse, or misconfigured cloud services.
7. The foundational cyber risk lifecycle involves five stages: identification, assessment, mitigation, monitoring, and response.
8. During identification, it is crucial to understand all assets, associated threats, and systemic vulnerabilities.
9. Assessment requires evaluating the likelihood and potential impact of identified risks, sometimes using risk matrices.
10. Organizations utilize various tools and methodologies for assessment, including Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) and vulnerability scanners.
11. Assessment can be qualitative (e.g., low, medium, high) or quantitative, translating potential losses into financial terms using methods like Factor Analysis of Information Risk.
12. Major risk frameworks used to standardize management include NIST’s Risk Management Framework and ISO/IEC 27005.
13. Mitigation strategies employ diverse controls: technical (e.g., firewalls, encryption, Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)), administrative (training, policies, access reviews), and physical.
14. Incident response planning involves simulating and executing protocols for managing real-world breaches.
15. A practical example demonstrated how a misconfigured S3 bucket was assessed as high impact with moderate likelihood and mitigated by securing permissions and implementing cloud security posture management.
16. Furthermore, specialized risk reduction measures include vendor risk assessments and purchasing cyber insurance.
17. Beyond the current module, the curriculum covers structured study across multiple topics, beginning with threat identification and classification.
18. Future study delves into core operational security, covering Network and OS security, and principles of Application security testing.
19. A major technical focus involves Cryptography in practice, detailing encryption, Public Key Infrastructure (PKI), digital signatures, and hash functions.
20. The course expands into advanced concepts, including Blockchain for cybersecurity and critical Incident response and forensics procedures.
21. Subsequent weeks will explore specialized, emerging technologies, notably AI, Machine Learning, quantum computing, and post-quantum cryptography.
22. Ethical and professional considerations, alongside sustainable digital ecosystems and EDI principles, form a key part of the curriculum.
23. The scope culminates in real-world cybersecurity case studies, requiring students to design, test, and communicate security solutions.
24. The material provides continuous learning opportunities, including dedicated lessons on the Incident Response lifecycle and digital forensics basics.
25. Ultimately, mastering these principles allows organizations to proactively align technical security efforts with overarching business priorities for survival in modern threat landscapes.

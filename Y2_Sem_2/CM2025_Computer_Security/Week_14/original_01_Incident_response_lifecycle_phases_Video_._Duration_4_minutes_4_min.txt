Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Welcome to this session on the Incidence Response Lifecycle. When an incident occurs, organizations need a clear, repeatable process to manage and learn from it. By the end of this session, you will be able to describe the full Incidence Response Lifecycle, understand the rule and purpose of each phase, apply the process to practical situations. Use post-incidence learning to improve preparedness. The Incidence Response Lifecycle is a standardized strategic approach for managing cybersecurity events. It helps reduce impact and ensures that teams recover faster and learn from events. Today, we'll focus on the most common model, the NIST Computer Security Incident Handling Guide, SB 800261. Preparation is key. This phase involves developing and testing incident response plans, training teams and assigning rules, implementing monitoring and detection tools, establishing legal and communication protocols. The stronger the preparation, the more effective the responses will be. Detection and analysis involve analysts, monitor systems and networks for sign-off incidents, user logs, alerts and anomalies for identification. Classifying and prioritizing incidents. Documenting all findings and timelines. The third phase is action-oriented. It includes short-term containment, that is, stopping the incidents, eradication by removing the threats, for example, malware and recovery by restoring systems and validating full functionality. For the long-term containment, it's critical to prevent reinfection or damage. After the event, organizations should conduct post-incidence reviews to identify what went well and what can be improved. This is an opportunity to revise policy and procedures, improve detection and response tools. Archive evidence and reports for compliance. This lifecycle is iterative. Lessons from one incident influence how you prepare for the next. To summarize, the lifecycle starts with preparation, moves to detection and analysis, containment, eradication and recovery are next. And finally, post-incidence activity. Visualizing the cycle will help you to reinforce its flow and importance. In summary, having an incidence-response lifecycle provides an organization with a structure and clarity during high-stress cyber events. ing it ensures that an organization has coordinated, effective and legally sound responses.

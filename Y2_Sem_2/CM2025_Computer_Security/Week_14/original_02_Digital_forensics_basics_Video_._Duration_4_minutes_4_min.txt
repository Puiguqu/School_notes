In this session, we'll explore the foundational concepts, tools, and practices used to analyze and present digital evidence. By the end of this session, you will define digital forensics and its scope, identify types and sources of digital evidence, describe the forensic investigation processes, recognize tools and best practices, explore case examples to connect theory to practice. Digital forensic is used in a wide variety of situations. From cybercrime investigation, ransomware, phishing, intellectual property theft, and insider threat analysis to employee's misconduct, HR disputes, regulatory compliance, or breach notification. Digital evidence can be obtained from a variety of sources, hard drives, USB, and SSDs, emails, instance messages, and documents, browsing history and cache, mobile devices, and cloud accounts, log files, and even records. The key is knowing where to look and how to access it without compromise. There are seven phases of the digital forensic process to ensure credibility, identification, preservation, collection, examination, analysis, documentation, presentation. Each phase must be documented clearly so that evidence can be used in code, audits, or internal reviews. Let's look at some examples of how digital forensics can be applied. In a phishing incident, a user reports a suspicious email. The forensic team extracts and examines the headers and attachments, uncovers the redirection to the malicious links from the log and checks the browser history and file system to show the drafted payload. The team then logs and collects screenshots for HR to use as legal evidence. In another case, suspicious data movement from an internal server leads to a USB log analysis, which shows that a copy has been made to a removable drive. Endpoint forensics shows that there have been unauthorized file transfer. With strong documentation and evidence, HR can take appropriate legal action. Several tools are used in digital forensics. FTK Imager and NCase for imaging and analysis, Volatility for memory analysis, Wireshark for network traces. These tools help extract and verify evidence effectively. To maintain integrity, follow these best practices. Use right blockers and imaging tools, preserve original data, keep a detailed chain of custody, observe every action, respect user privacy and legal boundaries. These rules uphold legal and ethical standards. To conclude, digital forensics is essential for uncovering the truth in digital investigations. With methodical processes and tools, analysts can translate data into evidence.

This topic helps us understand how organisations manage potential harm caused by cyber threats through structured planning and action. By the end of this session, you'll be able to define cyber risk and its drivers, describe how to identify and mitigate risk, understand the cyber risk management life cycle, use risk frameworks and models, apply best practices through real examples. Cyber risk is the possibility of loss, damage, or disruption from cyber threats. It can lead to operational disruptions, financial losses, legal action, or reputational harm. It stems from assets at risk, and vulnerabilities and threats in systems and processes. Common cyber risks include malware and ransomware infections, phishing attacks and social engineering, internal misuse such as threats and data leakage. Others stem from weak third-party security, unpatched systems, API abuse, or misconfigured cloud services. The cyber risk lifecycle includes identifying risks - understanding assets, threats, vulnerabilities, assessing by evaluating their likelihood and impact, deciding how to mitigate by implementing controls and strategies, and monitoring change by continuously tracking the risk environment, and responding to incidents. This cycle enables continuous improvement. Organisations use risk matrices, scoring models such as Common Vulnerability Scoring System, and vulnerability scanners and threat intelligence feeds to assess risks. Some use qualitative approaches, for example, low, medium, and high, while others quantify potential losses in financial terms. Frameworks help to standardise risk management. NIST's Risk Management Framework and ISO/IEC 27005 are widely used. Factor Analysis of Information Risk helps to translate risk into monetary value. COSO offers enterprise-wide governance for risk programs. Cyber risk mitigation involves using technical controls such as firewalls, encryption, and MFAs, administrative controls like training, policies, and access reviews, and incident response planning and simulations. Vendor risk assessments and cyber insurance also help reduce risk exposure. Let's look at a real-world example. A misconfigured S3 bucket left sensitive data exposed. Security teams assessed the risk as high impact with a moderate likelihood. They mitigated the risk by securing permissions and implementing cloud security posture management. Monitoring was deployed on alerts and continuous compliance tools and incident response protocols such as notification, forensic analysis and lessons learned used to manage the impact. To summarise, cyber risk management aligns technical security efforts with business priorities. Organisations that treat risk proactively are better positioned to survive modern threats.

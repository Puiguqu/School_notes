# Quantum computing and cryptography

1. The session provides an exploration of how quantum computing impacts cryptographic systems and discusses implemented defensive strategies.
2. Key learning objectives include explaining quantum computing mechanics, understanding threats to classical encryption, identifying post-quantum techniques, and detailing quantum key distribution (QKD).
3. Quantum computing leverages qubits, which are quantum bits capable of existing as 0, 1, or both simultaneously due to the principle of superposition.
4. These systems utilize entanglement and quantum gates to process information, enabling them to solve certain problems far faster than traditional classical computers.
5. Unlike classical systems processing one state at a time, quantum computers process multiple possibilities concurrently, allowing for complex calculations like integer factorization with high efficiency.
6. A primary theoretical concern is Shor’s algorithm, which can exponentially accelerate the factoring of large numbers.
7. This exponential speedup poses a direct threat, as it undermines the security foundation of classic cryptographic standards like RSA, DSA, and ECC by cracking their private keys.
8. Consequently, once scalable quantum computers are realized, currently deployed systems relying on RSA, DSA, and ECC will become cryptographically insecure.
9. Symmetric systems, such as the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES), exhibit greater inherent resilience to quantum attacks but will necessitate the use of significantly longer key lengths to remain secure.
10. While hashing functions are noted as being less immediately affected, they remain vulnerable to quantum-based attacks.
11. Quantum cryptography aims to develop methods that resist compromise from both classical and quantum computing adversaries.
12. This objective drives the standardization of new post-quantum algorithms (PQC).
13. Specifically, Kyber and Deletium are cited as replacement standards for RSA and ECC, utilizing advanced lattice-based cryptography.
14. The overall PQC landscape also includes code-based multivariant systems and hash-based systems.
15. Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) is a method allowing two parties to securely share encryption keys by exploiting fundamental quantum properties.
16. QKD's security mechanism is predicated on the principle that any attempt by an eavesdropper to intercept the transmission will inevitably alter the quantum states, thereby alerting the users.
17. Real-world deployments of such technology, exemplified by systems like China Satellite, are currently being implemented.
18. Despite these advanced concepts and implementations, significant challenges persist, including the immaturity of quantum hardware itself.
19. Furthermore, the industry faces the complex task of migrating to PQC across existing systems, which demands comprehensive, system-wide upgrades.
20. Effective cybersecurity requires striking a necessary balance among performance requirements, scalability constraints, and security level enhancements.
21. To manage this transition, dedicated educational efforts and robust transition planning are deemed absolutely essential.
22. Quantum computing, therefore, presents both considerable risks and revolutionary opportunities for the entire cybersecurity domain.
23. The takeaway emphasizes that proactive adoption of a post-quantum strategy and a thorough understanding of the underlying technology are mandatory.
24. The curriculum outlines a detailed 22-week structure covering cyber threats, security frameworks, network/OS security, application security, and specialized topics like blockchain and AI/ML.
25. The final stage of study, scheduled for Week 22, is dedicated entirely to the critical subject of Quantum Computing and Cryptography.

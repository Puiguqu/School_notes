# Post-quantum cryptography

1. This session introduces Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC), exploring cryptographic approaches designed to withstand future quantum computational threats.
2. PQC defines cryptographic methods secured even against quantum computers, representing a necessary replacement for vulnerable classical encryption techniques.
3. Unlike traditional methods, PQC designs are not based on integer factorization or discrete logarithms, which are susceptible to quantum attacks.
4. The urgent need for PQC stems from powerful quantum algorithms, such as Shor's algorithm, which can theoretically break foundational systems like RSA and ECC.
5. Even if large-scale quantum computers do not yet exist, sensitive data intercepted today risks decryption in the future.
6. Multiple distinct families of PQC algorithms have been developed to address this threat landscape.
7. These families include lattice-based algorithms, represented by candidates like Kyber and Dilithium.
8. Code-based cryptography is exemplified by algorithms such as McEliece.
9. Other architectural approaches include multivariate schemes, historically represented by Rainbow, and hash-based methods, such as Sphinx.
10. The global NIST PQC project, which commenced in 2016, is a critical international effort coordinating these standards.
11. Currently, Kyber has emerged as a leading candidate for encryption, while Dilithium is a primary candidate for digital signatures.
12. Industry standards are actively being prepared and formalized through the NIST process to finalize these replacements.
13. A primary technical characteristic of PQC algorithms is that they often possess larger key and output sizes compared to classical RSA or ECC implementations.
14. Despite these increased overheads, the resilience of PQC against quantum attacks makes this performance trade-off worthwhile.
15. Implementing PQC necessitates careful, comprehensive planning to ensure a smooth transition.
16. One highly recommended and safe deployment strategy is the adoption of hybrid cryptography.
17. Hybrid cryptography involves running both classical and PQC algorithms in parallel to ensure continuous compatibility and robust safety.
18. Successful PQC deployment requires more than just algorithmic updates; it demands thorough planning and systematic testing.
19. The associated adoption challenges are significant and multi-layered, including hardware compatibility gaps.
20. Additionally, systemic updates are required for protocols and software, affecting firmware, applications, and network infrastructure.
21. Effective organizational change also mandates comprehensive training and awareness programs for technical staff and end-users.
22. Overall, PQC is essential for securing the digital infrastructure against future quantum computing threats.

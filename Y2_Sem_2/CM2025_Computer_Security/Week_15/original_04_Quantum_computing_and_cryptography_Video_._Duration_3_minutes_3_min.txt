In this video, we'll explore how quantum computing impacts cryptographic systems and what defense can be implemented. By the end of this session, you will be able to explain how quantum computing works, understand why quantum computing threatens classical encryption, identify post-quantum cryptographic techniques, explore quantum key distribution and its applications. Quantum computing is based on quantum bits or qubits that can be 0, 1, or both at the same time due to superposition. It uses entanglement and quantum gates to process information and solve some problems much faster than traditional computers. Unlike classical systems that process one state at a time, quantum computers can process many possibilities at once. This enables them to perform complex calculations, like integer factorization, more efficiently. One of the most discussed risks is Shor's algorithm. It can factor large numbers exponentially faster, undermining RSA and ECC security by cracking private keys. Once scalable quantum computers exist, current systems will become insecure. RSA, DSA, and ECC will be breakable by quantum attacks. Symmetric systems like the Advanced Encryption Standard or AES are more resilient but will require longer keys. Hashing functions are less affected but still vulnerable. Quantum cryptography aims to resist attacks by quantum and classical computers. This is standardizing new post-quantum algorithms. For example, Kyber and Deletium will replace RSA and ECC in secure systems by using lattice-based cryptography and there will be code-based multivariant and hash-based systems. Quantum key distribution allows two parties to share encryption keys using quantum properties. If someone tries to intercept, it alters the quantum states, alerting the users. Real-world QKD systems are being deployed such as China Satellite. Despite the promise, challenges remain. Quantum hardware isn't mature and migrating to PQC across systems requires system-wide upgrades. A balance needs to be struck between performance, scalability, and securities education and transition planning are essential. To conclude, quantum computing offers risks and opportunities for cybersecurity. Proactive adoption of post-quantum strategy and understanding the technology is essential.

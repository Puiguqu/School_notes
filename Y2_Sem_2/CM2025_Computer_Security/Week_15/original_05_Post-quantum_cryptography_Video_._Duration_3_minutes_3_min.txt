In this session, we explore cryptographic approaches designed to withstand quantum threats. After this session, you will understand the need for post-quantum cryptography or PQC, learn about key algorithms, explore NIST processes, and identify deployment challenges and steps. PQC refers to cryptographic methods that are secure even against quantum computers. These are not based on integer factorization or discrete locks, which quantum computers can break, they are designed to replace vulnerable classical encryption. We need PQC due to the threat from quantum algorithms, like Shor's, that can break RSA and ECC. Even if quantum computers don't exist yet, at scale, sensitive data intercepted today could be decrypted in the future. There are multiple families of PQC algorithms: lattice-based like Kyber and Dilithium, code-based like McEliece, multivariant like Rainbow, and hash-based like Sphinx. The NIST PQC project, which began in 2016, has involved global cryptography experts. Kyber and Dilithium have emerged as a leading candidate for encryption and signatures. Final standards are being prepared. Compared to RSA or ECC, PQC algorithms often have larger key and output sizes. Although their performance is still being optimized, their resilience to quantum attacks makes the trade off worthwhile. Implementing PQC requires careful planning. One recommended approach is hybrid cryptography, using both classical and PQC algorithms in parallel to ensure compatibility and safety. Its deployment must be planned with testing and support. Adoption challenges include the lack of building hardware support, the need for protocol and software updates, and the need for training and awareness. Updates will be needed across firmware, apps, and networks. In summary, post-quantum cryptography will secure our digital future, so organizations should develop hybrid strategies and be prepared.

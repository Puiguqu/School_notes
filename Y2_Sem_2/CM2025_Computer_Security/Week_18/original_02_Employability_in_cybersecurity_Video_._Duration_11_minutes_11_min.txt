Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Hello, I'm Liz Wilkinson, Senior Careers Consultant at the University of London Career Service and Technology Careers Lead. I am here to give you an overview of possible career paths in cybersecurity. Cybersecurity is one of the most in-demand fields in technology today and it's evolving fast. As a computer science student, it offers you a career that's not only well paid, but also meaningful because you're protecting data, people and critical systems from attack. This video will guide you through the starting points in cybersecurity, the different team types you could work in and the wide range of roles available. We'll explore the technical skills, certifications and global employability skills which will help you develop your career. We'll also show you how AI tools and adopting a transdisciplinary approach can give you an edge. Let's get started. Most people begin their cybersecurity journey in security operations, often called the SOC or Security Operations Centre. This is the frontline where threats are detected, investigated and responded to. Typical entry roles include a security analyst, SOC analysis, monitoring alerts, reviewing suspicious activity and escalating incidents. An incident response technician involved in containing breaches and helping to restore systems. An IT support with security focus, handling user issues while managing access controls and security updates. The Security Operations Centre gives you exposure to real threats, tools and workflows. It's like the emergency room of cybersecurity and an excellent place to learn fast. As you progress, you'll hear about red, blue and purple teams, which are three different approaches to cybersecurity. The red team works in offensive security and involves ethical hackers simulating attacks to find weaknesses. The blue team is responsible for defensive security, protecting networks, detecting attacks and responding to threats. The purple team is a collaborative mix of both, sharing tactics to strengthen defences. Many beginners start in blue team roles because these roles build a strong defensive foundation. Over time, you may move into red or purple teamwork to develop a more complete skill set. Cybersecurity isn't only about technical hacking. There are varied roles where human behaviour, legal frameworks and business strategy matter just as much as code. For example, a social engineering specialist tests people's awareness through phishing campaigns or physical security tests. A compliance and regulation analyst ensures that organisations meet laws like GDPR or frameworks like ISO 27001. And a cybersecurity sales engineer helps companies choose and implement the right security products. These roles show that there's space in cybersecurity for people with skills in psychology, law or business, not just coding. These roles can be great roles for career changes moving into the technology sector. This module provides you with skills that are central to becoming a cybersecurity professional. These include systems and network security such as threat identification and classification, IDS IPS, authentication, incident response and forensics encryption, applied cryptography, blockchain and emergency technologies and secure coding and debugging, understanding vulnerabilities and applying application security principles. As well as these technical skills, this module emphasises designing solutions that are sustainable and inclusive, which are key areas of concern in the cybersecurity industry. You don't need to obtain every certification in cybersecurity at once, but a few can really boost your chances of getting hired. Pick certifications that match your intended career path. Managing your effort and time wisely will get the best return. Cybersecurity isn't just about technology, it's about people. Many breaches happen because of human error and explaining risks to non-technical colleagues is crucial. By studying cybersecurity, you will gain these key global employability skills. Adaptability and resilience, adjusting quickly to new threats and tools. Collaboration, working across IT, legal and operation teams. Communication, translating technical threats into business language. Complex problem solving, analysing incomplete data to make decisions. Employers often say they can teach technical skills, but it is the interpersonal skills, the self-management and work approach skills that make someone a truly effective cybersecurity professional. Developing and demonstrating your interpersonal work approach and self-management effectiveness can make you stand out as a cybersecurity candidate. To move into leadership, roles such as becoming a CISO, Chief Information Security Officer, you'll need more than technical expertise. Leaders in cybersecurity bridge the gap between technical teams and business executives. You can really accelerate your career by developing and demonstrating qualities such as strategic thinking, aligning security with business goals, risk management, understanding which threats matter most. People leadership, managing teams, budgets and stakeholders. Decision-making under pressure, handling incidents calmly and effectively. Cybersecurity touches every industry, from finance and healthcare to manufacturing and government. This means solutions often require expertise from multiple disciplines and domain knowledge. For example, healthcare security needs knowledge of medical data privacy. Industrial control system security requires understanding of engineering processes and legal compliance demands awareness of local and global regulations. The more you understand other fields, the more valuable you are because cyber threats rarely live in purely technical silos. So demonstrating that you can look at problems from different angles and listen to other perspectives is a valuable approach to have as a cybersecurity professional. Knowing the industry you're protecting gives you a massive edge. You'll understand the systems, jargon and what attackers might target. For example, a former banker in cybersecurity can spot fraud risks faster. A healthcare worker turned security analyst can anticipate patient data vulnerabilities. For career changes, your previous domain knowledge is a superpower. Don't hide it, combine it with cybersecurity skills and you'll stand out in a crowded market. AI is transforming cybersecurity and it can accelerate your career if you know how to use it, such as automating repetitive monitoring tasks, analysing large data sets to detect anomalies, assisting in malware classification and threat intelligence, helping with report generation and documentation. Learning how AI tools work and their limitations will make you a more efficient and future-proof cybersecurity professional. Here are my final tips for developing your career in cybersecurity. Tip 1. Start small, but start now, such as lab projects, online challenges and internships. Tip 2. Keep learning. The field and its problem-solving approaches evolve daily. Tip 3. Build your network. Join cybersecurity communities and forums. Tip 4. Document your work. A GitHub portfolio or blog can showcase your skills. Tip 5. Stay ethical. Integrity is your most valuable currency in this field. Cybersecurity isn't just a job. It's a career that can take you anywhere in the world, offering diverse career paths and challenges. The University of London Career Service is here to help.

Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Welcome to this session on the anatomy of real-world cybersecurity incidents. In this lesson, we'll dissect how attacks happen, what makes them successful, and what we can learn from past cases. By the end of this session, you should be able to identify the stages of a typical cyber attack, recognize common attack vectors and techniques, analyze high-profile cybersecurity incidents, learn key lessons for prevention and response. Most cyber attacks follow a repeatable pattern. It begins with reconnaissance, where attackers collect intelligence. Then they breach defenses, gain access via phishing, exploits, or misconfigurations. They gain deeper access by escalating privileges, before moving laterally, exploring internal systems. From there, they extract data, stealing sensitive information, and cover their tracks by deleting logs and via backdoors. Common attack vectors involve phishing and other forms of social engineering, exploit unpatched systems, steal poor passwords, gain unauthorized access via insider threats, and human error, and exploit third-party supply chain vulnerabilities. Let's preview the 2017 Equifax breach. A non-Apache-estrus vulnerability went unpatched, resulting in 147 million people's personal data being stolen. The poor system design and lack of basic control meant that the breach went undetected for months, resulting in a delayed response. To reduce the impact of a cyber attack and recover faster, organizations should have a solid incident response plan that includes preparation, policy, tools, training, detection, and analysis, monitoring threat intelligence, containment and eradication, isolating and cleaning systems, recovery, restoring operations, lessons learned, improving security posture. Lessons learned from real incidents are that often the issue isn't the attack itself, it's that businesses overlook basic security measures. Mispatches, poor visibility, and weak policies contribute more than advanced threats and lead to delays in detection that increase the damage. Clear incident response plans are therefore vital. Transparency and communication matter. Cyber incidents persist because complex digital systems grow faster than security resources. Many businesses still under-invest in security and struggle with scale gaps, and a lack of awareness and policy enforcement. Cyber security incidents often follow repeatable patterns. Predicting them allows us to predict, prevent, and prepare by building more resilient systems.

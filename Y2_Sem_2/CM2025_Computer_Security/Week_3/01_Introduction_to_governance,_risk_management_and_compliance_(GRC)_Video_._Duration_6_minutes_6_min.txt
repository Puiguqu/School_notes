Governance, Risk Management, and Compliance (GRC) is a critical topic in cybersecurity, recognizing that technical defenses are insufficient without strong governance, proper risk planning, and legal compliance. GRC fundamentally represents the three areas of responsibility for all cybersecurity operations. Governance dictates how an organization is directed and controlled, encompassing leadership, roles, accountability, and established policies. Risk Management focuses proactively on identifying potential threats, such as system vulnerabilities, human error, or supply chain weaknesses. Compliance involves meeting legal, regulatory, or contractual requirements, citing examples like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) or International Organization for Standardization (ISO) standards. Together, GRC ensures that all security decisions align perfectly with both business goals and legal obligations.

Governance elevates cybersecurity from a mere technical issue to a fundamental business priority, defining who is responsible for decision-making and promoting ethical behavior. Risk management requires assessing the likelihood and potential damage of identified threats, necessitating mitigation, acceptance, or continuous monitoring of risks. Compliance protects organizations from fines, reputational harm, and lawsuits by adhering to standards like the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) or PCI DSS for financial institutions. When working together, Governance defines roles and policies, Risk Management identifies threats, and Compliance enforces required standards, building trust and resilience.

Weeks 4 through 12 cover Topic 2, spanning various cybersecurity frameworks, standards, and foundational topics. Week 5 and Week 6 dedicate attention to Network and OS security principles. Week 7 and Week 8 delve into Application security principles, including testing and debugging.

Week 9 and Week 10 are dedicated to Cryptography in practice, covering encryption, Public Key Infrastructure (PKI), digital signatures, and hash functions. Subsequent weeks cover advanced topics: Week 11 and Week 12 address Blockchain for cybersecurity. Week 13 and Week 14 are reserved for Incident response and forensics procedures. The learning continues with Week 15 and Week 16, addressing emerging trends such as Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), quantum computing, and IoT security challenges. The curriculum concludes by Week 17 and Week 18, focusing on EDI principles, sustainable digital ecosystems, and employability. Final units include Week 19 and Week 20 for real-world cybersecurity case studies, design, testing, and communicating security solutions, leading up to Week 21 and Week 22 for examination preparation.

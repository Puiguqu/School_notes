Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Welcome to this session on Cybersecurity Standards and Policies In this video, you learned the difference between standards and policy, why they are both crucial in building a secure digital environment, and how organizations apply them to manage risk and support compliance. You'll also identify examples of global standards. Let's begin with some key definitions. Cybersecurity standards are formalized, best practice frameworks used to help organizations secure their information systems. They are typically developed by international and national organizations, such as ISO, NIS or the British Standards Institution. Standards help companies implement effective control, assess risk, and comply with laws and regulations. They are not always mandatory, but many organizations voluntarily adopt them to show commitment to cybersecurity. Here are four commonly recognized standards and regulations. ISO IEC 27-001 outlines how to establish an information security management system, or ISMS. NIS Cybersecurity Framework, CSF, a U.S. framework, offers guidance on identifying, protecting, detecting, responding to, and recovering from cyber threats. PCI-DSS is mandatory for organizations handling credit or debit card information. And GDPR, while not technically a cybersecurity standard, is a EU legal regulation with strict security and policy requirements. Each of these sets a baseline for what good cybersecurity looks like. What are cybersecurity policies? Cybersecurity policies are internal documents that define how an organization and its staff must behave to protect digital assets. They may cover topics such as acceptable use of systems, password management, email and internet usage, access control, and data classification. These policies help ensure consistency and accountability. They should reflect current industrial standards and legal obligations, and to be clearly communicated to all employees. Here are some common types of cybersecurity policies. An acceptable use policy, AUP, defines what staff can and cannot do on companies' systems. An access control policy details how access to the system and data is granted. A data classification policy outlines how data is labeled based on sensitivity. An incident response policy explains what to do if a breach occurs. A bring-your-own-device, BYOD, policy sets expectations for employees using personal devices for work. And a password policy ensures strong and secure authentication. Each policy should be clear, enforceable, and regularly updated. It's important to understand the difference between a standard and policy. A standard are external best practices. They are developed by formal bodies at a framework level, offer a model for building effective security controls, and are often used to measure compliance, maturity, or benchmarking. Policies are internal rules and procedures created by an organization. There are specific operational enforceable rules that ensure staff and systems align with those broader standards. In short, standards guide the structure, policies control the behavior. Why are standards and policies important? Cybersecurity standards and policies are more than paperwork. They create structures, protect data, define responsibility, reduce the risk of errors, insider threats, and legal trouble, all while increasing trust across the business. And prove to regulators and customers that the organization is serious about cybersecurity. To sum up, cybersecurity standards help organizations structure their defenses. Policies make sure staff know how to behave. Those are essential to reducing cyber risk and building trust. Reflection. Take a moment to think. Have you ever followed a policy at a school or in a job? Maybe a password rule or a no phone policy.

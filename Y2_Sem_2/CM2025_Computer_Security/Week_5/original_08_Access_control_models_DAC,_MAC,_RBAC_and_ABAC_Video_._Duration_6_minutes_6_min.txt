Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Welcome to this session on access control models. Access control is a fundamental concept in cybersecurity. It's all about deciding who gets access to what, when and how. In this session, we'll define access control, explore why access control is critical for cybersecurity. We'll introduce four key access control models and their purpose. Discretionary access control, DAC, Mandatory access control, MAC, Rule-based access control, RBAC, Attribute-based access control, ABAC. Let's begin by defining what access control is. Access control refers to the methods used to determine who is allowed to access information or resources within a system. It prevents unauthorized access, protects systems, files and data, enforces organization security policies, and ensures that users only get access to what they are supposed to do. There are different ways to implement this, and that's where our access control model comes in. Discretionary access control, or DAC, is a model where the owner of a resource decides who can access it. Let's say you create a file on your laptop. You can decide who else can open, edit, or delete it. This model is flexible but can be risky if users share access too freely. It's common in personal computers or file-sharing platforms. Mandatory access control, or MAC, is much stricter. Here, the system itself defines who can access what based on security labels or classifications like confidential, secret, or top secret. Users cannot change permissions, even if they own the data. This model is used in high-security environments such as military or government networks. It is very secure but inflexible. Rule-based access control. Rule-based access control, or RBAC, assigns access based on a person's job role. For example, a manager may access performance reviews, a finance user may access budgets, an IT administrator may access system configurations. RBAC is widely used in enterprise systems because it's easier to manage and scale well with organizations' growth. Attribute-based access control, or ABAC, is the most flexible model. Here, access is based on multiple attributes, not just user role, but also time of the day, location, device type, or data sensitivity. For example, a user might only be allowed to access sensitive data during business hours from a company device. ABAC is powerful but more complex to set up. It's used in cloud platforms and large dynamic environments. Here's a quick comparison of the four models. DAC gives control to the user, is best for small teams, is highly flexible, and has low security. MAC gives control to the system, is best for the government or military, has low flexibility and high security. RBAC uses job roles, is best for enterprises, has medium flexibility and medium security. ABAC uses flexible attributes, is best for cloud or large scale, has high flexibility and high security. Each has pros and cons. The right one depends on the organization's needs and risk tolerance. Access control is more than just managing logins. It protects sensitive data from unauthorized users, minimizes the risk of insider threats, helps meet legal and regulatory requirements like GTPR or the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, HIPAA, prevents mistakes like employees accessing systems they shouldn't. Good access control means only the right people have the right access at the right time. To summarize, access control is essential to cybersecurity. DAC, MAC, RBAC, and ABAC each offer different ways to control access. The best model depends on environment, size, and security level. Now think about this, if you were securing a university system, which model do you think would work best and why? Thanks for following along with this session on access control models.

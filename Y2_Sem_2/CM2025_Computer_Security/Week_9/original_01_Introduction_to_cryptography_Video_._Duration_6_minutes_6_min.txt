In this video, you will learn what cryptography is, why it is important and how it is used to protect information in cybersecurity. We will look at the basic concept, the most common types of cryptographic systems and how cryptography is used in real-world applications like secure messaging, banking and web browsing. What is cryptography? Cryptography is the science of securing data. It involves converting information from a readable form into something that cannot be understood without the correct method or secret key. This process is called encryption. Only someone with the correct key can return the data to its origin form, which is called decryption. Cryptography helps protect our personal message, bank details and passwords from being read by others. It is one of the most important tools in cybersecurity today. Why is cryptography so important? It allows us to protect sensitive data at the send or the sort. For example, when you log in to a website or send an email, cryptography keeps your details private and safe from others. It also helps make sure that messages have not been changed or read by someone they were not meant for. Cryptography builds trust in digital systems by making sure digital communication is private, secure and authentic. Let's us go over a few key terms. Encryption. Encryption is the process of changing information so that it cannot be understood without the key, a scrambled form of data. Decryption is the process of reversing that. A key is like a secret password or number used to make and undo the changes. A cipher is the actual method or algorithm that performs the encryption and decryption. Understanding these basic terms will help you as we look at the different types of cryptography systems. Symmetric cryptography uses a single key for both encryption and decryption. This means the same secret key is needed by both the person sending the data and the person receiving it. Because of this, the key must be shared in a safe way before communication starts. This method is fast and efficient and often used for large amounts of data, such as in secure file storage or messaging apps and so on. A common example of a symmetric algorithm is the advanced encryption standard. Asymmetric cryptography uses two different keys. One is public and can be shared with anyone. The other is private and must be kept secret. The public key is used to encrypt a message. Only the person with a matching private key can decrypt it. This method is useful when the two parties don't already share a secret key. It is widely used in secure e-mails, digital certificates, and websites that start with HTTPS. Cryptography is used all around us. When you visit a secure website, such as your bank, your browser, and so on, they all use cryptography to protect your data. Digital signatures use cryptography to confirm that a message or file really came from a trusted source. Online banking, card payment, and even secure messaging apps like Signal and WhatsApp use cryptographic methods to keep your information private and safe. Cryptography is the reason we can safely work, shop, and communicate online. Cryptography is powerful, but it's not perfect. It provides excellent protection for privacy and supports secure identity checks online. However, one of the biggest challenges is key management. If a key is lost or stolen, security can be broken. Another challenge is keeping the cryptographic method up-to-date. Older systems might use weak ciphers that can be cracked. Also, if people use weak passwords or share keys carelessly, it doesn't matter how strong the system is. So, cryptography must be combined with good security practices. To sum up, cryptography is the science of protecting information. It works by changing data into a form that only someone with the correct key can understand through encryption. You learn the differences between symmetric and asymmetric cryptography, along with real such as secure websites and messaging apps. You also saw how important it is to protect keys and use modern methods. Cryptography is the heart of cybersecurity and essential for protecting privacy and trust in a digital world.

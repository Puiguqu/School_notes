### 05_What_are_cyber_threats_Video_._Duration_6_minutes_6_min

# What are cyber threats?
Video•
. Duration: 6 minutes
6 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2025-computer-security/lecture/dKJbT/what-are-cyber-threats

This educational module addresses cyber threats, defining them as any attempt to gain unauthorized access to a system, network, or digital device with the intent to cause harm. Cyber threats affect all aspects of life, requiring vigilance for individuals, businesses, and governments alike. The most common types include malware, phishing, ransomware, social engineering, information manipulation, and supply chain attacks. Malware, or malicious software, functions like a digital virus, capable of stealing data, spying on activity, or destroying files. Phishing is a highly successful threat where attackers use seemingly legitimate emails to trick victims into revealing confidential information, such as passwords or credit card details. Ransomware locks a user's files and demands payment, presenting a growing and costly threat. Social engineering is a non-technical attack that relies on psychological manipulation, such as a caller pretending to be tech support. Information manipulation and interference involves spreading false data to confuse or influence decisions, impacting everything from elections to financial markets. A critical threat is the supply chain attack, which targets less secure elements, such as third-party vendors, to gain access to a major organization’s system. The SolarWinds attack exemplifies this, where malware was injected into a legitimate software update to infiltrate major government institutions undetected for months. Historical examples highlight the severe impact of these threats, notably the 2017 WannaCry ransomware attack, which infected over 200,000 computers globally and forced hospitals to cancel surgeries. Furthermore, the Equifax data breach in 2017 involved the theft of personal data, including social security numbers, from nearly 150 million people due to a vulnerability in update software. Cybersecurity is crucial because threats affect personal data control and, for businesses, can damage reputations and cause massive financial losses. Subsequent weeks advance the knowledge base, covering topics like cybersecurity frameworks and standards in Week 3. ### 07_Classifying_cyber_threats_Video_._Duration_7_minutes_7_min

# Classifying cyber threats
Video•
. Duration: 7 minutes
7 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2025-computer-security/lecture/8Jj0e/classifying-cyber-threats

This session provides a deep dive into the classification of cyber threats, enabling the categorization of threats based on their origin, operational vector, and resulting damage. Threat classification considers three main dimensions: who is responsible, how the attack operates, and what type of damage is inflicted. Threat origins are divided into insider threats and external threats. Insider threats stem from within the organization, involving current or former employees, contractors, or third-party partners. These can be classified as malicious, when acted upon deliberately for personal gain or revenge, or negligent, arising from accidental actions like losing a device or falling for phishing. Conversely, external threats originate outside the organization and include cybercriminals, hacker groups, state-sponsored actors, and hacktivists, exemplified by the 2017 WannaCry attack on the NHS.

Attack vectors are categorized into network-based threats and endpoint threats. Network-based threats target the digital infrastructure, such as servers, routers, or cloud systems, without needing access to a specific device. Key network attacks include Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks, which overwhelm servers with traffic, and Man-in-the-Middle attacks, where communication is secretly intercepted. Another network risk is port scanning, used to identify exploitable weaknesses across systems. Endpoint threats target the devices users actively utilize, including laptops, tablets, and smartphones. These can be delivered via infected USBs or phishing emails, or via keyloggers that steal credentials by recording every keystroke.

The impact of cyber attacks is assessed through financial and reputational damage. Financial impact can be immediate, such as paying a ransom (e.g., the Colonial Pipeline incident), or long term, involving legal fees and system downtime. Reputational damage occurs when sensitive data is exposed or systems are publicly compromised, eroding public trust. The infamous 2014 Sony Picture Hack exemplifies how data leakage can cause severe, lasting brand damage. To summarize, analysis requires considering the actor (insider vs. outsider), the vector (network vs. endpoint), and the impact (financial vs. reputational).

Incident response is covered in Weeks 13/14 (Incident response and forensics), while Week 15/16 tackle emerging trends, including AI, ML, quantum computing, and IoT security challenges. The final learning segments address advanced theory and application, encompassing Weeks 17/18 (EDI principles) and Weeks 19/20 (Real-world cybersecurity case studies). The curriculum culminates in Exam Preparation Week (Week 21) and the final Exam Week (Week 22).

2. Unlike reactive measures, CTI provides crucial contextual and timely information, enabling organizations to predict attacks and build effective defenses.
3. It encompasses detailed information, including attacker identity, motivations, methods of attack, and early warning indicators.
4. CTI is categorized into four main types, each serving different organizational levels and purposes.
5. Strategic Intelligence, aimed at senior leadership, focuses on broad trends and emerging risks for long-term planning.
6. Tactical Intelligence, valuable to security analysts, details how attackers operate, including their tools, techniques, and procedures (TTPs).
7. Operational Intelligence provides real-time insights into ongoing attack campaigns, allowing for immediate defense adjustments.
8. Technical Intelligence is the most granular form, providing specific details like malware hashes, malicious IP addresses, or phishing domains.
9. CTI enhances decision-making by transforming raw data into actionable knowledge, allowing precise risk assessment and detection.
10. Intelligence can be gathered from both internal and external sources.
11. Internal sources include valuable data derived from system logs, incident reports, and user activity.
12. External sources are diverse, ranging from government advisories, such as the UK's NCCS and the US's CISA.
13. Other vital external sources include Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT) platforms, sector-specific communities (ISACS), commercial intelligence feeds, and internal logs.
14. Effective CTI requires combining multiple sources to achieve a comprehensive and fuller picture.
15. The application process is cyclical, beginning with collecting data from various trusted sources.
16. This gathered intelligence must then be analyzed for relevance and accuracy, and correlated with the organization's specific environment.
17. Action involves implementing measures like blocking malicious domains, updating firewall rules, or issuing immediate alerts.
18. The final step is a continuous cycle of reviewing and refining defenses, as the threat landscape evolves daily.
19. Mitigation strategies must form layers of defense, as no single measure is sufficient.
20. Practical defenses include enforcing Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) to block unauthorized accesses.
21. Other layers involve using endpoint protection and firewalls to reduce attack surfaces, performing regular patching, and conducting continuous network monitoring.
22. A case example, ransomware defense, requires a coordinated approach: blocking emails, applying the principle of least privilege, and ensuring off-site backups.
23. Furthermore, developing and testing an incident response plan and detecting lateral movement are crucial defensive components.
24. Ultimately, CTI is critical not just for data gathering, but for making sense of it, acting on it, and building smarter, layered defenses.
25. Mitigation is maximized when it is informed by CTI, built in layers, and continually refined to anticipate the unexpected.

GRC fundamentally represents the three areas of responsibility for all cybersecurity operations. Governance dictates how an organization is directed and controlled, encompassing leadership, roles, accountability, and established policies. Compliance involves meeting legal, regulatory, or contractual requirements, citing examples like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) or International Organization for Standardization (ISO) standards. Governance elevates cybersecurity from a mere technical issue to a fundamental business priority, defining who is responsible for decision-making and promoting ethical behavior. Risk management requires assessing the likelihood and potential damage of identified threats, necessitating mitigation, acceptance, or continuous monitoring of risks. Compliance protects organizations from fines, reputational harm, and lawsuits by adhering to standards like the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) or PCI DSS for financial institutions. When working together, Governance defines roles and policies, Risk Management identifies threats, and Compliance enforces required standards, building trust and resilience.

Intrusion detection systems are crucial for maintaining cybersecurity across various network infrastructures.

1.  **Definition and Necessity:** IDS are monitoring tools designed to detect malicious activity, acting as a critical layer of defense. The need for IDS stems from the inherent difficulty in securing complex networks against sophisticated, evolving cyber threats.

2.  **Theoretical Foundation (The Denning Model):** Historically, the methodology was pioneered by Denning, establishing the concept of monitoring system activity to identify deviations from normal operational baselines. This principle forms the core of modern anomaly detection.

3.  **Key Concepts of Detection:** Detection methods are categorized into signature-based detection (matching known attack patterns) and anomaly-based detection (identifying behaviors that deviate statistically from the norm).

4.  **Performance Metrics:** The effectiveness of an IDS is measured by false positive rates (flagging legitimate activity as malicious) and true positive rates (correctly identifying actual attacks).

5.  **The Challenge of Zero-Days:** A significant challenge is "zero-day" exploits—attacks exploiting vulnerabilities unknown to vendors—which necessitates robust anomaly detection capabilities.

6.  **System Components:** An IDS system typically comprises sensors (collecting network data), a central management system (analyzing the data), and an alerting mechanism (notifying administrators).

7.  **NIDS vs. HIDS:** Network Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS) monitor traffic across an entire network segment, while Host-based Intrusion Detection Systems (HIDS) monitor specific system resources, such as OS logs or file integrity.

8.  **Historical Evolution:** Early systems were reactive, requiring predefined rule sets. Modern systems are increasingly proactive, incorporating machine learning to adapt and predict emerging threats.

9.  **Machine Learning Integration:** The integration of Machine Learning (ML) is transformative, utilizing algorithms like Support Vector Machines (SVM) or deep neural networks to process vast streams of data and establish sophisticated baselines of "normal."

10. **Data Handling and Scalability:** Scalability is paramount; systems must process petabytes of data in real time, requiring optimized data streaming and analysis pipelines.

11. **The Operational Cycle:** An effective IDS follows a constant cycle: *Collect $\rightarrow$ Analyze $\rightarrow$ Alert $\rightarrow$ Respond $\rightarrow$ Learn*.

12. **Deployment Strategy:** Due to the high volume of data, effective deployment requires strategic placement of sensors to maximize coverage while minimizing data overload.

13. **Response Mechanisms (IPS):** While IDS only *detects*, its complementary role is the Intrusion Prevention System (IPS), which actively *blocks* detected threats by modifying network packets or terminating sessions.

14. **Modern Synthesis:** The current standard utilizes a hybrid approach, combining the precision of signature matching with the adaptability of ML-driven anomaly detection, creating a robust, multi-layered security posture.

### 08_Access_control_models_DAC,_MAC,_RBAC_and_ABAC_Video_._Duration_6_minutes_6_min

# Access control models: DAC, MAC, RBAC and ABAC
Video•
. Duration: 6 minutes
6 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2025-computer-security/lecture/cLfei/access-control-models-dac-mac-rbac-and-abac

Access control is a fundamental concept in cybersecurity, fundamentally deciding who is allowed access to what, when, and how. It refers to the methods used to determine authorized access to information or resources within a system. Its primary function is to prevent unauthorized access, protect sensitive data, and enforce organization security policies. Access control models provide various mechanisms for implementing these necessary security measures.

Discretionary Access Control (DAC) is a model where the resource owner determines access permissions. For example, the owner of a newly created file can dictate who has the ability to open, edit, or delete it. This model is noted for its high flexibility, making it common in personal computers or file-sharing platforms. However, DAC can pose risks because users might share access too freely.

Mandatory Access Control (MAC) operates under a much stricter, system-defined framework. In MAC, the system itself defines access based on security labels or classifications, such as 'confidential' or 'top secret.' Users cannot change these permissions, even if they are the data owner. This model provides very high security and is utilized in high-security government or military networks. Despite its security, MAC is criticized for being inflexible.

Rule-Based Access Control (RBAC) assigns access rights primarily based on an individual's job role within an organization. A manager might access performance reviews, while a finance user accesses budgets, for instance. RBAC is widely used in enterprise systems because it is easily scalable and manageable as an organization grows.

Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC) represents the most flexible and powerful model available. Here, access decisions are based on multiple attributes, moving beyond just the user role. These attributes can include time of day, physical location, device type, or the data's sensitivity. For example, access to sensitive data might only be allowed during business hours and from a company device. While ABAC is potent, it requires a complex initial setup.

Comparing the models highlights distinct trade-offs: DAC gives control to the user and is best for small teams, but has low security. MAC gives control to the system, offering high security and being ideal for government use, though it lacks flexibility. RBAC uses job roles, making it excellent for enterprises needing medium security and flexibility. Conversely, ABAC uses multiple flexible attributes, providing high security and flexibility, making it suitable for cloud platforms and large dynamic environments.

Effective access control is crucial not only for managing logins but also for minimizing insider threats. It is essential for meeting legal and regulatory requirements, such as GDPR or HIPAA. Good access control ensures that only the right people possess the right level of access at the right time. The selection of the appropriate model depends critically on the organization's specific needs and risk tolerance. Ultimately, access control is an indispensable component of robust cybersecurity posture.

Key management is defined as the comprehensive process of handling cryptographic keys throughout their entire lifecycle. Symmetric cryptography is a specific encryption method where a single, shared key is utilized for both encryption and decryption. ### 03_Asymmetric_cryptography_Video_._Duration_6_minutes_6_min

# Asymmetric cryptography
Video•
. Duration: 6 minutes
6 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2025-computer-security/lecture/j3VYS/asymmetric-cryptography

Asymmetric cryptography is a method of encryption that utilizes two distinct, yet related, keys. One key is the public key, which can be openly shared with anyone. The other key is the private key, which must remain strictly secret and known only to the owner. This system allows someone to share their public key, enabling others to send a secure message that only they can read using their private key. The two keys work together to protect data and secure communications. Unlike symmetric encryption, which uses a single, shared key for both encryption and decryption, asymmetric methods use different keys for these processes. This difference removes the critical need for the sender and receiver to share a secret key in advance. Furthermore, this method allows for heightened security and identity verification over open networks like the internet. A public key is designated for encrypting data or verifying signatures, while the private key is used for decrypting data or creating signatures. A key pair comprises these two working keys, and encryption is the process used to protect the information. To ensure privacy, Alice encrypts a message using Bob's public key, meaning only Bob's corresponding private key can successfully decrypt it. This unidirectional process ensures that even Alice cannot read the message again. Digital signatures prove both the message's authenticity and its message integrity, using the sender's private key. Anyone can then use the sender's public key to verify this signature, confirming the message originated from a trusted source and has not been altered. If the message is tampered with or the signature does not match, the system immediately detects the error. Asymmetric cryptography plays a crucial role in secure web browsing via HTTPS and facilitates secure email systems, such as those using Pretty Good Privacy (PGP). It is also fundamental in blockchain networks, cryptocurrency transactions, and for protecting data exchanged between cloud services. However, a significant technical limitation is that asymmetric encryption is generally slower than symmetric methods and requires more processing power. Therefore, in practical systems, the two methods are often combined in hybrid systems to balance speed and robust security. Key algorithms central to asymmetric cryptography include Reverse Shamir-Atleman (RSA), widely utilized for both encryption and signatures. Another notable algorithm is Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC), which provides comparable security using smaller key sizes. Additionally, Diffie-Hellman is employed specifically for the secure exchange of cryptographic keys. These algorithms adhere to internet standards and are used across various applications and devices. In summary, asymmetric cryptography leverages key pairs to secure data and establish identity without needing pre-shared secrets. Its common applications include web security, digital signing, and encrypted email. This mechanism provides strong protection and enables identity verification across open networks.

### 03_Side_channel_attacks_Video_._Duration_6_minutes_6_min

# Side channel attacks
Video•
. Duration: 6 minutes
6 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2025-computer-security/lecture/IfXoh/side-channel-attacks

Side-channel attacks (SCAs) represent a sophisticated class of threat that bypasses traditional software vulnerabilities. Instead, SCAs exploit indirect physical or behavioral signals leaked by a device during computation. These signals include measurable power consumption, electromagnetic radiation, timing variations, and even sound emissions. Strong cryptographic systems are not immune; they can be compromised if these physical side-channels are not properly secured.

Attackers utilize these leaks by measuring physical outputs during sensitive operations, such as decryption or key generation. They then apply statistical analysis and modeling techniques to deduce private keys or sensitive data. Several distinct methods characterize these attacks. Timing analysis measures the variable processing time required for an operation to complete. For example, if password validation halts upon the first incorrect character, an attacker can exploit this timing variability to deduce characters sequentially.

Power analysis focuses on monitoring changes in the electrical energy consumed by a device during computation. Simple Power Analysis (SPA) seeks distinctive power patterns that reveal specific instructions or data flows. A more advanced technique is Differential Power Analysis (DPA), which statistically compares numerous power traces to successfully recover secret keys.

Electromagnetic (EMI) attacks utilize probes or antennas to capture faint signals radiated by processor or memory components. These captured emissions can contain minute traces of the data being processed, even allowing attacks to traverse physical barriers like walls. Acoustic analysis records ambient sounds, such as keystrokes or CPU fan whines, to extract information, as demonstrated by extracting RSA keys using a nearby smartphone microphone.

These attacks pose a critical threat to embedded devices and smartcards, which are highly vulnerable if protections are absent. Case studies confirm that even secure environments can be compromised, such as measured power trace analysis successfully extracting secret keys from smartcards in minutes.

Defending against these side-channel threats requires a multi-layered defense strategy combining software and hardware countermeasures. Software solutions necessitate using algorithms with constant-time complexity, ensuring the runtime remains invariant regardless of the input data. Hardware defenses include physical shielding to mitigate electromagnetic emissions and the use of secure enclaves or coprocessors to isolate critical computations.

Countermeasures also involve introducing noise into power usage or timing to complicate statistical analysis. Effective defense requires good physical security and continuous monitoring to limit exposure to sophisticated physical probing. In summary, SCAs exploit the physical reality of computation—power, time, sound, and radiation—to undermine cryptographic security. Protecting against them mandates strict adherence to constant-time programming and robust hardware and software hardening.

Blockchain fundamentally enhances security by eliminating the dependency on a central authority, thereby building trust across various sectors. # Cyber kill chain and MITRE ATT&CK
Video•
. Duration: 4 minutes
4 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2025-computer-security/lecture/JHsi2/cyber-kill-chain-and-mitre-att-ck

The session introduces two foundational threat modeling tools essential for cybersecurity analysis: the Cyber Kill Chain and the MITRE Framework. These models help understand adversary operational methods and inform defensive strategies. The goal of studying these frameworks is to enhance detection and incident response capabilities.

The Cyber Kill Chain (CKC), established by Lockheed Martin, outlines seven sequential stages that adversaries follow during a cyber attack. These stages are: Reconnaissance, Weaponization, Delivery, Exploitation, Installation, Command and Control (C2), and Action on Objectives. The core defense strategy based on the CKC is to detect and disrupt the attack at the earliest possible stage. Recognizing these stages allows defense teams to build layered and preventative security measures. For instance, email filters can tackle delivery and installation, while network monitoring addresses C2 channels. The CKC provides a useful linear narrative to visualize the overall attack flow.

In contrast, the MITRE ATT&CK Framework organizes attacker actions into a dynamic matrix structure. Its categorization system progresses hierarchically, moving from Tactics to Techniques, and finally to Subtechniques. While the CKC offers a macro-level view, MITRE offers granular, practical details about attack methodologies. Combining both models dramatically enhances incident response analysis. For example, in analyzing a phishing attack, the CKC identifies delivery and execution, while MITRE precisely pinpoints the Initial Access (spear phishing) and the persistence mechanism (using a registry key).

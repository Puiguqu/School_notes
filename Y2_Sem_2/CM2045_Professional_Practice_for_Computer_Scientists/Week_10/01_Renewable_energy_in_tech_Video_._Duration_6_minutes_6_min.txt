# Renewable energy in tech

The tech industry, a major consumer of energy, requires massive amounts of electricity for data centers, cloud services, and digital infrastructure. According to Bloomberg New Energy Finance, the global tech industry consumes over 200 terawatt hours (TWh) of electricity annually, equating to the consumption of a mid-sized country like Sweden. As digital services and the demand for AI and cloud computing escalate, the environmental impact of tech companies’ energy footprint has become a significant issue. Tech companies are motivated to transition to renewables not only by reducing environmental impact but also because sustainability is recognized as beneficial for business. Furthermore, the economic parity of wind and solar power with fossil fuels, combined with climate change pressure, drives these ambitious sustainability goals.

Google was a pioneer, achieving 100% renewable energy for its global operations, including data centers and offices, starting in 2017. Building on this, Google announced a goal to become carbon-free by 2030, meaning all data centers must run on clean energy every hour of every day. Microsoft made a commitment to become carbon-negative by 2030, requiring them to remove more carbon than they emit. By 2050, Microsoft aims to remove all carbon emitted by the company, directly or indirectly, since its founding in 1975.

Microsoft achieves this through heavy investments in renewable energy and signing Multiple Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) with Solar and Wind Farms. Amazon committed to reaching 100% renewable energy across all operations by 2025 under its Climate Pledge. Amazon’s initiatives include building large-scale solar and wind farms, such as the Virginia Solar Farm, which generates 80 megawatts of clean energy. Additionally, Amazon launched the Clean Energy Accelerator for AWS to help startups develop innovative renewable solutions. Jeff Bezos also pledged $10 billion to fund the Bezos Earth Fund for climate change efforts.

A common strategy among these tech giants is the PPA, a long-term contract allowing companies to purchase renewable energy at a fixed price, guaranteeing a market for clean energy providers. Examples include Facebook signing a PPA with a 300-megawatt wind farm in Texas and Apple investing in solar projects generating over 1 gigawatt globally. Renewable energy transforms data centers by switching away from carbon sources for both computing power and cooling.

To optimize usage, companies utilize AI-driven algorithms to adjust cooling systems dynamically based on real-time data. This operational advancement allows companies like Google and Microsoft to potentially reduce energy consumption by as much as 40%. Future innovations, such as liquid cooling and submerged data centers like Microsoft's Technatic, promise further reductions in energy demand. Challenges persist, as renewable energy sources are often intermittent, necessitating robust storage solutions like advanced battery storage and grid balancing. Despite these hurdles, the path forward shows a clear trend toward a green and sustainable tech industry. The continuous investment in solar, wind, and efficient technologies by companies like Google, Microsoft, and Amazon is fundamentally reshaping the global technology sector.

# Transdisciplinary approach to sustainability

Technology presents a dual reality for sustainability, offering both immense challenges and significant opportunities. Rapid advancements can lead to increased resource consumption, resulting in environmental degradation and electronic waste. For example, the full life cycle of electronic devices contributes substantially to pollution and resource depletion. Conversely, technology provides innovative solutions, such as renewable energy technologies like solar and wind power. These advancements reduce dependence on fossil fuels, thereby lowering overall carbon emissions. Furthermore, improvements in smart grids, energy-efficient systems, and sustainable agriculture enhance resource management. Balancing these contrasting challenges and opportunities is crucial for successfully achieving a sustainable future.

A prominent modern challenge is posed by Artificial Intelligence (AI), which consumes vast amounts of energy. Training and running AI models require high energy consumption, leading to substantial carbon emissions. Data centres, which house AI servers, are major contributors to this massive electricity demand. Additionally, the infrastructure for AI generates e-waste and requires critical minerals, often obtained through unsustainable mining practices. Water usage in cooling systems for data centres also strains local water resources, presenting a geographical challenge. Therefore, balancing AI’s benefits with these environmental impacts is essential for sustainable development.

Addressing complex environmental dilemmas necessitates diverse perspectives and interdisciplinary knowledge. The approach of bringing together professionals from various technical and non-technical disciplines is termed transdisciplinary working. Transdisciplinary human teams are highly effective methods for leveraging new technologies to execute organizational strategies. Such teams deliver creative, enhanced solutions and are less susceptible to groupthink or blind spots. Crucially, they are better positioned to identify the impact on a full range of stakeholders and users of any proposed solution.

Employers in the tech sector increasingly value transdisciplinary working skills in recruits and future leaders. Individuals must demonstrate the ability to actively listen and recognize the professional or cultural lenses and biases present in a situation. Effective work with colleagues from diverse perspectives is key to tackling workplace dilemmas. Practical application of these skills can be gained through resources like the University of London Careers Service webinars. A sample webinar, "Global Dilemmas: Environmental Impact," illustrates how to apply a transdisciplinary approach. When viewing this content, one should consider multiple lenses: the academic lens (specialism interest), the lived experience lens (global location and prior experience), and the career stage lens (starter, developer, or changer). These combined perspectives fundamentally affect how sustainability dilemmas are viewed professionally. Successfully navigating this requires holistic thinking to ensure environmental goals are met while managing technological costs and social impacts.

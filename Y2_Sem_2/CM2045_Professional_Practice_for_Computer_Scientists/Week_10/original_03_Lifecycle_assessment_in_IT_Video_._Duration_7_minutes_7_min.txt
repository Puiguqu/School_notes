A topic inspired by the Environmental Protection Agency, the EPA's, analysis on how electronics impact the environment at every stage of their life cycle. Electronics are an essential part of modern life, but they come with a significant environmental footprint. From the extraction of raw materials to manufacturing, usage and disposal, each stage has environmental costs. We have policies here at Goldsmiths around disposal of electronic equipment, but this varies from institution to institution. Today we'll walk through the key stages of an electronic product's life cycle, look at some data and quotes from industry and environmental experts, and discuss strategies to reduce the overall environmental impact. Ready? Let's get started. Let's start with the basics. What is a Life Cycle Assessment, or an LCA? An LCA is a method used to evaluate the environmental impacts of a product, from its creation right the way through to its disposal. It considers the full lifespan of a product, everything from raw material extraction to manufacturing, distribution, use and finally end-of-life disposal. As the Environmental Protection Agency, EPA, states, LCAs provide a way to measure and reduce the ecological damage associated with electronics, which are responsible for toxic e-waste, resource depletion and greenhouse gas emissions. The LCA breaks down into four main stages. The raw material extraction, manufacturing and assembly, use phases and end-of-life disposal. Each stage has a significant environmental impact and by understanding these stages, we can identify ways to reduce the carbon footprint and other environmental harm associated with electronics. The first stage is raw material extraction. The production of electronics begins with the mining of metals and minerals like copper, gold, lithium and rare earth elements, materials crucial for the creation of circuit boards, batteries and other components. However, this extraction is not without its costs. Mining operations consume large amounts of energy and water and result in significant environmental degradation. In fact, according to the EPA, the extraction process for electronics is responsible for 40% of the total environmental impact of a typical device. An example, the mining of cobalt for lithium ion batteries, commonly used in smartphones and laptops, has been linked to both child labour and significant environmental harm in countries like the Democratic Republic of Congo. This does not even include other impacts such as disrupting local economies and wildlife, which are equally valuable but peripheral considerations. The second stage is manufacturing and assembly. This is where raw materials are transformed into the electronics we use every day – smartphones, laptops and tablets. The manufacturing process is energy intensive, contributing heavily to greenhouse gas emissions. For instance, the production of a single smartphone creates around 85kg of CO2, according to the Green Electronics Council. This stage also involves the use of toxic chemicals in the manufacturing of chips, displays and batteries, which can have harmful effects on both the environment and human health if not managed properly. we enter the use phase, which accounts for the energy consumption while electronics are in operation. While some devices like laptops and smartphones use relatively little energy individually, the cumulative impact of billions of devices in use around the world is massive. Data centres, which power our cloud services and streaming platforms, are another major energy consumer during this phase. According to the International Energy Agency, data centres globally account for nearly 1% of the world's electricity consumption, which as you can imagine contributes significantly to carbon emissions. As more services go online and cloud computing grows, this impact is expected to increase. Finally, we reach the end-of-life stage. This is where electronic devices are discarded, often as e-waste. The world generates around 53.6 million metric tonnes of e-waste annually, and only about 17% of that is properly recycled, according to the Global E-Waste Monitor. E-waste can contain hazardous materials like lead, mercury and cadmium, which pose risks to both the environment and human health if they end up in landfills. For example, improper disposal of electronics can lead to toxic chemicals seeping into soil and water, polluting ecosystems. One solution, the circular economy approach which encourages the recycling and reuse of electronic components to reduce the environmental footprint of e-waste. One company making strides in reducing the environmental impact of its electronics is Apple. Apple has committed to using 100% recycled aluminium in its products, and through their Daisy robot, they are able to disassemble old iPhones and recover valuable materials like cobalt, aluminium and gold. In 2020, Apple's recycling efforts diverted over 48,000 metric tonnes of electronic waste from landfills. By focusing on closed-loop recycling, Apple is setting an example for other tech companies on how to reduce the environmental impact at the end of a product's lifecycle. So what can we do to make electronics more sustainable? Here are some strategies. Design for longevity. Companies should create devices that last longer and are easier to repair, reducing the need for constant replacements. Energy efficiency. Both manufacturers and consumers should prioritise energy-efficient devices to reduce the environmental impact during the use phase. Recycling and e-waste management. Improving e-waste recycling programmes and encouraging consumers to recycle their old devices will help recover valuable materials and reduce the impact of disposal. Sustainable materials. Manufacturers can explore alternatives to rare earth metals or invest in technologies that reduce the need for raw material extraction. In conclusion, lifecycle assessments are a critical tool for understanding the environmental impacts of electronics and identifying ways to reduce them. From the extraction of raw materials to the disposal of e-waste, every stage has a significant ecological cost, but by implementing sustainable practices we can reduce the footprint of our devices. As the EPA points out, by using LCAs to guide our choices, both companies and consumers can work together to build a more sustainable future.

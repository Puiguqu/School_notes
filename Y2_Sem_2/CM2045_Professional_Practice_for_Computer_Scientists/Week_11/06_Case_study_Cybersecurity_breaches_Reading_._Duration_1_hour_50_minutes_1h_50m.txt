The provided material outlines a study focused on major cybersecurity breaches and the profound challenges facing large organizations. These companies struggle to balance providing accessible services with the critical task of securing massive amounts of digital content. A system exploit, when it occurs, can trigger various adverse consequences. These range from the unauthorized exposure of unsecured data to the denial of access for users seeking important products and services. The analysis explicitly defines its scope, clarifying that it is not a course on computer security methods. Therefore, the primary technical focus remains on understanding two critical dimensions: the sheer scale of the attacks and their associated real-world impact. The academic objective requires researchers to select one specific, prominent breach for detailed investigation. This investigation must be complemented by engaging with the theoretical framework presented by Schneier. The required reading is B. Schneier's work, specifically *Data and Goliath: The hidden battles to collect your data and control your world*. This book was published in 2016 in New York by W.W. Norton & Company. The analysis encourages reflection on the broad implications of these devastating breaches.

The source identifies three specific historical instances of highly publicized data breaches. The first is the Equifax data breach, which occurred in 2017. This major vulnerability exposed the highly sensitive personal information of 147 million people. The root cause was identified as a vulnerability within the company’s web application software. Secondly, the Yahoo! data breach transpired between 2013 and 2014. This incident was particularly vast, affecting all 3 billion Yahoo! user accounts. Its failure was attributed to a complex mix of poor inherent security practices and profoundly inadequate incident response protocols. Thirdly, the Sony PlayStation network outage occurred in 2011. This event resulted in a lengthy loss of service access for its users. Furthermore, this service was one that many individuals had purchased paid subscription fees for. These cases collectively illustrate the systemic risks inherent in managing vast digital infrastructures. By studying these events alongside Schneier’s concepts, one can better understand data control. The analysis thus shifts from mere technical failure to societal and systemic risk management.

So what can we use to help us with this? Policy. Who gets access to what, when and how? That's what a good policy tells us. It's the rulebook, the code of conduct, a map to help us through cybersecurity challenges. But let's not forget the human factor. We can have all the fancy tech in the world. Mobile device management to manage those mobile devices, endpoint detection and response to detect those sneaky attacks, multi-factor authentication to keep hackers out, but if someone clicks on a phishing email, well, we still have a problem. Humans are often the weakest link. We need to train them, educate them and make them security aware. Speaking of threats, let's look at the usual suspects, shall we? Viruses, malware, ransomware, they're all out there waiting for an opportunity. And then there's the insider threat, the unhappy employee, the accidental data leak. We need to be vigilant, proactive and always one step ahead. Whether intentional or accidental, both types of threats pose interesting challenges for us as security professionals. It's a digital race out there. The good guys versus the cyber criminals, a constant back and forth. We've got encryption to scramble our data, penetration testing to probe for weaknesses and the principle of least privilege to keep access restricted. Speaking of least privilege, let's have a quick look at those Unix file system permissions. You know, the chmod plus 755 business. Remember, 755 means the owner gets full access, read, write and execute, while everyone else gets read and execute access only. No messing about with files they shouldn't be touching. It's all about giving just enough access to do the job and no more. Need to edit a config file? Fine. Have write access. Just need to run a script? Read and execute access will do. Bob's your uncle. Or at least for the sake of those security analogies, we rely on to teach these concepts. And don't forget about separation of duties. Don't want one person having the keys to the kingdom, do we? Think of them as our guides to building a secure digital framework fortress. ISO 27001, NIST, they're all there to help us assess the risk, implement controls and comply with those ever so important industry standards.

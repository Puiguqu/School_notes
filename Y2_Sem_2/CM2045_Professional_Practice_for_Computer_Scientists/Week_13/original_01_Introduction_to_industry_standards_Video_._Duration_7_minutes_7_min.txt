Now I know what you might be thinking, standards, sounds a bit dry doesn't it? But trust me, standards are absolutely crucial to the technology we use every day. They're the unsung heroes, working tirelessly behind the scenes to ensure interoperability, safety and efficiency. So what are standards? A standard is a documented agreement containing technical specifications or other precise criteria to be used consistently as rules, guidelines or definitions of characteristics to ensure that the materials, products, processes and services are fit for purpose. For example, think of a simple light bulb. The standard for the bulb's fitting ensures it will work in any appropriately designed lamp. That's the power of standards. Some of the key standards organisations include the IEEE, the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers, focusing on electrical, electronics and computing standards. Think Wi-Fi 802.11 and Ethernet 802.3. ISO or ISO, International Organisation for Standardisation. They've developed standards for a wide range of industries including manufacturing, healthcare and technology. For example, ISO 9001 for quality management. The W3C or the World Wide Web Consortium set standards for the World Wide Web such as HTML, CSS and XML. In the context of standards, an RFC or Request for s is a formal document created and used by the Internet Engineering Task Force or the IETF to propose and define standards for the internet. Think of it as a detailed technical document that outlines how a particular technology should work. They follow an open and collaborative consultation process. That's where the comments bit comes from. Some examples of format wars and standards in action are VHS versus Betamax, a classic example of a standards battle. VHS ultimately won despite arguably being technically inferior, demonstrating the importance of market forces and consumer adoption in standards adoption. CDs versus DVDs, another format war, this time with DVDs emerging victorious due to their higher storage capacity. These battles highlight the role of innovation and backward compatibility in setting standards. The Blu-ray soon came along and overtook both due to higher fidelity capacity with increased storage space. Much like the floppy disk, technology is constantly developing and so standards often shift. Some standards in computing include TCP over IP, or what we know as Transmission Control Protocol over Internet Protocol, the fundamental communication protocol of the internet. Without a standardized protocol, the internet as we know it simply wouldn't exist. HTML, Hypertext Markup Language, the standard markup language for creating web pages. Imagine if every website used a different language. Browsing the web would be chaos. IBM PC, IBM's decision to use off-the-shelf components and published specifications led to the widespread adoption of the IBM PC architecture, paving the way for the modern PC industry. Some common core computer science standards. Data encoding, ASCII, Unicode, ensuring consistent representation of characters and symbols across different systems. Programming languages, C++, Java, Python. They define syntax and semantics and enable programmers to write code that can be understood by others and executed on different machines. Data structures, arrays, linked lists, trees, provide standardized ways to organize and store data efficiently. Algorithms, sorting, searching, encryption, well-defined procedures for solving common computational problems. Standards add value in a number of ways, including interoperability, allowing different systems and devices to work together seamlessly. Safety, ensuring products and services meet minimum safety requirements. Efficiency, promoting efficient use of resources and reducing waste. Innovation, providing a common foundation for innovation and development. Consumer protection, guaranteeing a certain level of quality and performance. When standards go wrong, it can result in the following. Incompatibility, imagine if your phone charger only worked with your specific phone model. That's what a lack of standards can lead to. Security risks, poorly designed security standards can leave systems vulnerable to attack. Remember the Heartbleed bug in OpenSSL? Stifled innovation, overly restrictive standards can sometimes hinder innovation by limiting experimentation. The terms de facto and du jour are used to describe two different ways in which something can be considered a standard. They are contrasting legal concepts that distinguish between what is true in practice versus what is true according to the law. De facto means in fact or in practice. So how does this apply to standards? A de facto standard is one that has become widely adopted and used in practice, even though it may not have been formally approved by any standards organization. It emerges organically through market forces and user preferences. Examples of this are the QWERTY keyboard layout. There's no law saying we have to use QWERTY, but it's the dominant keyboard layout because it's what everybody uses. Microsoft Windows. For many years, Windows has been the de facto standard for desktop-operated systems due to its widespread use. De jour, meaning by law or according to law. How it applies to standards. A de jour standard is one that has been officially recognized and approved by a standards organization or government body. It's typically established through a formal process involving experts and stakeholders. For example, GSM, Global System for Mobile Communication, a de jour standard for mobile communication. IEEE 802.11, or as we know it, the Wi-Fi standard, a de jour standard for wireless networking. ISO 27001, Information Security Management, a de jour standard for information security management systems. Standards might not be the most glamorous aspect of the tech industry, but they are absolutely essential. They underpin the technology we rely on, enabling communication, innovation, and progress.

It's not just coding, it's a whole journey from idea to finished product, and even beyond. Today we're going to look at IEEE 12.2.07, a standard that maps out this journey. The software lifecycle. Get ready for some process talk, but trust me, this will make a lot of sense. Why do we need a lifecycle standard? Software can be complex. Think of a huge system like air traffic control, or even a game with millions of lines of code. We need structure to manage this complexity. Different teams, different goals, programmers, designers, testers, project managers, they all need to work together. A standard provides a common language and framework. Consistency is key, whether it's a small application or a massive enterprise system, a defined lifecycle helps ensure quality and predictability. IEEE 12.2.07 and the big picture. It's not just code, this standard covers everything from gathering requirements to maintaining the software after it's released. It defines processes, not specifics. It doesn't tell you how to code, but it defines the processes involved in software development. An adaptable framework. It's not a one-size-fits-all solution. You can tailor it to different project types and sizes. The key processes in 12.2.07 are acquisition, figuring out what the software needs to do, getting the resources and making a plan. Supply, actually building the software, including design, coding and testing. Development, this is where the core coding happens, but also things like documentation and reviews. Operation, deploying the software, providing user support and keeping it running smoothly. Maintenance, fixing bugs, adding new features and adapting the software to changing needs. Breaking down the processes. Each process has activities. For example, requirements analysis is an activity within the acquisition process. Tasks within activities. Each activity is further broken down into specific tasks. And defined inputs and outputs. Each task has defined inputs, what you need to start, and outputs, what you need to produce. 12.2.07 emphasizes documentation. Why? Because it helps track progress, ensure consistency and transfer knowledge. There are a range of different document types such as requirements documents, design documents, test plans, user manuals and more. Traceability, the standard helps link different documents and activities so you can see how everything connects. Some of the benefits of using 12.2.07 are Improved communication, everyone's on the same page using the same terminology. Reduced risk, by following a structured approach you're less likely to miss important steps or make costly mistakes. Higher quality software, the standard promotes best practice and helps ensure the software meets its requirements without oversights or assumptions. Increased efficiency, a well-defined process can streamline development and reduce wasted effort. 12.2.07 in the real world is widely used. Many organizations, especially those developing critical systems use 12.2.07 as a guide. Certifications exist and companies can get certified to demonstrate their compliance with the standard. It's not just for large projects, even small teams can benefit from the principles of 12.2.07. Something beyond the standard, while 12.2.07 is more traditional it can be adapted to work with agile processes. We'll talk about these a little bit later in the course. It encourages continuous improvement and ongoing evaluation and improvement of the software lifecycle. It might seem like a lot of process talk, but it's really about making software development more efficient, predictable and successful. I like consistency, predictability and systematic processes. Hence the reason why standards are useful to me.

Hackers are constantly trying to break in, steal data, or just cause problems. This lecture isn't about scaring you, but empowering you. We'll explore how to implement security and best practices, guided by the IEEE and ISO standards, to make your tech truly secure. Where can we look for guidance on security standards and best practice? Let's find out. Thankfully, we don't have to invent security from scratch. Organizations like the IEEE and ISO provide frameworks and best practice. ISO, or the IEC, 27001, is a comprehensive standard for creating an information security management system. Think of this as a blueprint for your security. IEEE 802.11i is specifically for Wi-Fi security, but it exemplifies how standards define protocols like your WPA2 and WPA3 to protect wireless networks. NIST, or NIST, the National Institute of Standards and Technology, are a US-based institution, but their guidelines are globally influential, covering everything from cryptography to risk management. We've talked about them already and how influential they can be in this space for informing and guiding our practices. Good security has layers, making it harder for attackers to penetrate. Best practice looks at checking at multiple levels, client-side, SQL sanitization, server-side validation, etc. A good example of this is email validation. RFC 5322 will do this. Validation level. This is where your code lives. Secure coding practices prevent vulnerabilities like SQL injection attacks or cross-site scripting. Input validation is key. The Government Digital Services also provide some guidance on how to capture input to reduce accidental errors, for instance, when someone is filling in a form. Something as simple as splitting date of birth into three separate inputs, the day, the month and the year, can simplify the process. Enabling accessibility and making your system more resilient to errors. Network level. Firewalls, intrusion detection systems and secure network configurations keep the bad guys out of your network. Data level. Encryption protects data both in transit over HTTPS and at rest through things like encrypting databases. Access control ensures only authorized users see sensitive information. ISO 27001 is the foundation. It's not just tech. This standard emphasizes a holistic approach including policies, procedures and employee training. Risk assessment. Identify your assets, what needs to be protected and the threats against them. This guides your security measures. Control selection. ISO 27001 provides a catalogue of controls like access control, cryptography and physical security to choose from based upon your risks. Continuous improvement. Regularly review and update your security measures to adapt to new threats. Let's look at some real world examples of the importance of these standards. The Equifax breach in 2017. A failure to fix a known vulnerability led to the exposure of millions of records. This highlights the importance of vulnerability management and adhering to security standards. GDPR compliance. The EU's General Data Protection Regulation mandates strong data protection measures. Implementing ISO 27001 can help organizations comply. Secure online banking. Let's use multiple layers of security including strong authentication, often multi-factor, encryption and fraud detection to protect your money. These standards make up the basis of how our data is kept safe from intruders and accessible to us, the genuine users. Building secure applications isn't just about ticking boxes. It's about understanding the threats, applying best practice and continuously adapting. IEEE and ISO standards provide a roadmap but it's up to us to implement them effectively and build a secure digital world. The guidance is there, we just need to do our best to follow it. Much like following semantic guidelines when you're writing HTML pages, it benefits everybody to follow a uniform protocol. This is what standardization is all about.

# Managing team conflicts

Conflict in computer science teams is an inevitable professional challenge, ranging from basic technical debates (e.g., tabs versus spaces, naming conventions, or architectural choices) to complex disagreements like front-end versus back-end design feasibility. Furthermore, process friction can arise from methodologies like Agile, where contentious aspects such as story points create tension regarding time estimation. To navigate these tensions, the Thomas Killman Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) provides a comprehensive framework for understanding conflict dynamics. The TKI identifies five distinct conflict styles based on two axes: assertiveness and cooperativeness.

The first mode is **Competing**, characterized by high assertiveness and low cooperativeness, adopting the stance, "My solution is clearly superior." This approach is useful when a critical bug requires an urgent fix or facing a technical impasse. However, overreliance can alienate teammates, stifle alternative solutions, and create a toxic work environment. Next is **Collaborating**, which demands high assertiveness and high cooperativeness, prompting, "Let's whiteboard this and find the best approach together." This style is highly valued for designing complex systems or refactoring legacy code, emphasizing shared vision. A drawback is that collaboration can be intensely time-consuming and unsuited for quick decisions.

The third mode is **Compromising**, which exhibits moderate assertiveness and moderate cooperativeness, allowing the developer to negotiate, "Okay, we'll use your database solution, but I get to choose the API framework." Compromising is best used when integrating different modules or resolving time-constrained merge conflicts. Its potential drawback is that it may lead to suboptimal solutions or partial team dissatisfaction. **Avoiding** represents the 'code ostrich' style, featuring low assertiveness and low cooperativeness, such as, "I'll just work on my own tasks and hope this blows over." This strategy is useful when conflict is minor, personality-driven, or emotions are high. However, avoiding conflict can allow problems to fester and hinder valuable feedback exchange.

Finally, **Accommodating** involves low assertiveness and high cooperativeness, adopting the peacemaker stance, "Sure, I'll refactor my code to match your style." This style is appropriate for maintaining positive team dynamics or when the issue is deemed less important to the individual. The main risk of accommodating is that overusing the tactic can lead to resentment and prevent valuable perspectives from being considered. By mastering these five styles, teams can strategically transform disagreements into opportunities for growth and innovation.

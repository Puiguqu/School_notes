1. Kanban is a visual system designed for managing the work itself, offering a powerful framework for improving efficiency and collaboration in computer science projects.
2. It is a visual approach to workflow management, providing clear principles for optimizing processes.
3. The origins of Kanban trace back to manufacturing, specifically Toyota’s innovative production system developed in the 1940s.
4. This history connects directly to the principles of lean manufacturing.
5. Lean manufacturing focuses on optimizing a production line by prioritizing key steps and stages to deliver value.
6. A core concept derived from this history is "just-in-time" inventory, which minimizes waste and maximizes efficiency by replenishing items only when needed.
7. Kanban boards facilitate visualizing the flow of work through structured columns and cards.
8. A fundamental principle is to limit the work in progress (WIP), thereby focusing on completing existing tasks before starting new ones.
9. This limitation helps reduce multitasking and improves the overall flow of work.
10. Other principles include visualizing the entire workflow, ensuring all work items and their statuses are visible to the entire team.
11. Teams must also manage the flow by continuously monitoring the work system and identifying any potential blockages.
12. When a blockage occurs, the team learns lessons and must chunk tasks accordingly.
13. Priority should be given to tasks that are stuck or stopping the entire system from moving forward.
14. Process policies must be made explicit, meaning the rules and guidelines for how work moves through the system must be clearly defined.
15. Kanban boards use columns to represent distinct stages, such as To Do, In Progress, Testing, and Done.
16. Cards represent individual work items, holding details about a specific task, its assignee, and its current status.
17. Swim lanes can be utilized to categorize cards by project, team, or priority.
18. In computer science, Kanban is applied to track user stories, bug fixes, and general feature development.
19. It is also utilized by IT support to manage incident tickets and service requests effectively.
20. Kanban methods complement other Agile frameworks, such as Scrum, by providing a crucial visual layer for workflow management.
21. Benefits of Kanban include improved visibility, ensuring everyone knows the status of all tasks.
22. It also promotes reduced waste by minimizing multitasking and focusing efforts efficiently.
23. Furthermore, Kanban increases flow by helping teams identify and address systemic bottlenecks.
24. Implementing Kanban promotes enhanced collaboration and fosters a culture of continuous improvement through constant feedback.
25. Ultimately, Kanban is not merely a tool for placing notes on a board, but a practice for optimizing work flow and delivering value effectively.

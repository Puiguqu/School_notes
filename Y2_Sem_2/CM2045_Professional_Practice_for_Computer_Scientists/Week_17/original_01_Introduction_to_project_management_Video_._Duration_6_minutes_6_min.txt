This isn't just a buzzword, it's a philosophy that has revolutionized how we build software. Today, we'll explore the origins of Agile, why it became so popular and why even its creators are now questioning its implementation. Let's start with waterfall project management. This is the old way. Imagine building a house by designing every detail, then laying the foundation, then the walls with no changes allowed. That's waterfall project management. The original paper on waterfall actually warned against its use. It can be rigid, inefficient, and lack consideration for broader implications of things like user centered design principles. It is rigid and inflexible, great for predictable projects, but software is rarely predictable. Changing requirements means costly rework, and these days, the world is changing so fast, it's difficult to keep up. The idea that the scope and features of a project will remain the same for a decade or longer almost seems laughable. The need for change. In the late '90s, the software world was craving a more flexible, responsive approach. The Internet and the web were becoming popular, computational speed and power was increasing, it was a change that was necessary based on broader market conditions and the demand for more and better software solutions. In 2001, 17 software gurus gathered to discuss alternatives to waterfall. They weren't all in agreement, but they produced the Agile manifesto with four core values, individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, adaptability over following a plan. It's about principles, not rules. Agile is a mindset, not a rigid set of procedures, at least, that was the vision. Some examples of Agile in action are Scrum. This is a popular framework with sprints, daily stand-ups, and retrospectives to promote iterative development and continuous feedback. This essentially boils down to regular project updates, looking forwards in trying to predict risks, pitfalls, and challenges, and learning from mistakes as you go. Talk, listen, learn. A bit like what we do here. Kanban is an agile project management tool to help visualize your workflow, limit working progress, and optimize flow. It is essentially a series of activities moving from a list of desired features often referred to as a backlog to some working code. The really important part of this is to make sure things are moving, visual verification, and to prioritize the delivery of things that offer the highest utility proposition. If you use tools like Trello, try to limit the bandwidth of items. Working progress should be limited to the size and capabilities of your team, and only add things when you have space and time to work on them. Extreme Programming. Extreme Programming has an emphasis on technical practices like pair programming, test driven development, and continuous integration. Test driven development can be a tedious process, but what it does is it keeps you on task and on track. If you pair it with something like a Kanban, you can visually see features appear when your unit tests pass and track progress quite easily. It's similar to checkbox items where you take things off your list as you progress. Agile delivered results, faster releases, happier customers, and more adaptable teams. They call them sprints for a reason. The Agile backlash. Agile-fall. Many organizations adopted Agile in name only, sticking to rigid structures and missing the point. Calling it Agile and then doing waterfall simply does not produce results. Cargo cult Agile, blindly following ceremonies and rituals without understanding the underlying principles. There are a handful of companies that jump onto the newest trend, whether it's a tech stack or a set of tools. You have to think about whether or not it is a good fit for you, your problem, your company, your culture, and so forth. Even the Agile manifesto authors are critical of how it's often implemented, emphasizing the need to focus on the values, not just the practices. I understand how easy it is to get hung up on semantics and syntax, especially when they are integral to how we work. Sometimes you just have to take a step back and consider if you're too focused on the details to see the bigger picture at play. The future of Agile. Agile remains valuable, but it needs to be adapted and applied thoughtfully. My own view of Agile is that it maps many of the usability principles and user centered design aspects from early Chi research in the '90s. It's the right ideas, though sometimes I don't always agree with things like story points as a false metric. It's guessing, and the empirical evidence suggests that most developers aren't very good at predicting how long something will take even with years of experience. Focus on outcomes, not processes. Delivering value to the customer remains the ultimate goal. Again, I feel this maps extremely closely to user centered design. Embrace continuous improvement. Regularly reflect on your Agile practices and adjust them to fit your team and your project. Agile was a revolution, but it's not perfect. By understanding its origins, its strengths, and its pitfalls, we can use Agile principles effectively to build better software and create more collaborative, responsive teams.

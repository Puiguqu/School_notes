Scrum is an agile framework that's revolutionized how teams build software. This lecture is an introduction to Scrum. Scrum is iterative and incremental. It breaks down projects into short cycles called sprints, usually between two to four weeks. Self-organizing teams. It empowers teams to manage their own work and make decisions. Continuous feedback. It allows you to regularly inspect and adapt your process through sprint reviews and retrospectives. The Scrum framework includes the following roles. The product owner. Represents the customer, defines the product vision, and prioritizes the backlog – the features that we want to build. The Scrum master. Facilitates the process, removes impediments, and ensures the team adheres to Scrum principles. Think of them as process-focused rather than product-focused. The development team. The self-organizing group responsible for building the product. Agile is heavily focused around self-organizing teams – allowing people to work together with who they think are a best fit for the type of project and challenges being faced. I often recruit people outside of my realm of experience to make sure our skills complement each other as much as possible, reducing any gaps we might have in knowledge. Artifacts. The product backlog. A prioritized list of features and requirements. Usually, priorities emerge from the things that provide the most value to users, and the things that are easiest to build. If they're both of these things, then they tend to take precedence. The sprint backlog. The subset of product backlog that the team commits to completing in a sprint. Often these are described as features and a collection of features are described as a Minimal Viable Product, or an MVP. A demo of some working features together to show the look and feel. Increment. The working software produced at the end of each sprint. Version 0.0.0.1 perhaps. Events include. Sprint planning. The team plans the work for the upcoming sprint. As you work more, you get a sense of how long things tend to take. The daily scrum. A short daily meeting to synchronize and discuss progress. Stand-ups are usually a big part of this. A quick stand-up meeting with the idea of getting people speaking briefly and then moving towards their work environment to be productive. A sprint review. The team demonstrates the increment to stakeholders and gathers feedback. This is a must to ensure buy-in and verify and validate against expectations. Does it work? Does it work the way it's intended? Without doing this, you cannot be sure. Sprint retrospectives. The team reflects on the sprint and identifies areas for improvement. Critical evaluation is key to improving. Scrum works because of the following characteristics. Flexibility. It adapts to changing requirements and priorities. Collaboration. Encourages teamwork and communication. Transparency. Provides visibility into progress and challenges. And continuous improvement that fosters a culture of learning and adaptation. Here are some examples of scrum in action. Building a mobile app. A team uses scrum to iteratively develop and release new features based on user feedback. Developing a website. A design team uses scrum to create and refine user interfaces in collaboration with developers. Even non-software projects, scrum can be applied to any project that requires flexibility and collaboration, from marketing campaigns to research projects. Scrum is more than just a set of procedures. It's a mindset that embraces change, collaboration and continuous improvement.

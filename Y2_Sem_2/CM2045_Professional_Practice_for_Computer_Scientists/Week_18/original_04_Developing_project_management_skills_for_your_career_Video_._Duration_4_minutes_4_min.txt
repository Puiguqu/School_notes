In this lecture, we're going to explore how you can use our University of London Careers micro-modules to develop your project management skills as you study. The University of London Career Service is here to help. We offer you the opportunity to develop your skills and career insights while you study with us by completing our new careers and employability micro-modules available on the University of London virtual learning environment. At the time of recording, we are offering three micro-modules focusing on global employability skills, professional impact profile and success, and career planning. These micro-modules were developed by professional experts at the University of London Career Service. On the completion of each micro-module, you are awarded a certificate of completion, which will be recognised on your degree transcript. Professional impact profile and success is the micro-module with the most content on project management skills. You are free to browse this standalone content at your convenience, even if you don't want to complete the whole micro-module. The professional impact profile and success micro-module is organised into four strands, the persuasive professional, the strategic professional, the entrepreneurial professional, and the market-aware professional. In the entrepreneurial professional strand, there is a dedicated section on project management skills, which focuses on the three phases of the project lifecycle and how you can develop your project management skills. As an illustration, one section of this topic highlights how you can develop your project management skills outside work. At home, many major domestic tasks such as childcare, home improvements or planting a garden benefit from good project management skills and provide the opportunity to practice your planning and problem-solving skills. In your community, volunteering on community projects can broaden your experience and give you the opportunity to learn from others' approach to project management in an atmosphere less competitive than professional work. In your studies, how can you use a project management approach to improve the effectiveness of your study habits? And how can what you are learning through your study improve your project management at work? This topic also provides worked examples of how you can demonstrate your project management skills in your CV, application or interview answers. For example, my experience as a University of London distance learning student has strengthened my ability to manage my time. So to balance the demands of my academic studies and my work deadlines, I prioritise very carefully. I'm also aware of the need to be flexible and realistic. For example, I liaised with my line manager at work to ensure I could apply my studies for a specific module to a relevant work task to maximise my learning. This careful planning meant I met both my academic and professional deadlines. As I mentioned previously, other content in the professional impact profile and success micro-module is also relevant to developing your project skills. In the persuasive professional strand, we provide advice on negotiating effectively and in the strategic professional strand, we explore time management. Our University of London Career Service micro-modules provide many resources and insights to help you enhance your project management skills and develop your career.

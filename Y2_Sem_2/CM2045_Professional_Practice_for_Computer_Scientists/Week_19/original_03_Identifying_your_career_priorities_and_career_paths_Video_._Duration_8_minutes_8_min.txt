Your BSc Computer Science degree equips you with both technical skills and global employability skills, preparing you for a range of career pathways in technology. This lecture explores how to identify your career priorities and choose from numerous specialisms like data science, machine learning, web development and more, each offering unique roles and growth opportunities within the dynamic tech industry. Clarifying your career priorities is essential throughout your career development, whether you are a career starter at the beginning of your career, a career developer looking for specialisation or promotion, or a career changer transitioning from another field. For entry-level job seekers, understanding your strengths can help you align with a role such as a front-end developer or data analyst. Career developers benefit by targeting roles that deepen expertise or demonstrate strategic thinking. Career changers can focus on fields that utilise their transferable global employability skills and sector insights, such as moving from education into UX design in edtech. Knowing your personal career priorities at every stage ensures a more targeted job search and smoother career progression. Employers and recruiters value candidates who demonstrate clear, well-defined career goals and motivations, as it shows commitment and a long-term perspective. For instance, a recruiter will favour a candidate who has focused their skills and experiences towards a specific role, such as an IoT engineer, over someone with a broad but unfocused approach. When your CV shows alignment with a role, such as game development or user experience, employers see a candidate prepared to contribute immediately and likely to make a quick impact within the position. The key concept in identifying careers' priorities is aligning your unique combination of skills, values and interests with specific roles in the tech sector. For example, if you are drawn to data-driven decision-making, consider a specialisation like data science, which offer roles such as data engineers or data analysts. Alternatively, those interested in creative problem-solving might excel in game development or virtual reality. Each tech path leverages a different combination of technical skills, such as programming, data handling and UX design, and requires complementary global employability skills like complex problem-solving and collaboration. Using these elements helps guide you towards a role that fits your strengths and aspirations. When clarifying your personal career priorities, ask questions focused around what you want out of work and life. What areas of technology excite me most? Do I prefer working with data, creating user experiences or developing software? What types of problems do I want to solve? Am I motivated by creative freedom or do I prefer structured problem-solving? How much money do I want to earn? How flexible do I want my work to be in terms of location and working hours? Answering these questions helps you make your career satisfying by aligning your career with personal interests, whether that is developing immersive virtual worlds, building intelligent algorithms or designing user-friendly applications. They also help you make your career sustainable by empowering you to find work that integrates well with what you want out of your wider life. Explore key tech specialisations. Once you have got a sense of what is important to you, it is time to research the careers opportunities open to you by studying the BSc Computer Science. Here is a quick overview. More detail is available in our University of London Career Service VLE resources. Machine Learning and AI focuses on developing algorithms to automate data analysis, commonly used in industries like finance, healthcare and entertainment. Typical roles include machine learning engineer and AI specialist. Data science involves collecting and analysing data to help organisations make informed decisions. Jobs in this field include data scientist, data engineer and business data analyst. Web and mobile development encompasses the creation of websites and applications. Roles include front-end developer, back-end developer and mobile app developer. Physical Computing and Internet of Things, IoT, focuses on connecting physical devices to the internet, useful in sectors like manufacturing and smart home technology. Roles include IoT engineer and embedded software developer. Game development involves creating interactive video games with roles such as game designer and game programmer. Virtual Reality offers immersive tech solutions across industries with job titles like VR developer and environment artist. User Experience focuses on human-computer interaction with roles such as UX designers and UX developers. To determine if a tech specialty would suit you well, consider what does a typical day look like in this field? What sort of problems would I be solving? How much contact with people would I have on a daily basis? What core skills are needed and do I have them? What industries or sectors rely on this specialisation? What challenges do professionals face in this area? What is needed for the entry-level jobs in this specialty? How can I grow and progress within this specialty? AI can be a powerful tool as you explore tech roles and match what they offer against your personal career priorities. For example, you can use AI-driven platforms to identify high-demand skills in a particular specialty such as machine learning or analyse industry trends. However, it is important to approach AI critically. Use it as a supplement, not a replacement, for your own personal reflection and research. For example, while AI may suggest jobs based on your resume, you should still take the time to talk with people working in the field to ensure the role aligns with your interests and personality.

# Design a heuristics paper
Reading•
. Duration: 1 hour
1h - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-cm2045-professional-practice-for-computer-scientists/supplement/kCB2L/design-a-heuristics-paper

The discussion introduces the concept of heuristics as applied to specialized systems, such as Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs). For deeper study, Sean McGrath's paper, "Breaking the workflow: Design heuristics to support the development of usable digital audio production tools: framing usability heuristics for contemporary purposes," is recommended. This academic resource is cited from the AM '20: Proceedings of the 15th International Audio Mostly Conference, specifically pages pp.130-137. The text encourages the reader to search for this paper in the Online Library or use a simple Google search for DAWs to understand the system's scope. Initially, students are prompted to devise three practical heuristics for DAWs, considering aspects like Discoverability, Learnability, and Accessibility. This theoretical understanding then transitions into a practical application at the University of London. The main objective is to enhance learning materials by creating visual reminders and comprehensive guidelines for effective learning. This process allows students to gain valuable experience in instructional design and improve the peer learning experience. Task 1 outlines the process, beginning with choosing a specific focus area for instruction. Students must select a known concept, module, or learning objective to "teach" to other students. Alternatively, a focus can be placed on a learning strategy, such as the University of London's CAFE model, or general presentation principles. Step 2 requires developing a set of clear and concise heuristics based on the chosen focus. These heuristics must guide effective learning or instructional design principles. Ideally, they should be applicable both by educators designing and evaluating their learning technologies. These heuristics must offer practical advice and include visual reminders to support comprehension and retention. Task 3 mandates designing the resulting heuristic paper itself. This presentation must be well-structured and incorporate visual elements like diagrams, charts, icons, and illustrations. The goal of the visual design is to reinforce key points, ensuring the paper is easy to understand and visually appealing. Following creation, Task 4 requires evaluating the work against specific assessment criteria. These criteria include Clarity and conciseness, checking if the heuristics are easy to remember. Another key evaluation point is Visual effectiveness, assessing if the visuals truly reinforce the heuristics. The student must also consider Practicality, determining if the heuristics are applicable and useful for learning. Finally, Originality demands that the paper demonstrates a creative approach to heuristic design. Examples of heuristics are provided for learning a concept, such as breaking down complex ideas or using active recall techniques. For presenting learning material, advice includes using clear language and structuring information logically with headings. Success hinges on considering the audience by tailoring heuristics and visuals specifically to target learners. Furthermore, incorporating a variety of visuals and keeping the material concise are advised tips. Students are also encouraged to seek feedback from peers to refine their heuristic design paper.

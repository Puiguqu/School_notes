# Design a heuristics paper

. Duration: 1 hour

In the video lecture we explored the concept of heuristics for specialised systems – digital audio workstations. If you're interested in learning more about this concept, you can read Sean's paper listed below: McGrath, S. 'Breaking the workflow: Design heuristics to support the development of usable digital audio production tools: framing usability heuristics for contemporary purposes' AM '20: Proceedings of the 15th International Audio Mostly Conference pp.130-137. You can search for this paper in the Online Library. A quick Google search for DAWs will give you a sense of what these systems look like. As a novice, what heuristics do you think you might devise for making these systems useful? Discoverability? Learnability? Accessibility? See if you can quickly draft three heuristics for DAWs that would be useful for someone new to this domain. You are now going to explore the principles of heuristic design and apply them to enhance learning materials at the University of London. By creating visual reminders and guidelines for effective learning, you will gain valuable experience in instructional design and contribute to improving the learning experience for your peers. Task 1. Choose a focus: Select a specific concept, module or learning objective that you want to ‘teach’ to other students. Choose something you know well! Alternatively, focus on a learning strategy like the University of London's CAFE model or a set of principles for presenting learning material effectively. 2. Develop heuristics: Based on your chosen focus, create a set of clear and concise heuristics that guide effective learning or instructional design. These could be used by educators to either design or evaluate their learning technologies - but the best heuristics tend to be good at doing both! These heuristics should offer practical advice and visual reminders to aid comprehension and retention. 3. Design a heuristic paper: Present your heuristics in a well-structured paper, using visual elements like diagrams, charts, icons and illustrations to reinforce key points. Ensure your paper is easy to understand, visually appealing and memorable. 4. Evaluate your work: Once you’ve created your heuristics paper, evaluate it against the following assessment criteria. Try to be objective when considering the following points and think about where and how you can improve your paper. Clarity and conciseness: Are the heuristics easy to understand and remember? Visual effectiveness: Do the visuals effectively reinforce the heuristics? Practicality: Are the heuristics applicable and useful for learning? Originality: Does the paper demonstrate a creative approach to heuristic design? Examples of heuristics For learning a concept: Break down complex ideas into smaller, manageable chunks. Connect new information to prior knowledge. Use active recall techniques to test your understanding. For presenting learning material: Use clear and concise language. Structure information logically with headings and subheadings. Incorporate visuals to enhance understanding. Tips for success Consider your audience: Tailor your heuristics and visuals to the target learners. Use a variety of visuals: Incorporate different types of visual elements to maintain engagement. Keep it concise: Focus on the most important heuristics and avoid overwhelming the reader. Seek feedback: your paper with peers for feedback and improvement.

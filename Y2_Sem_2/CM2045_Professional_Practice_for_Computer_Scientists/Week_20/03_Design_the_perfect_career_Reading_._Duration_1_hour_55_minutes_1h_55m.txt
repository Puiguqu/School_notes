The provided text summarizes two key academic frameworks—design thinking for career planning and building a developmental professional network—to guide career development.

First, the material draws upon *Designing your life: Build the perfect career, step by step* (Burnett and Evans, London: Vintage, 2018). This resource strongly encourages applying a design thinking approach to one's career roadmap. It advocates for a smart, systematic method of prototyping within a career context. The core function of this methodology is to demystify both career planning and the process of networking. A critical technical concept highlighted is the nature of a prototype itself. Burnett and Evans (2018) emphasize that "a prototype is not a thought experiment." Instead, it "must involve a physical experience in the world." The data needed for sound decision-making is derived from real-world work, making prototyping the best way to gather necessary data. Students are prompted to consider how they might use this prototyping approach to explore various career options. Furthermore, they must identify suitable contacts within their network for 'prototyping conversations.' These conversations require formulating specific questions to help improve the student’s overall career planning design.

Secondly, the text integrates findings from *An intelligent career: Taking ownership of your work and your life* (Arthur, Khapova and Richardson, Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2017). This material focuses specifically on the impact of the 'Knowing-whom' dimension on professional success. It provides a structured guide for building a developmental network tailored to specific career achievements. This framework suggests actionable ways to broaden professional contacts and sources of inspiration. Arthur, Khapova, and Richardson (2017) stress the importance of systemically thinking about this 'developmental network.' This involves defining what one intends to both take from and contribute to the people within that network. The reading material, found in the Online Library in the ProQuest Collection, outlines core responsibilities for the student. Students must identify potential members for their developmental network. They must also articulate the unique qualities they can bring to the network of others. A final prompt directs the student to integrate their University of London experience to enhance this developmental networking capacity.

Technical details associated with the readings include Chapter 6, Prototyping (OL-CM2045-Burnett-11161 Accessible.pdf), and Chapter 5, With whom do you work? (pp. 78–87). Both sources provide frameworks for systematic personal development. The overarching theme linking both academic pieces is the move from abstract planning to concrete, experiential action.

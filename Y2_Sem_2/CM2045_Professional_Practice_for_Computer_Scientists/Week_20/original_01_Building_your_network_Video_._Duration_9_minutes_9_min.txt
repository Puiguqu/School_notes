In this lecture, we're going to explore how you can build your regional and global network as a technology professional. Building a strong professional network is vital in technology careers, connecting you to people who can share advice, mentorship and potential job leads. This lecture will discuss practical strategies for expanding your network, leveraging both online tools and regional resources, and making the most of networking opportunities to accelerate your career. Employers and recruiters know that hiring through trusted recommendations is often more reliable and efficient than traditional recruitment methods. Many job openings, especially in tech, are filled before they are even advertised. Networking allows you to tap into these hidden opportunities. When you build relationships, people are more likely to consider you for roles within their companies. Employers value referrals because they come with a level of trust, which is why many unadvertised roles are filled through connections. Recruiters also appreciate candidates who network because they tend to be more engaged in the industry, proactive and resourceful. A solid professional network can indicate a candidate who not only has the technical skills required for a role, but also the interpersonal skills to thrive in a team-oriented environment. The key concept in professional networking is about building relationships that provide mutual value. It's not just about what others can do for you, but also how you can contribute to their goals. Think of networking as a cycle. You help others and in turn they support you. This concept of reciprocity makes networking a long-term investment in your career. By offering your skills, insights and support to others, you strengthen relationships that could benefit you down the line in unexpected ways. For example, in 10 years' time, you might be working with someone whom you met in your degree programme today. So how can you expand your network? You need to find a common link. The link may be that you're both students on this BSc Computer Science programme, or the wider University of London connection that all our students and alumni share, whatever their degree programme. Networks also develop through mutual acquaintances. Your existing professional, family or social contacts may be able to put you in touch with people they know who work in organisations or fields of interest to you. Beyond your immediate circles, attending industry events, whether in person or online, is a fantastic way to meet new professional contacts. You can gain first-hand information about jobs and how to improve your chances of being employed. Look out for conferences, hackathons and discussions taking place in your region, as well as online events globally. At the University of London, students are encouraged to engage in live chats during our Career Service webinars and panels, using this opportunity to make new contacts and gain insights. Networking at events may feel daunting at first, especially if you're not used to speaking to strangers. If that is the case, consider teaming up with a friend or fellow attendee to build your confidence. Remember that networking is an expected and valued part of these events. GitHub, LinkedIn, X and online discussion boards are also excellent places to build your contacts and ask questions. Connect to individuals and organisations that interest you and use keyword searches to explore related fields and discussions you can follow. Once you have made a link with a friendly individual, consider inviting them for a virtual coffee over Zoom or Teams. When networking in person or online, it is vital to make a good first impression. When networking at an event, do some homework beforehand. Research the people you want to contact so you are prepared and present yourself in a good light. Think about what your objectives are. Do you want to understand more about the industry or are you looking for specific advice about applying? When introducing yourself at face-to-face events, mention what your professional or academic interests are or why you are attending the event, and then ask an open-ended question to get the conversation going. Make a note of their name and any key points they make, along with their email address or social media handle, so that you can follow up with them if appropriate. When networking at online events, engage actively in the chat box. Respond to other participants' comments and questions in a positive way. If person-to-person messaging is enabled, use it to reach out to people and exchange contact details to facilitate further conversation after the event. Or you may be able to find them and follow up on social media platforms. For your follow-up conversations, it is useful to prepare some questions in advance. For example, what made you choose this career? What is a typical day at work like for you? What background experience is useful and how do people typically obtain it? Where and how are vacancies advertised? What would you look for in someone's CV or application? What advice would you give someone looking for work in this industry? What are the most important current issues in this sector that are likely to affect your job? Are there any particular individuals or organisations that I should be aware of? How would you recommend I keep up to date with developments in this field? What is the typical career progression in this type of work? How do you see this job developing in the future? up your meeting with a short courtesy email to thank them for their time. Include some details from your conversation to remind them of who you are and what you're looking for. Keep them informed of your progress, particularly if it resulted from advice that they gave you. Maintaining your network is essential if you intend on asking questions in the future and can be a gateway for meeting more potential contacts. Focus on providing value to your contacts. relevant articles, offer project assistance and congratulate people on their achievements. Stay in touch by engaging with their posts or sending a quick message to check in. It is also beneficial to actively seek ways to collaborate, whether it is on a project, a research initiative or simply exchanging ideas. Build relationships by showing genuine interest and creating a positive impression. Building a professional network develops and demonstrates your global employability skills. It requires adaptability to navigate different cultural norms, collaboration to work with people worldwide and communication to express ideas clearly. Networking also involves complex problem solving as you learn from others' perspectives, creativity and innovation as you exchange ideas and emotional intelligence to understand and respect different viewpoints. Ultimately, networking helps develop leadership, persuasion and negotiation skills. These skills will help you progress in your career development. Networking is a key skill for tech professionals, providing support, job leads and professional growth. From learning about new opportunities to collaborating on projects, a strong network can guide you at every career stage. Start building your network today to enhance your employability, find hidden job opportunities and expand your professional impact. The University of London Career Service is here to help. We provide resources to help you build your regional and global network. You can connect with our global LinkedIn page and the University of London Alumni Ambassador Network. We also offer webinars and pathways on different regional job markets which can help you build your regional network.

In this lecture, we're going to explore how you as computer science students can use your presentation skills to improve your performance in recruitment scenarios, such as interviews or assessment centres. Being able to deliver a persuasive presentation to a mixed technical and non-technical audience is often critical in securing your next role in the technology sector. In this lecture, we will explore strategies to improve your presentation skills, enhancing your performance during these critical job hunting phases. Good presentation skills will improve your performance at various stages of job hunting, from interviews to assessment centres. At the interview stage, these skills will help you structure your answers to interview questions and to appear confident and credible. In many interview scenarios, you will be asked to create and deliver a short presentation on a specific topic. The interviewers may then ask you follow-up questions. In more complex assessment centre scenarios, you may be asked to present solutions to case studies or work on group tasks. In each of these scenarios, your ability to clearly convey ideas, demonstrate problem-solving abilities and communicate well with others is crucial. Strong presentation skills help you showcase your technical expertise and interpersonal abilities. Creating a positive impression on recruiters and giving you an advantage over other candidates. Employers and recruiters value candidates with good presentation skills. They are looking for people who can articulate ideas clearly and work well with others. Presentation skills reflect one's communication abilities, which are vital for teamwork, client and funder interactions and leadership roles. In technology sectors, where complex concepts must often be communicated to non-technical stakeholders, clarity and confidence in presenting are especially valued. Recruiters view strong presentation skills as a sign of professionalism and preparedness. By demonstrating these skills, you show potential employers that you can handle real-world situations and effectively share your expertise, making you a more attractive candidate for their team. The key to success here is mastering both the content and delivery of your presentations. As other content in this topic has demonstrated, it is not enough to have technical knowledge. You must also engage your audience and convey your message effectively. This involves clear structuring, emphasising key points and using visuals to enhance understanding. Practising non-verbal cues like eye contact, posture and gestures can help establish a stronger connection with your listeners. And practice, practice, practice. In particular, practice with any technology that you are using as part of your presentation. Remember, successful presentations are not just about impressing others, but about ensuring they understand and remember your points. In a recruitment scenario, your presentation skills can be used to demonstrate how you would make a positive impact in the job and as part of the team. Focusing on these aspects helps you communicate better, build confidence and make a memorable impact. In recruitment scenarios for the technology sector, it is crucial to demonstrate your ability to make complex information accessible. Tailor your presentation to your audience, whether they are technical peers or non-technical stakeholders. Sometimes you will be briefed to create your presentation for a particular audience. At other times, you should address your presentation to the mix of roles represented on the recruitment panel. Avoid technical jargon unless you are certain the audience will understand it. Instead, use analogies or visual aids to explain complicated ideas. Spell out acronyms and abbreviations unless they are well known by non-specialists. Keep visuals clear and data focused, emphasising how the information impacts the company or project goals. Practice delivering key points confidently and aim to demonstrate how your solution or insights address real-world problems. This sector values innovative thinking, so it can be impactful to highlight fresh perspectives or novel solutions in your presentations. The University of London global employability skills are fundamental to good presentation skills. Effective presentation demands clear and concise communication, helping you to share complex ideas more easily. Emotional intelligence is important because good presentations require thought about what matters to your audience and how they might react to your points. Additionally, delivering presentation often involves teamwork or peer feedback, enhancing your collaboration skills. In recruitment scenarios, you can harness the power of collaboration to improve your presentations by discussing and rehearsing your approach with friends and mentors. In the technology sector, being able to communicate innovative ideas and problem-solving strategies is essential, as is the ability to connect with a global and diverse audience. Therefore, strong presentation skills play a pivotal role in showcasing these key employability skills. AI can be a powerful tool for refining your presentation skills. You can use AI-based software for grammar and language checks and enhancing your message clarity. AI-driven platforms like ChatGBT can help generate content ideas or can be used for practice question and answer sessions, allowing you to prepare for likely interview questions. AI can also help with presentation timing and provide feedback on tone and pacing. For example, Microsoft PowerPoint has an inbuilt recursal coach. However, using AI critically is essential. Don't rely solely on it. Instead, use AI to assist with your preparation, but use human intelligence to improve your understanding of the audience and ensure the presentation is targeted at the right level for them on this occasion. Focusing on making your delivery as human, engaging and interactive as possible. At the University of London Career Service, we provide both digital tools and human interaction to help you prepare your presentation skills for recruitment scenarios. Your approach to presentation skills for interviews will vary depending on whether you are a career starter, a career developer or career changer. As a career starter, focus on building confidence and learning the basics of structuring a presentation and delivering within the allocated time and to ensure that you are responding to the task. For career developers, work on refining your delivery skills and tailoring your message to align with your growing expertise and shift into a more strategic perspective. Career changers should highlight their transferable skills and structure their presentations to highlight links between their previous experience and the new role. Regardless of your career stage, careful design, rehearsal and self-reflection are crucial in ensuring that your presentations align with your current career goals and showcase your value effectively. A recruitment short presentation scenario will often be followed by a short question and answer session in which the interviewers ask follow-up questions. Take some time beforehand to think of the questions that might be asked in response to your presentation, perhaps discussing it with a friendly colleague or mentor. In the interview, actively listen to each question and take a moment to formulate your response. If necessary, clarify it to ensure understanding. Be concise, focusing on directly answering the query while reinforcing key points from your presentation. If you don't know the answer, be honest and suggest how you would go about finding out. Use questions as an opportunity to showcase your global employability skills of complex problem solving and adaptability and resilience.

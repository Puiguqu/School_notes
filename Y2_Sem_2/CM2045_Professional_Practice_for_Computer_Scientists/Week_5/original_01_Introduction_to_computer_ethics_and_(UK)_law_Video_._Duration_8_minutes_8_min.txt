But before we get ahead of ourselves, let's start by defining the basics. What do we mean by ethics and law? And more importantly, why should you, as a computing professional, care? Ethics is all about the principles that govern what's right and wrong. It's subjective, varies by culture, and often has no hard enforcement. In contrast, the law is a set of rules enforced by governments, and if you break them, well, let's just say that you might be swapping your coding skills for a lawyer's business card. Now, here's where it gets messy. Just because something is legal doesn't mean it's ethical. For instance, in the early days of the internet, companies could collect mountains of personal data legally, but was it ethical? That's where things start to get tricky. We've probably gone a bit too far the other way now, because you have to pretty much agree to sign your life away to navigate the web. Still, informed consent is a big part of what we do and important to seek from our users. Let's take a case study you're probably familiar with, the Cambridge Analytica scandal. A company harvested personal data from Facebook users, legally, mind you, under Facebook's terms and conditions, but then used that data for, let's say, questionable purposes, like political influence. The legal landscape was murky, but the ethical backlash was enormous, sparking public outrage and contributing to a much-needed reform of data protection laws, like the UK's Data Protection Act 2018 and the infamous GDPR. This brings us to a key question. How do we, as computing professionals, navigate this rapidly changing legal and ethical landscape? And it is changing. In the UK, the legal framework for digital technology is evolving constantly. We have legislation like the Computer Misuse Act in 1990, which was one of the first attempts to address cybercrime. It criminalises unauthorised access to computer systems, a fancy way of saying, hacking is bad, don't do it. But this law was passed before smartphones, social media and cloud computing even existed. That's right, the law is often playing catch-up with innovation. Back in 1990, the idea of storing data in the cloud sounded like sci-fi. Yet here we are, three decades later, asking whether 30-year-old laws are still relevant in today's tech landscape. Take the Data Protection Act 2018, which incorporates the principles of the GDPR. It governs how personal data is collected, stored and used in the UK. But even this recent law struggles to keep pace with AI systems that can predict our behaviour or algorithms that decide whether you get a loan or even a job interview. You'll cover concepts like this from a technical perspective if you do data science and machine learning at level 6, but here we're interested in the impact it has on human beings. Ethical questions spring up everywhere. Are these decisions fair, transparent and bias-free? There are a number of ethical dilemmas that remain in computing. Computing professionals aren't just following laws, they have to think ethically. Let's say you're developing facial recognition software. The law might allow it, but is it ethical if that software has higher error rates for people of colour, as several studies have shown? It's not just enough to say that there are technical limitations of the system when in practice this can unfairly lock people out of systems and cause genuine harm. Access needs to be equitable and these implicit biases can leave people feeling disillusioned and hurt. Computing ethics tackles issues like privacy, security, intellectual property and social responsibility. You'll hear terms like the digital divide, which refers to the gap between those who have access to technology and those who don't. Are we, as developers and engineers, making that gap wider or narrower? Issues such as privacy tend to be culturally influenced. Where some cultures might see age as sensitive information, others are happy to share things like their location and medical history without any concerns. Let's consider the impact of intellectual property for a moment. Intellectual property laws protect the rights of creators, whether it's software, music or even that meme you made last night. But how strict should those protections be? For instance, should software patents exist or do they stifle innovation by locking down ideas that others could build on? What if Linus Torvalds decided to make Linux closed source? We all use tools like Tex, the C programming language, Apache, Python, liberal licenses that make them accessible to all. Imagine not being able to read Shakespeare or Mark Twain because they're prohibitively expensive. Lawrence Lessig, an American legal scholar, once argued that code is law. What he means is that the design of our software, our algorithms, can have as much of an impact on our freedom as the laws passed by Parliament. This is why it's critical for you as computing professionals to understand not just the technical but also the legal and ethical implications of your work. Are you creating tools that empower people or are you building systems that surveil, restrict and control them? Speaking of classic text, I'm often reminded of 1984 when this discussion comes around. Think about online piracy. Illegal in the eyes of law, but some argue that copyright laws are outdated and overly restrictive. Or how about data encryption? The UK government has long debated whether law enforcement should have backdoor access to encrypted messages. Ethical? Legal? It depends who you ask. Who should have access? When? For how long? And the debates don't stop there. Look at AI. If an AI makes a mistake, say in diagnosing a patient or deciding an insurance claim, who's legally responsible? The developer? The company? The machine itself? Mester backpropagation algorithm senior? These are questions that UK law is still grappling with and they're likely to be topics of heated debate for years to come. We've seen AIs become racist, sexist and homophobic within a short space of time. What if they became invasive or even violent?

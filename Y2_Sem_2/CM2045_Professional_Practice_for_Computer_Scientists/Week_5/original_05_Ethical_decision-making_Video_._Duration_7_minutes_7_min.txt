Well it is, but it also raises some ethical concerns. One of the biggest ethical questions in this study was, how do we keep people safe while they're listening to their favourite tracks? We know music can be immersive, sometimes too immersive. Blocking out the sounds of the real world like approaching cars, cyclists or other pedestrians can be dangerous. To address this, the study experimented with jawbone headphones. Unlike regular earbuds, these headphones transmit sound through the bones near your ears, keeping your ear canals clear so you can still hear environmental sounds. This setup meant participants could listen to music without completely tuning out the world around them, helping them stay aware of potential hazards. As one participant commented, it was nice to still hear what was going on around me without the music cutting me off from the environment. A major feature of the GeoTrack system was its ability to dynamically adapt the music based on where the user was walking. For example, when a participant approached a busy road or a pedestrian crossing, the music would automatically become quieter and simpler. Imagine you're vibing to an energetic song with lots of layers, drums, vocals, guitar and suddenly, as you near a crossing, the system fades most of the instruments, leaving just a soft piano melody. This gives you mental space to focus on the potential danger, like cars or bikes whizzing by, while still enjoying the music. This approach reflects an important balance between entertainment and safety. The idea was to prevent the music from becoming a distraction when users needed to be most alert. Adjusting the music when near hazards may sound like a simple concept, but there are deeper ethical concerns here. How much can you simplify the music before it stops being enjoyable? How do you ensure people still feel the emotional connection to their music without risking their safety? In this study, we tried different approaches. For example, near a busy road, the mix might lower the volume of vocals and drums, but leave instrumental layers intact to keep the mood. How do we keep music adaptive without ruining the experience? Here's another interesting ethical question. How much control should users have over this adaptive system? While this ensured that participants stayed aware of their surroundings, some participants mentioned that they would have preferred more control over the music, particularly when it came to their favourite songs being altered. This leads to the broader ethical question in tech design. Do we prioritise user autonomy, or do we prioritise their safety, even if it means taking away some control? Do we always know best? Participants gave varied feedback about the experience. Most people agreed that the adaptive music enhanced their walk, making it feel like a soundtrack to their journey. This suggests a key challenge for developers of adaptive systems. How do you ensure the music always fits while maintaining users' safety? The system needs to balance technical precision with the unpredictability of real-world environments. Who has access to this data? How long is it stored? What are the potential risks of data breaches? Ethically, developers must ensure that all data is anonymised, stored securely, and used responsibly. Users should also be fully informed about what data is being collected and why.

The need for computational power is surging, as global data consumption is predicted to double every two years, reaching over 175 zettabytes by 2025. Streaming, cloud computing, and AI are the primary drivers of this massive data increase. Currently, the information and communication technology sector accounts for around 4% of global electricity usage, with continuous growth anticipated. Furthermore, data centers alone contribute roughly 3% of global CO2 emissions, a figure comparable to the entire airline industry. Beyond energy, data centers pose a major water challenge, exemplified by Microsoft’s 2020 consumption of over 250 million liters in Quincy, Washington. To address these conflicts, sustainable computing strategies are being implemented across three major themes: Green AI, Cloud Optimisation, and Circular Computing. Green AI approaches seek to dramatically reduce the energy footprint associated with training complex models. For instance, training a single large language model like GPT-3 can emit 284 tonnes of CO2, equivalent to five cars' entire lifecycles. Researchers are mitigating this by using smaller models and adopting transfer learning to cut down energy demands. NVIDIA has responded with hardware-optimized solutions like the DGX-A100 systems, which can reduce overall power consumption by up to 30%. In cloud optimisation, companies are implementing dynamic resource scaling, which adjusts server capacity based on real-time demand. Another key development is edge computing, processing data closer to the source to reduce both latency and energy use. Major providers are committed to renewable energy, with Google Cloud aiming to operate completely on carbon-free energy by 2030. Google Cloud achieved 100% carbon neutrality in 2020 and now uses predictive models to adjust power, resulting in a 40% reduction in cooling energy needs. Circular computing aims to counteract the massive problem of e-waste, which reached 57 million metric tonnes in 2021 alone. This discipline extends hardware lifecycles through recycling, refurbishing, and repurposing, thus curbing new manufacturing waste. Dell demonstrates this with its Optiplex 7070 Ultra series, a modular desktop made from recycled plastics. Beyond corporate efforts, the industry must design hardware that is inherently more repairable and modular to reduce waste. Consumers can play a vital role by optimizing their code, opting for refurbished tech, and prioritizing sustainable products. Overall, the path to greener computing requires constant innovation and a commitment to efficiency at every level. Adopting these sustainable practices—from AI training to resource management—is essential for minimizing the environmental cost of technological advancement.

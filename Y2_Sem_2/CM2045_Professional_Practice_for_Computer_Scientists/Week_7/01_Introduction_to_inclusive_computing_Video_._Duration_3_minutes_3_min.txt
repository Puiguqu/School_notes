Inclusive computing focuses on designing technology usable by the widest range of people, ensuring nobody is left behind in the tech revolution. At its core, it means designing technology that is not merely available but is also usable and enjoyable for everyone. This approach requires accounting for diverse factors, including physical, cognitive, and sensory abilities, alongside language, socioeconomic, and cultural variations. The significance of inclusivity is profound because approximately 15% of the global population lives with some form of disability. Failure to adopt inclusive tech means potentially excluding 1 billion people from fully accessing or benefiting from modern tools. Furthermore, inclusivity benefits innovation by pushing technological boundaries when designing for all users.

Major examples of this concept include Microsoft’s Xbox Adaptive Controller, which accommodates gamers with limited mobility through large buttons and various ports. Another powerful example is Google's Project Euphonia, an AI project that helps individuals with speech impairments communicate effectively by training the system on non-standard speech patterns. To successfully implement inclusive computing, several principles must be followed. Technology must offer Flexibility in use, providing multiple ways for users to interact with it, such as text-based and voice controls. Interfaces should be Simple and intuitive, making them easy to understand even for first-time users. Crucially, information must be Perceptible, meaning it is provided in multiple formats, such as adding captions to benefit those with hearing impairments or those in noisy environments.

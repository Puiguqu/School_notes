# Ethical impacts of technology

The lecture examines the profound ethical impact of modern technology, acknowledging that while technology offers incredible advances in medicine and communication, it creates significant ethical dilemmas. Technology's power necessitates responsibility, covering issues ranging from AI loan decisions to social media influence on elections. Key ethical questions transcend mere feasibility and focus instead on what is morally right. The discussion begins with privacy, a critical concern exacerbated by the vast collection of personal data by companies like Google and Meta. A Pew Research study highlighted that 79% of Americans are worried about how their personal data is utilized by corporations. Government overreach was exemplified by Edward Snowden's revelations regarding the NSA's surveillance programs, challenging the balance between security and privacy. The Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal serves as a prime case study, detailing how data was harvested without consent for political manipulation during the 2016 US election. This scandal led to global outrage and the establishment of regulations like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe.

A second major concern is bias and discrimination embedded in AI systems, which makes decisions on crucial aspects of life, such as hiring or loan approvals. These AI systems are only as unbiased as the data they are trained on, and that data often reflects human biases. Amazon's AI recruiting tool demonstrated this flaw by penalizing resumes containing words associated with women, as it was trained on historical male-dominated data. Furthermore, MIT studies revealed that popular facial recognition systems had an error rate of approximately 34.7% when identifying dark-skinned women. This bias was tragically highlighted in 2020 when Robert Williams was wrongfully arrested in Detroit due to a mistaken identification by such software.

Turning to the economic sphere, automation and job displacement pose a significant ethical challenge. Oxford University reports that up to 47% of US jobs could be automated in the next two decades, affecting both manual and white-collar labor. This raises the core ethical question of who benefits from automation, and whether society should facilitate worker retraining. Autonomous vehicles introduce the classic moral dilemma, known as the trolley problem, forcing algorithms to decide between minimizing overall harm or prioritizing the passenger's life.

The final major focus is the digital divide, which creates inequity by separating those with technological access from those without it. Even though 60% of the global population has internet access, billions remain excluded from the digital economy. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed this divide when an estimated 16 million students in the US lacked the resources needed for remote learning. To mitigate these risks, the report outlines several strategies: implementing ethical design principles, ensuring diversity in tech development teams, and enforcing stronger government regulation, like GDPR. Finally, public education is crucial to empower users to make informed decisions regarding the privacy they trade. By prioritizing ethics from the start, technology can be guided to benefit all segments of society.

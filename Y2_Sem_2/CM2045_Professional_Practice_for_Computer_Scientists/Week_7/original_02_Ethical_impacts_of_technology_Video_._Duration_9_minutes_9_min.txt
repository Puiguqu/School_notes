It's no secret that technology has transformed nearly every aspect of our lives, but with great power comes great responsibility, ethically speaking. Whether it's AI deciding who gets a loan or social media shaping elections, technology creates some tough ethical dilemmas. Today we'll explore the most significant ethical impacts of modern technology, look at some case studies where things went very wrong, and discuss how we as technologists and users can navigate these issues. I've intentionally blurred the lines a bit between some of these topics because I want you to be introduced and reintroduced to these elements on a regular basis. We'll continue this process of exploring different concepts at both granular and abstract levels of depth in order to really see the bigger picture. Technology has both positive and negative effects. On one side, it has enabled incredible advances in medicine, communication, and education. But on the other side, it's responsible for privacy breaches, job automation anxiety, and widening the digital divide. Ethical questions aren't just about what's technologically possible, they're about what's right. Just because we can create AI systems that track every move you make online doesn't mean we should. Or should we? Let's start with privacy. In the digital age, privacy has become one of the biggest ethical concerns. Think about how much personal data is collected by companies like Google, Meta, or even your fitness apps. In fact, a study by Pew Research found that 79% of Americans are concerned about how their personal data is being used by companies. For many, it's an unknown about what data is being collected and how it's being utilised. But it's not just companies. Governments are in on it too. Edward Snowden's revelations about the National Security Agency's surveillance programmes show just how far governments can go in tracking citizens. This raises a critical ethical question. How much privacy are we willing to trade for security or convenience? I would argue that we've already conceded a great deal, but we're also seeing a pushback. One of the most infamous cases of privacy invasion is the Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal. In 2018, it was revealed that Cambridge Analytica had harvested data from millions of Facebook users without their consent to create targeted political ads during the 2016 US presidential election. The ethical breach here wasn't just about violating privacy. It was about manipulating public opinion. As one user famously put it in a post after the scandal broke, We're not Facebook's customers, we're its product. The scandal led to global outrage, fines and new regulations like the GDPR, the General Data Protection Regulation, in Europe. But it raises a key ethical question. How do we balance technological innovation with user consent and transparency? Many systems are allowed to exist untethered, and that has risk all by itself. up is bias and discrimination in AI systems. AI is increasingly making decisions about important aspects of our lives. For example, who gets a loan, or who's hired for a job. But these systems are only as unbiased as the data they're trained on. And guess what? Our data often contains human biases. Take for instance Amazon's AI recruiting tool that was found to discriminate against women. The algorithm had been trained on resumes submitted over the past 10 years. Resumes which predominantly came from men. As a result, the AI penalised resumes that included the word women, or were from women's colleges. This demonstrates the fundamental flaws in many of these systems. Another example of bias in technology is the racial bias in facial recognition software. A study from MIT found that popular facial recognition systems, such as those used by law enforcement, had an error rate of around 34.7% when identifying dark-skinned women, compared to less than 1% for lighter-skinned men. These kinds of errors aren't just inconvenient, they're dangerous. In 2020, a black man named Robert Williams was wrongfully arrested in Detroit because facial recognition software mistakenly identified him as a suspect. This raises ethical concerns about discrimination and accountability in AI. How can we ensure AI systems are fair and transparent when they're increasingly being used in high-stakes situations? People put a huge amount of trust in these systems, but they very often get it badly wrong. Let's move to the economic side of tech ethics. Automation and job displacement. As AI and robotics become more advanced, more jobs are at risk of being automated. According to a report from Oxford University, up to 47% of jobs in the US could be automated in the next two decades. This isn't just about robots replacing factory workers, it's about automation affecting white-collar jobs, from accountants to radiologists. The ethical question here is, who benefits from automation? Are we building a future where only tech companies profit while millions of workers are displaced? Or are we finding ways to retrain workers for new, tech-enabled jobs? Many of you will work on automated solutions using libraries like Selenium. Would you be as willing to develop these tools if you knew they were making people jobless? Here's an interesting example from the world of automation. Self-driving cars. These vehicles are poised to reduce traffic accidents, but they also present a classic moral dilemma. Imagine a scenario where a self-driving car has to decide between hitting a pedestrian or swerving and putting the passenger's life at risk. This is known as the trolley problem, but in real life. Should the car prioritise the life of its passenger, or should it try to minimise overall harm? How should the algorithms be programmed to make these decisions, and who gets to decide the ethical framework? Companies like Tesla and Waymo are grappling with these very questions as they develop autonomous vehicles. Would you be more likely to buy a vehicle that prioritised your life over someone else's? Of course. What about if this is only a premium option to a handful of wealthy individuals? Doesn't seem fair, does it? Finally, let's talk about the digital divide. While technology has the potential to democratise information and access, it's also creating a wider gap between those who have access to those technologies and those who don't. In a world where 60% of the global population has internet access, that still leaves billions without the ability to participate in the digital economy. Even within wealthy nations, there's a divide between urban areas with high-speed internet and rural communities struggling with poor connectivity. This raises questions about equity and access. Is it ethical to create a world driven by technology when large parts of the population are left behind? The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the digital divide like never before. As schools moved online, millions of students without reliable internet access or devices were left behind. In the US, an estimated 16 million students lacked the tools needed for remote learning. At Goldsmiths, we barely saw an impact in the computing department, but I saw many of my colleagues in other departments struggle to make the transition to online only. Both educators and students missed out, but at least they had the resources to mitigate many of these issues. This isn't just a technological issue, it's an ethical failure. We need to ensure that as we move towards a more digital world, education, healthcare and economic opportunities are accessible to everyone, not just the tech-savvy or well-connected. So how can we mitigate these ethical risks? Here are a few strategies. Ethical design principles. Companies need to implement ethical design frameworks that prioritise transparency, fairness and privacy from the start. Diversity in tech development. Building diverse teams of developers and decision makers can help reduce biases in the technologies that we create. Stronger regulation and accountability. Governments do need to step in with stronger regulations like the GDPR in Europe to ensure companies are held accountable for how they handle data and how they use AI. While other places in the world might be able to innovate more freely, they remain in a space where this uncertainty also poses risks. Public education. Today we need to educate the public on the ethical implications of technology so that they can make informed decisions about the products they use and the privacy they trade. You'll see some poor design practices if you study interaction design, but these are the sorts of things you need to be aware of if you are to understand the risk. In conclusion, the ethical impacts of technology are profound and complex. From privacy breaches to AI biases, from job automation to the digital divide, we must continually ask ourselves, how do we build a future where technology benefits everyone, not just the privileged few? The answers aren't always simple, but by putting ethics at the forefront of our innovations, we can create a world where technology uplifts society rather than divides it.

Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Welcome to today's session on accessibility in software design. Today we're going to explore how accessible design makes technology usable for everyone, regardless of their abilities. Accessibility isn't just a technical feature, it's a human feature. The goal? To ensure no one is excluded from using the apps, websites and tools we build. In this lecture we'll look at a case study to see how one major company tackled accessibility challenges, and we'll explore some best practices that can help ensure inclusive software design. Let's start with the basics. What is accessibility in software design? At its core, accessibility means designing technology that can be used by people of all abilities, including those with physical, sensory, cognitive or learning disabilities. The World Health Organization reports that 1 billion people globally live with some form of disability. That's about 15% of the population. So, if your software isn't accessible, you could be excluding a huge number of potential users. But it's not just about numbers. It's about creating equal access to the digital world. As Tim Berners-Lee, the inventor of the web, famously said, the power of the web is in its universality. Access by everyone, regardless of disability, is an essential aspect. One of the best examples of a company embracing accessibility in software design is Microsoft. Over the last decade, Microsoft has placed accessibility at the core of its design philosophy, culminating in features that support users with disabilities across its products, whether it's Windows, Office or Xbox. In 2019, Microsoft introduced a feature in Microsoft Word and Outlook called Immersive Reader, designed to support people with dyslexia and other reading disabilities. Immersive Reader allows users to break down text into simpler chunks, change font sizes, spacing and even highlight syllables to improve readability. What's more, it includes a read aloud feature that uses AI to read the text in clear, natural speech. According to Microsoft, our mission is to empower every person and every organization on the planet to achieve more. That means everyone, including the one billion people with disabilities. After releasing Immersive Reader, a study by the British Dyslexia Association showed that students using the tool improved their reading comprehension significantly. The tool wasn't just useful for people with dyslexia, it turned out to benefit all users, proving that accessible design can help everyone. This concept is known as universal design, where products are designed to be accessible to the widest possible range of people. It's a win-win. As a designer, you not only make your product more inclusive, but you also make it better for all users. Obligations in accessibility. It's not just ethical to create accessible software, it's also the law. Many countries have strict regulations around digital accessibility. For instance, the Americans with Disabilities Act, ADA, requires that businesses and public services make their websites and digital products accessible to people with disabilities. In the European Union, the Web Accessibility Directive mandates that public sector websites and apps meet accessibility standards. Failure to comply can lead to hefty fines. In 2019, Domino's Pizza found this out the hard way. A blind man, Guillermo Robles, sued the company because he was unable to order pizza from their website and mobile app using a screen reader. The U.S. Supreme Court sided with Robles, highlighting that companies must ensure their digital platforms are accessible. This ruling set a legal precedent and it's a major warning for businesses. Let's look at some key accessibility features that should be integrated into any software design. Screen reader support. For users with visual impairments, screen readers like JAWS or NVDA read aloud what's on the screen. Websites and apps need to be compatible with these tools, including providing alt text for images and ensuring text is properly structured. Keyboard navigation. Some users can't rely on a mouse due to motor impairments, so your software should be fully navigable with just a keyboard. Every interactive element, buttons, links, menus, should be reachable using the tab key. Color contrast and text size. People with low vision or color blindness benefit from high contrast text and the ability to adjust font sizes. Tools like WebAIM's contrast checker help ensure that text is legible for all users. Captions and transcripts. For people with hearing impairments, video content should include captions. According to a Facebook study, 80% of viewers are more likely to watch a video to completion when captions are available. Not just people with hearing impairments, but also users in noisy environments. Let's circle back to the Domino's case for a moment. The core issue wasn't just that the website was inaccessible, it was that Domino's didn't think digital accessibility was a priority. Their defense argued that the ADA written in 1990 didn't explicitly apply to the internet. But the courts disagreed and the lesson for all businesses is clear. Digital accessibility is no longer optional. Just like you make sure your physical store has a wheelchair ramp, your online platforms must be designed for accessibility. As a court case concluded, the failure to make digital platforms accessible violates the basic rights of individuals with disabilities under the ADA. So don't wait for a lawsuit to remind you. Start designing with accessibility in mind from day one. Let's not forget the benefits. Designing accessible software doesn't just protect you from legal risks, it also opens your product to a larger audience. Accessible design leads to higher user engagement. When more people can use your product, engagement naturally increases. Customer loyalty. Users appreciate companies that consider their needs and they're more likely to stick around if they feel included. Innovation. Accessibility features often lead to innovation that can improve the experience for everyone. The classic example? The dropped curb. Originally designed to help wheelchair users, it ended up being useful for parents with strollers, cyclists and delivery people. In the world of software, the same principles apply. Voice recognition and text-to-speech tools were originally designed for accessibility, but now they're used by millions for convenience. The future of software design is inclusive by default. As technology evolves, so do the tools we use to ensure accessibility. AI-powered features like real-time transcription are becoming more common, and platforms like YouTube and Zoom now offer automatic captioning to ensure everyone can follow along. It's important that we continue to innovate, not just because it's the right thing to do, but because it's smart business. Accessible design isn't a niche concern. It's a crucial part of creating products that work for everyone. To wrap up, accessibility in software design is about creating inclusive, usable experiences for everyone, regardless of ability. Companies like Microsoft are leading the way with accessible features that benefit millions of users, while cases like Domino's remind us of the legal and ethical responsibilities that come with building digital products. Remember, accessibility isn't just about meeting requirements. It's about opening the door to a more equitable and innovative digital future.

# Employer voice – inclusive computing

Organizations must incorporate diverse employee perspectives by focusing initially on comprehensive cultural training for internal stakeholders. Secondly, they must involve diverse groups and stakeholders—whether by co-designing or monitoring applications—in the decision-making process to ensure every voice is heard. A third, crucial step is conducting regular cultural and societal audits to review systems and guarantee usability across different groups, preventing bias or discrimination. Ethically and legally, frameworks like the UK's Equality Act and the EU's AI Act demonstrate the potential for significant legislative and reputational damage. Crucially, organizations must account for inclusivity regarding not just products, but also users and vendors.

To ensure computing platforms are accessible to neurodiverse employees, HR must first train on neurodiversity, educating line managers and stakeholders. Secondly, investment must be made in assistive technology and customizable platforms to cater to varied needs, such as text-to-speech and speech-to-text capabilities. Furthermore, understanding that focusing on equity leads to inclusion is paramount for improving the workforce. When automating systems that monitor employee performance, transparency is the primary requirement, necessitating clear communication about how algorithms function.

Companies must implement regular audits for bias and adopt a "human-in-the-loop" approach whenever any automated process is deployed. The advanced capability of generative AI should be utilized as an audit tool to locate biases within existing organizational systems, such as hiring or performance management pipelines. Designing systems must incorporate an "intersectionality by design" approach, evaluating identities including subcategories like race and gender. Practical steps include ensuring inclusive design principles and maintaining diverse implementation teams, coupled with rigorous feedback loops and phased rollouts.

Regarding digital literacy, an organization is obligated to invest in tailored upskilling programs, as digital fluency is now comparable to basic standard literacy. Furthermore, by collaborating with NGOs, organizations can increase their social mark while bridging the literacy gap among underrepresented groups. This strategic focus simultaneously benefits the individual, the employer, and the organization’s overall profitability.

In tech, inclusivity isn't just a buzzword, it's a key part of building ethical, sustainable and innovative technology. Whether you're designing software, working on AI or developing the next big app, inclusivity ensures that everyone benefits from what you're building. Today we'll cover some practical strategies for implementing inclusive practices in your work. We'll look at tools, methodologies and best practices that you can start using today with examples from industry leaders like Microsoft, Google and others who are doing inclusivity right. Before we look into the how, let's quickly talk about why inclusivity is so critical in technology. According to McKinsey, companies with higher diversity are 35% more likely to have financial returns above their industry's median. It's not just about representation, it's about better decision making, broader market reach and more innovative solutions. As Tim Cook, CEO of Apple once said, the most diverse group will produce the best product. If you're going to have a creative environment like Apple's, you need different perspectives. In computer science, inclusivity ensures that we're designing technologies that don't exclude people based on gender, race, disability or any other factors. Goldsmiths holds the same ethos. We have people from all walks of life coming together to do amazing things. Let's jump into our first strategy, inclusive design thinking. This approach involves thinking about inclusivity from the very beginning of the design process. It means considering a wide range of user needs and experiences, including those of people with disabilities, cultural differences and various cognitive abilities. Take Microsoft's inclusive design toolkit, which encourages developers to ask questions like who is excluded by this design and how can we extend this to include more people? By building inclusivity into the design process, you end up with solutions that work for everyone. One of the key principles here is design for one, extend to many. This means that by designing for users who might face the greatest barriers, such as people with disabilities, you can create solutions that improve the experience for a wider audience. , let's talk about the importance of diverse development teams. The more diverse your team is, the more perspectives you bring into the development process. This diversity helps you identify any problem areas early on and think creatively about how your technology can serve all users. For example, Google uses diverse hiring practices to ensure its teams reflect the global user base they serve. By bringing in people from different backgrounds, experiences and abilities, they are able to design products like Google Maps that consider accessibility features such as wheelchair-friendly routes and voice navigation for people with visual impairments. Quick tip, when forming teams, actively seek out underrepresented groups in tech. Whether it's women, people of colour or people with disabilities, more perspectives means more inclusive solutions. A great practical approach to inclusivity is putting accessibility first in your software development. This involves not treating accessibility as an afterthought or something to add in later, but adding it into the core functionality of your product from the start. One best practice here comes from the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, or WCAG, which provide standards for ensuring that websites are accessible to all users. Things like high contrast colour schemes, keyboard navigability and screen reader compatibility should be considered from the outset. Consider Slack, which has introduced features like these. These features not only benefit people with disabilities, but also improve the overall user experience for everyone. One of the most pressing challenges in tech right now is algorithmic bias in AI systems. AI models are only as good as the data that they're trained on. And if that data contains biases, the AI will reflect and possibly even amplify them. To combat this, computer scientists need to implement bias detection and mitigation techniques during the development of AI systems. For example, IBM has developed the AI Fairness 360, a toolkit designed to help developers detect and mitigate bias in machine learning models. This tool allows developers to test their algorithms against fairness metrics, ensuring that the technology doesn't unfairly disadvantage certain groups of people. To quote Joy Buolamwini, a computer scientist and founder of the Algorithmic Justice League, AI is not neutral. We have a responsibility to ensure it reflects the best of humanity, not the worst. Inclusive development doesn't stop at design and coding. It's equally important to conduct user testing with a diverse group of people. This ensures your product works for a wide variety of users, including those with disabilities, different languages, and various levels of technical literacy. Consider the way that Apple tests its accessibility features like voiceover and magnifier with a variety of users, including people with visual impairments. By testing in real-world conditions and gathering feedback from diverse users, Apple ensures that their devices are usable by all. The key here is to listen. Your users are your greatest resource when it comes to spotting issues and opportunities for inclusivity. Another practical strategy is writing inclusive code. This means ensuring that the code you write can be easily adapted to support different languages, screen sizes, and accessibility tools. Things like properly labelled HTML tags for screen readers, or using variable names that are clear and neutral contribute to making your software more inclusive. For example, when working on websites, ensuring that all images have descriptive alt text allows screen readers to describe the content to users who are visually impaired. Similarly, using ARIA roles, accessible rich internet application roles, in your web design helps people who rely on assistive technology navigate your application more easily. The best part? Writing inclusive code isn't difficult. It's just about being mindful. Accessibility isn't a set-it-and-forget-it approach. It's something that must be continuously improved. Offering ongoing training programs for developers is essential in staying updated on the best practices for accessibility and inclusivity. Many companies, including Adobe, offer internal training programs where developers learn how to incorporate inclusivity into their daily work. This might include training on unconscious bias, learning how to test for accessibility, or understanding the diverse needs of users. To sum up, inclusivity isn't just a box to tick. It's a responsibility and an opportunity. By following these strategies, using inclusive design thinking, forming diverse teams, prioritizing accessibility, mitigating bias in AI, testing with diverse users, writing inclusive code, and continuously learning, you can create technology that serves everyone. Thanks for joining me today.

Some screen readers may require using CTRL in conjunction with the alt key Welcome to today's lecture on assessing ethical impacts in technology. In a world where tech is evolving faster than ever, ethical considerations have become critical to ensuring we're not only building powerful tools, but also responsible ones. Whether it's social media platforms, shaping public discourse, AI systems making decisions for us, or data being collected and sold behind the scenes, we need to ask, what are the ethical impacts of the technology that we create? Today we're going to focus on how we can assess these impacts, including holding companies accountable for their actions. We'll dive into real world examples like social media platforms, and discuss best practices for embedding ethics into tech development. Let's get started. So why do we need to assess the ethical impacts of technology? Technology is integrated into every part of our lives, from the apps we use, to the AI that recommends content, to the algorithms that decide who gets a loan. As creators of these technologies, computer scientists have a responsibility to think about how their work affects society. A study by Pew Research found that 79% of Americans believe that tech companies need to be held accountable for the products that they create. Think about it. Algorithms can reinforce biases, social media can spread misinformation, and data breaches can expose personal information. Ethical assessments help us mitigate these risks. They force us to ask tough questions like, is this technology being used for harm? And who benefits, and who might be excluded? With great power comes great responsibility, and nowhere is that more true than in tech. Let's start by looking at one of the most visible ethical issues in tech today. Social media accountability. Platforms like Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube have reshaped the way we communicate, but they've also been criticised for allowing the spread of misinformation, hate speech, and polarising content. In the Facebook Cambridge Analytica scandal, where millions of users' data was harvested without consent to influence elections, this wasn't just a breach of privacy, it raised ethical questions about the role of social media in manipulating public opinion. Mark Zuckerberg has testified multiple times before Congress, promising reform, but the ethical questions remain. How do we hold these platforms accountable? Social media companies are now facing pressure to fact-check content, promote transparency, and curb harmful behaviours. But are these steps enough? Holding platforms accountable is no easy task. It's a balancing act between free speech and preventing harm. Here are some strategies tech companies and regulators can use to assess the ethical impacts of social media. Content moderation. Platforms can implement stronger moderation systems that use both AI and human reviewers to flag inappropriate content. For example, YouTube has been improving its AI system to better detect hate speech and misinformation, but it still relies on human oversight to make the final decisions. Transparency reports. Social media platforms should regularly publish transparency reports that detail how they can handle user data, what kind of content is being removed, and why. This increases accountability and gives users insight into the platform's decision-making processes. Independent audits. Independent third-party audits can be conducted to assess whether platforms are complying with ethical standards. This includes evaluating algorithms for bias and examining how user data is used and stored. One practical tool for assessing the ethical impacts of technology is an ethical impact assessment. Similar to environmental impact assessments, EIAs provide a structured way to evaluate the ethical implications of a tech project before it is deployed. An EIA considers factors like privacy and data security, how is user data being collected, stored, and shared, what are the risks of a data breach, bias and fairness, is the technology designed in a way that avoids reinforcing bias or discrimination. Social impact. How will the technology affect different social groups? Will it create a divide between those who have access to it and those who don't? For example, AI development companies like OpenAI have begun integrating EIAs into their workflow to assess how their models could be misused or cause harm. Universities such as this one have ethics committees with independent assessment of risk for projects. Not just an afterthought, but something that could affect funding decisions for public research. Even companies with the best intentions can stumble when it comes to ethics. Take Google for example. In 2019, they established an AI Ethics Council to help guide the development of their AI products. The council was tasked with assessing the social and ethical impacts of AI systems. However, the council quickly became controversial when it was revealed that some of the appointed members had conflicting interests and had made controversial statements about issues like climate change and diversity. Amid backlash, Google dissolved the council just a week later. This highlights an important lesson. Ethical oversight must be transparent, diverse, and genuinely committed to serving the public good. Simply creating a committee doesn't solve ethical problems. There has to be a clear plan for how ethics are integrated into the development process. Bias in AI systems is another area where ethical assessments are crucial. AI is used in everything from facial recognition to job recruitment, but biased data can lead to discriminatory outcomes. You may remember from an earlier lecture that Amazon's AI hiring tool was scrapped after it was discovered that it was biased against women. How many times have you failed a capture that was impossible or been unfairly challenged by technology? For some people, that's an everyday occurrence based on something as arbitrary as the colour of their skin. One way to assess and mitigate bias is by using bias detection tools like AI Fairness 360 from IBM, which I mentioned earlier. These tools evaluate AI models to ensure that they are not unfairly discriminating against certain groups. But beyond tools, diverse teams of engineers and researchers can help identify and reduce bias in AI systems. Joy Buolamwini, a leading voice in AI ethics, put it well, Algorithms are not neutral. We must actively work to make them equitable or they will reinforce the biases of society. We spend lots of time at Goldsmiths on decolonizing the curriculum, but so much of colonialism is deeply ingrained in contemporary pedagogical practices. It is really difficult to see things like a lack of diversity, representation, and sometimes even intrinsic bias. Ethical technology also requires strong regulations. Without oversight, companies can easily prioritize profits over people. That's why frameworks like GDPR, the General Data Protection Regulation in Europe, have been so impactful. GDPR requires companies to get explicit consent before collecting data, and it gives users the rights to access and delete their data. In the US, there's growing interest in passing similar legislation, especially after the fallout from Cambridge Analytica and the increasing power of AI systems. Laws like the California Consumer Privacy Act, CCPA, are steps in the right direction, but much more is needed to ensure tech companies are legally accountable for the ethical impacts of their products. So how can computer scientists ensure their work is ethically sound? Here are a few best practices for ethical tech development. Build diverse teams. As we've discussed, diversity in development teams helps reduce bias and ensures a wider range of perspectives. Conduct ethical impact assessments. Before launching a product, consider conducting an EIA to assess potential ethical risks, from privacy concerns to social inequality. Incorporate transparency. Whether through transparency reports or open source code, making your processes visible to the public is a critical way to build trust and demonstrate accountability. Engage with ethical frameworks. Familiarise yourself with ethical frameworks like the IEEE's Ethics in Action or Google's AI Principles. These guidelines help developers make decisions that prioritise ethical considerations alongside technical goals. Sometimes looking at guidelines like those provided by the American Psychology Association can help us to understand contemporary challenges in areas of social computing and studies around usability. We must always be thinking critically about how we can do better. In conclusion, assessing the ethical impacts of technology isn't just an optional extra. It's a fundamental part of creating responsible, equitable systems, whether it's holding social media platforms accountable, mitigating AI bias or implementing ethical frameworks, we have a duty to ensure our innovations are a force for good. As Sheryl Sandberg, COO of Meta once said, we need to work together to ensure that our technology works for everyone, not just the privileged few. This was a hard topic to cover because many of my friends and colleagues have been victims to poor technological and design choices. It's not nice to think about people suffering.

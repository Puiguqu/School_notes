1. Sustainability in Computing addresses the significant environmental footprint of small electronics like smartphones, laptops, and tablets.
2. Creating these devices requires extracting finite resources, generating toxic waste, and producing considerable amounts of CO2.
3. According to the Global E-Waste Monitor, the world generated 53.6 million metric tons of e-waste in 2019, a figure that continues to rise.
4. Alarmingly, only 17% of this e-waste is properly recycled, allowing the rest to leach harmful chemicals into landfills, often in developing nations.
5. A core challenge is the use of rare earth elements, including neodymium, tantalum, and cobalt, which are critical for modern electronics.
6. Extracting these elements is highly resource-intensive, causing severe environmental damage and soil degradation, particularly evident in mining sites like those in China and the DRC.
7. Furthermore, the mining for cobalt, used in lithium-ion batteries, carries a devastating human cost, with reports indicating major issues with child labor.
8. The average smartphone lifespan is only about two years, driving rapid consumption and contributing to the tech waste problem.
9. In 2019 alone, the United Nations University reported that e-waste contained $57 billion worth of recoverable precious metals, most of which was lost due to improper recycling.
10. A major driver of waste is planned obsolescence—a strategy of designing products with a limited lifespan, such as software slowdowns or non-replaceable batteries.
11. This issue, emphasized by Gay Gordon Byrne’s "Right to Repair" movement, forces consumers into buying new devices rather than fixing old ones.
12. Manufacturing is incredibly resource-heavy; a single smartphone, for instance, utilizes 62 different metals, including gold and rare earths.
13. The production process requires large amounts of water, energy, and chemicals, contributing significantly to climate change.
14. Specifically, a 2016 Green Electronics Council report found that manufacturing one typical smartphone generates 85 kilograms of CO2, a weight exceeding the device itself by more than ten times.
15. To address this, tech companies are initiating sustainability commitments, such as Apple using 100% recycled aluminum in the MacBook Air.
16. Google aims to make all its hardware products carbon neutral by 2030, while Microsoft is heavily investing in circular computing to achieve carbon negative status by the same year.
17. Complementing corporate efforts is the critical "Right to Repair" movement, supported by legislation in the European Union and states like California.
18. Advocates push for laws that empower consumers to repair devices through third parties without voiding warranties.
19. The circular economy model promotes using biodegradable plastics, recycled metals, and plant-based materials in production.
20. Companies like Fairphone exemplify this by building modular smartphones designed to be easily repaired using responsibly sourced and recycled components.
21. Consumers play a vital role by participating in responsible recycling and take-back schemes offered by tech companies.
22. More impactful than recycling, however, is extending a device’s life through repair and upgrading rather than immediate replacement.
23. Curricular coverage confirms this focus, covering specific modules like Week 9 Topic 5: Sustainability in computing, Part 1, and Lesson 9.1 and 9.2.
24. The curriculum also structurally addresses supporting topics, including Week 7 Topic 4 (Inclusive computing) and Week 6 Topic 3 (Legal and ethical foundations).
25. Ultimately, making computing sustainable requires a collaborative effort spanning legislative changes, corporate circular models, and consumer behavioral shifts.

Inspired by Gay Gordon Burns' TED talk, You Deserve the Right to Repair Your Stuff, we're going to explore the major sustainability challenges facing the tech industry. Small electronics like smartphones, laptops and tablets are in every part of our lives, but their environmental footprint is anything but small. We'll discuss the materials used in electronics, the environmental impact of production and what can be done to make computing more sustainable. Let's get into it. Most people don't think about the environmental cost behind their smartphones or laptops, but creating these devices involves extracting finite resources, producing toxic waste and generating significant amounts of CO2. According to the Global E-Waste Monitor, the world generated 53.6 million metric tons of e-waste in 2019, and that number is climbing. That's like throwing out 4,500 Eiffel Towers worth of electronics each year. What's more, only 17% of that e-waste is properly recycled. The rest? It ends up in landfills, often in developing countries, where it can leach harmful chemicals into the environment. One of the biggest sustainability challenges in computing is the use of rare earth elements. These materials like neodymium, tantalum and cobalt are critical for manufacturing small electronics. They're used in everything from the magnets in your smartphone speakers to the batteries that power your laptop. The problem? Extracting these materials is highly resource-intensive and often leads to severe environmental damage. For example, mining rare earth elements in countries like China and the Democratic Republic of Congo has caused significant pollution and soil degradation. Mining for cobalt, used in lithium-ion batteries, also has a devastating human cost, with reports of child labour being a major issue in extraction sites. Let's talk about e-waste, one of the biggest sustainability challenges in tech. As consumers, we're upgrading our gadgets faster than ever. The average smartphone lifespan is just two years, and many laptops don't last much longer. When the devices reach the end of their life, they often end up in landfills, where toxic components like lead, mercury and cadmium can leak into the soil and groundwater. A report from the United Nations University stated that in 2019, e-waste generated $57 billion worth of recoverable precious metals, including gold and silver, but the vast majority of that material was lost because the devices weren't recycled properly. One of the biggest drivers of e-waste is planned obsolescence, a strategy used by manufacturers to design products with a limited lifespan, encouraging frequent upgrades. Whether it's slowing down old smartphones with software updates, or making devices with non-replaceable batteries, planned obsolescence is a huge sustainability problem. Gay Gordon Byrne emphasises this issue in her TED Talk. You deserve the right to repair your stuff, pointing out that companies deliberately make it difficult to repair or upgrade electronics. This forces consumers to buy new devices rather than extending the life of the ones they already have. This might be the reason I still use a desktop and Linux. I can upgrade incrementally without spending lots of money or throwing things away that might be useful. I also don't think my department head would be happy if I tried to buy a new machine every six months. The production of electronics is incredibly resource heavy. For example, creating a single smartphone uses 62 different metals, including precious metals like gold and rare earth elements. The manufacturing process requires large amounts of water, energy and chemicals. A 2016 report by the Green Electronics Council found that manufacturing a typical smartphone generates 85 kilograms of CO2. That's more than 10 times the weight of the device itself. The energy required to mine materials, transport components and assemble the product contributes significantly to climate change. We all want the newest gadgets, but at what cost? Fortunately, many tech companies are starting to address these sustainability challenges. Apple has committed to using 100% recycled aluminium in the construction of some of its products like the MacBook Air. They've also introduced recycling robots like Daisy, which disassemble old iPhones to recover valuable materials. Google has pledged to make all of its hardware products carbon neutral by 2030, and Microsoft is investing heavily in circular computing, aiming to be carbon negative by 2030. But while these initiatives are important steps forward, they only address part of the problem. Consumer behaviour and government policy also play a critical role. One important movement pushing back against planned obsolescence is the right to repair movement. Advocates argue that consumers should have the right to repair their own devices or have them repaired by third parties without voiding warranties or facing major barriers from manufacturers. In recent years, legislation in the European Union and states like California have aimed to give consumers more power to repair their electronics. Gay Gordon Brown is a leading voice in this movement, advocating for laws that make it easier to fix broken devices rather than forcing people to throw them away and buy new ones. One way to combat the environmental impact of electronics is through the use of sustainable materials and by creating a circular economy for electronics. Companies are starting to look at biodegradable plastics, recycled metals and even plant-based materials to reduce the environmental cost of production. For example, Fairphone is a company that builds modular smartphones designed to be easily repaired using responsibly sourced materials and recycled components. Their goal is to extend the life of devices and reduce the need for constant upgrades. I own an acoustic guitar made from sustainable wood so that I know that I'm not contributing to the problem. These sacrifices are often worth it to motivate consumer markets to accommodate customers. Consumers also have a huge role to play in making computing more sustainable. Recycling old electronics responsibly is one of the most impactful ways to reduce e-waste. Many tech companies are now offering take-back schemes where consumers can return old devices for recycling. But it's not just about recycling. Extending the life of your electronics through repair and upgrading rather than buying new devices can help reduce demand for resource-intensive manufacturing. How many of you have fixed some tech that belongs to a friend, a colleague or a family member? Feels good, doesn't it?

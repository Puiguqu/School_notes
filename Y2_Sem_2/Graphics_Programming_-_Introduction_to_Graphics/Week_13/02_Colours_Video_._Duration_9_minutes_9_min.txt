The study shifts from 2D primitives to understanding color representation in light and digital media. Physically, daylight is a mixture of colors, demonstrated by passing sunlight through a prism. This is because sunlight consists of light from different wavelengths, and the refraction angle varies with wavelength. Specifically, blue light, having a shorter wavelength, bends more, while red light, with a longer wavelength, bends less. Human vision relies on three types of photosensitive receptors: rods, cones, and intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells. Rods are primarily responsible for low-light vision but do not mediate color vision. Cones are essential for color vision and are generally more energy-intensive to activate. The eye contains approximately 4.5 million cone cells, compared to roughly 90 million rod cells. These cones come in three types: S-cones (short wavelength), M-cones (medium wavelength), and L-cones (long wavelength). The brain integrates signals from these receptors to perceive all visible colors. Digitally, screens use the principle of light mixing, represented by pixels. Each pixel is composed of three regions dedicated to red, green, and blue light. To reproduce colors, the screen turns on these three regions at varying intensities. Consequently, colors are programmatically described using the Red, Green, and Blue components, or location within a three-dimensional RGB space. For this digital system, the range of values for each primary channel is from 0 to 255. The combination of these channels approximates the visual spectrum, with maximum output in all three channels resulting in white. The industry standard color space is sRGB, co-created by HP and Microsoft, which defines a common, standardized range for screen and print display. While sRGB is crucial for cross-device consistency, it only utilizes a limited portion of the possible colors. To programmatically explore these colors, p5.js can use nested loops to map color values. For instance, using a nested loop `stroke(i, j, 0)` explores the plane formed by red and green components. Finding colors in raw RGB space is difficult, necessitating alternative, intuitive color spaces. A more usable system is the HSB (Hue, Saturation, Brightness) color model. Hue represents the local color, mapped onto a color wheel, with values from 0 to 360 degrees. Saturation defines the purity of the color, ranging from 0 to 100%. Brightness describes the color's intensity, also ranging from 0 to 100%. When implementing this in p5.js, the `colorMode` must be switched to `(HSB)`. The structure of the nested loops can then utilize the three parameters: `i` for hue (0-360), and `j` for saturation or brightness (0-100). Varying the hue on the x-axis while keeping saturation constant demonstrates the full color wheel. Adjusting the brightness component on the y-axis then shows the spectrum from bright to darker tones.

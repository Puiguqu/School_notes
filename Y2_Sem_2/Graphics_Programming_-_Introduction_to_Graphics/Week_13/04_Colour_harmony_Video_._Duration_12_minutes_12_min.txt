1. Harmony in color theory refers to the aesthetically pleasing combination of different colors, a concept employed in design, gaming, and web development.
2. The discussion introduces basic topics like complementary and triadic colors, advising viewers to research color theory for deeper understanding.
3. Complimentary colors create the strongest visual contrast when placed adjacent to one another.
4. These highly contrasting pairs, sometimes called opposite colors, are found at opposite ends of the HSB color wheel.
5. Technically, to find a complementary color when coding, one must pick a color at a specific hue angle and add 180 degrees to find its opposite.
6. Triadic colors, conversely, are three colors that are equally spaced around the color wheel.
7. Triadic color schemes, such as red, green, and blue, offer high contrast while maintaining a more balanced aesthetic than complementary pairs.
8. To effectively manipulate color space in code, it is necessary to change the color mode to HSB (Hue, Saturation, Brightness).
9. The procedural generation process often requires setting `noStroke` to prevent visible lines between shapes and defining the desired rectangle placement using `rectMode center`.
10. An example of color manipulation involves filling a primary shape with a color (e.g., red at hue 0, full saturation, full brightness).
11. To create a complementary pair, one could place a contrasting color, such as turquoise, within the initial red shape, demonstrating high contrast.
12. The mathematical structure for calculating triadic colors begins by defining a `colorDiff`, which is set as $360 / 3 = 120$ degrees.
13. If the starting color is `startColor` (e.g., 0 degrees), the three triadic hues are $0$, $0 + 120$, and $0 + 240$.
14. For procedural generation of color patterns, such as patterned bricks, the HSB mode is essential for structured traversal of the color space.
15. The process involves using nested, two-dimensional loops to place structural elements, like bricks, defined by `brickWidth` and `brickHeight`.
16. Initially, using `random(360)` for the hue, and `random(100)` for saturation and brightness, resulted in poorly unified and incoherent color patterns.
17. To achieve organic variation, the approach shifts to using a defined `hueStart` (e.g., 30 for sandstone orange) and restricting the hue variation.
18. A localized variation is managed by defining a random range: `hueRoot + randVar`, where `randVar` is calculated from a negative to positive variation range.
19. Realism is enhanced by restricting the saturation and brightness to a tighter range, such as 0-100 for saturation and 50-100 for brightness.
20. Limiting the color variation around a core hue ensures that the colors fit together organically, maintaining a unified theme suitable for game assets.
21. The procedural approach allows the creation of materials with natural, subtle variations, like the mud tones of a sandstone wall.
22. Alternatively, when designing a marine-themed scene, one could use a narrow range of hues centered around a blue value (e.g., near 200 degrees).
23. The key finding is that strict adherence to color harmony theory—rather than purely random values—provides visually appealing and functional color palettes.
24. This controlled variance is crucial for generating assets (like tiles or natural landscapes) that feel unified yet detailed.
25. The use of `colorMode HSB` facilitates this control, enabling developers to programmatically generate colors that adhere to specific harmonious relationships.

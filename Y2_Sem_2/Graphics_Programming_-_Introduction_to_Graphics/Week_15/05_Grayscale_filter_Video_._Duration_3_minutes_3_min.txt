Initially, the foundation for image manipulation was established using the `invertFilter` code structure. This existing framework serves as a template for programming the `grayscaleFilter`. Grayscale conversion is fundamentally the process of taking a full color image and reducing its color information to single, intensity-based values. Functionally, this involves implementing the same core logic structure, which means looping over every single pixel in the image.

The discussion details two primary methods for calculating a grayscale value. The simplest and quickest method is the mean average, which involves calculating the arithmetic average of the three primary color channels: $(\text{R} + \text{G} + \text{B}) / 3$. However, this simple mean average is technically inaccurate because human vision does not perceive all three color channels with equal sensitivity. To maintain the exact perceived brightness and fidelity of the original image, a weighted average calculation must be employed. This sophisticated technique is known as the luma calculation.

The correct luma formula assigns specific weights based on human visual perception. The resulting weighted average formula is: $\text{gray} = \text{r} \times 0.299 + \text{g} \times 0.587 + \text{b} \times 0.114$. The weights reflect that green (G) has the highest importance (0.587), followed by red (R) (0.299), and blue (B) has the least impact (0.114).

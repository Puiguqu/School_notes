1. Computer vision is defined as an interdisciplinary field that develops techniques enabling computers to make intelligent assertions about digital images and video.
2. Unlike simple data acquisition—such as merely taking images from a camera—CV requires the processing of data to allow the computer to make decisions.
3. In this context, "decision" means transforming visual images into descriptions of the world that can interface with other thought processes and elicit appropriate action.
4. Examples of CV tasks include determining if faces are present and locating them in an image, or analyzing if a person is smiling or surprised.
5. The foundational challenge of computer vision has been recognized since 1966 by pioneers like Marvin Minsky, illustrating that the problem remains complex even today.
6. Historically, scientists have often misclassified the difficulty of problems, once believing that challenges related to senses (like hearing or vision) were simpler than those involving higher brain functions.
7. The complexity of CV is highlighted by the fact that while Deep Blue beat the world chess champion, CV systems lagged far behind in equivalent human vision tasks.
8. Human vision is inherently automatic, occurring so fast that failure in even a small circuit can impair understanding, as seen in conditions like prosopagnosia (face blindness).
9. CV tasks are fundamentally used to identify the unique superiority of human ability over machines.
10. The process of vision begins with the signal reaching the brain, as the image captured by the retina is considered low quality.
11. Consequently, it is the brain, not merely the eye, that facilitates the process of seeing.
12. Multiple circuits analyze the signal at various levels, including specialized circuits for detecting vertical lines, horizontal lines, and movements.
13. The human brain builds a robust world model by combining signals from multiple sources, such as vision, the soles of the feet, and the ears.
14. Furthermore, human vision is not immediately innate; babies are born almost blind, requiring years of training to fully execute adult vision tasks.
15. The brain also possesses a critical attention system that filters a deluge of visual information, allowing focus on relevant details while discarding irrelevant data.
16. The transcript notes that, technically, any raw math data or image array consists only of pixels containing numbers.
17. Crucially, without special programming, a machine is incapable of making any semantic assertion about an image.
18. The entire field of computer vision emerged specifically to fill the critical gap for this necessary programming.

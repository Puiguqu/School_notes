Computer vision (CV) is an expansive and rapidly developing field of technology. This discipline enables systems to process and interpret visual data from cameras and other sources. One of its early applications is character recognition, where scanning algorithms read and sort destination addresses. Another critical use case is autonomous vehicles, which rely on CV to detect road boundaries, traffic, and pedestrians. CV is also central to medical imaging, providing non-invasive visual representations of internal body structures. These techniques aid in the diagnosis and treatment of diseases by revealing internal abnormalities. Face recognition technology, a mature CV subfield, is now reliable for consumer products and office security systems. Furthermore, CV is used in sports analytics, automatically collecting detailed player performance metrics. For instance, tracking a football player involves monitoring passes, shots, and distances across multiple cameras (up to 16 at a single stadium). The fundamental goal of developing CV systems is extracting information and data from live camera footage by processing individual pixels. The industry standard tool for such advanced work is OpenCV (Open Computer Vision). Launched in 1999 by Intel, OpenCV was initially designed for CPU-intensive CV applications. It is an open-source library containing decades of advanced CV implementations. Technically, OpenCV is written in C++ to ensure maximum processing speed. However, its portability is achieved through bindings and wrappers for multiple languages. Users can therefore access this functionality using Python, Java, and Matlab.

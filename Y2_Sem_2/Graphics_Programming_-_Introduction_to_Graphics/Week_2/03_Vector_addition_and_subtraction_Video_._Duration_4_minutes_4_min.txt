The core concept presented involves using vectors for movement and interaction within a graphical environment. A vector is composed of components, specifically X and Y, defining direction and magnitude. Vector addition is fundamentally achieved by summing the individual corresponding components of two vectors. For example, adding vector $\vec{A}$ (with components 5 and 2) and vector $\vec{B}$ (with components 3 and 4) results in vector $\vec{C}$, calculated as $C_x = 5 + 3 = 8$ and $C_y = 2 + 4 = 6$. In animated graphics, adding velocity to a current location yields the new location in the subsequent frame. This process of updating location (New Location = Location + Velocity) is crucial for simulating movement over time.

Vector subtraction follows a similar principle to addition, but instead of summing components, one set of components is taken out of the other. Subtraction is particularly important for dealing with physical forces, such as friction. A practical example of subtraction involves calculating the difference between the mouse's coordinates and the canvas's center point. Programmatically, if `mouse` stores coordinates (mouseX, mouseY) and `center` is calculated as (width/2, height/2), the subtraction operation is executed as `mouse.sub(center)`. The resulting line segment drawn on the canvas thus represents the distance from the canvas center to the current mouse position.

Conceptually, subtraction is the inverse operation of addition within the vector space. If the new location is defined by Location + Velocity, then subtracting the old Location from the new Location yields the Velocity vector. Furthermore, subtracting the old location from the new location calculates the required distance or "steps" needed to traverse between the two points.

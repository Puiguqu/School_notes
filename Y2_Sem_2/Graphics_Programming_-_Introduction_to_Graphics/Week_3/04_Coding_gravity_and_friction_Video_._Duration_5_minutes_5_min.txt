# Coding gravity and friction

. Duration: 5 minutes
5 min - Summary

URL: https://www.coursera.org/learn/uol-graphics-programming/lecture/ysAaY/coding-gravity-and-friction

1. The video session introduces the concept of inertia and examines how to simulate forces, beginning with basic gravity.
2. Gravity is defined as the natural phenomenon by which all things possessing mass are attracted to one another.
3. On Earth, gravity is the force that gives weight to physical objects and causes them to fall downwards.
4. In the P5 environment, gravity is technically represented as a force vector of size (0, 0.1) added to the acceleration.
5. To implement custom gravity, the force is applied using the function `ball.applyforce(gravity)`.
6. While applying this force successfully makes the simulated ball bounce up and down, the resulting simulation lacks realism.
7. The main issue identified is the absence of friction, which is the force resisting the relative motion of sliding objects.
8. Friction is conceptualized as a smaller force acting in the opposite direction of motion, which slows down the object.
9. To code friction, one must calculate the opposite vector to the object's current velocity vector.
10. The process involves obtaining the velocity vector, calculating its opposite, and then scaling it.
11. A crucial component is the friction coefficient, which dictates the amount of friction between surfaces.
12. This coefficient must be scaled by the opposite velocity vector to create the friction force.
13. For simplifying the simulation, a standard friction coefficient of 0.01 is used, assuming uniform surface resistance.
14. The technical implementation begins by copying the current velocity: `ball.velocity.copy`.
15. The code then determines the opposing force by multiplying the velocity by -1.
16. To ensure correct magnitude, the resulting vector must be normalized using `friction.normalize()`.
17. This normalized vector is then scaled by the required friction coefficient (0.01).
18. The final opposing force is applied to the object using the function `applyforce(friction)`.
19. Running this corrected code demonstrates that the ball slowly loses energy as it bounces due to air and ground friction.
20. The simulation now incorporates multiple accumulated forces acting on the ball.
21. These forces include the constant gravitational pull and the opposing force of friction.
22. It is vitally important to remember that in every frame, the sum of the acceleration must be recalculated.
23. The `applyforce` function accumulates these various forces acting on the object.
24. This multi-force accumulation mechanism is key to creating a realistic physics simulation.
25. Ultimately, the successful combination of gravity and friction provides a far more complex and accurate model of physical movement.

# Introduction to generative art and design

Generative art is defined as art created, in whole or in part, by an autonomous system, which dictates features otherwise requiring explicit decisions from the artist. This field merges two seemingly disparate worlds: the structured precision of programming and the subjectivity of art. While programming involves precise, logical tasks using a language interface, generative art uses strict processes subversively to create unpredictable, expressive work. Conceptually, a generative artist acts like a gardener, setting the rules and conditions, while the resulting work emerges from a collaboration between the designer and the system. To produce such forms, designers use logical decisions and mathematical formulas, allowing the art to "grow" rather than being assembled conventionally. Generative content emerges from the collaboration between the programmer and the machine, achieving an organic look through mechanical means. This process allows creators to explore the full gamut of options without being limited by personal taste bias. Furthermore, generative techniques are vital for creating fluid, non-repeating artworks that can be generated independently, such as unique patterns. Procedural generation, a key application, allows computer systems to create quasi-random content, such as randomizing map layouts in *Civilization* or generating entire worlds in *Minecraft* and *No Man's Sky*. A well-built content generation system can thus provide unlimited content, preventing players from merely learning the game through memory. Generative techniques also influence fabrication, where digital objects are used with technologies like 3D printers, CNC mills, or laser cutters. The course curriculum structure demonstrates this technical focus, covering foundational weeks like Vectors and Animation (Week 2) and Forces/Physics using *Matter.js* (Weeks 3 and 4). Advanced generative topics include the introduction of Perlin noise and the study of Sine and Fractals (Weeks 8 and 9). Later modules delve into computational imaging, covering Colour, Images, Pixels (Weeks 13 and 14) and comprehensive Image Processing (Weeks 15 and 16). Crucial later topics involve advanced technical processes such as Computer Vision, which is broken down into three distinct parts across Weeks 17, 18, and 19. Overall, the curriculum progression culminates in the application of randomness and noise functions, solidifying the mastery of these powerful generative tools.

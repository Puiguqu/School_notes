The foundational concepts begin with trigonometry, which is crucial for solving various geometric and physical problems.
In a right triangle, defined by a 90-degree angle, the relationships between angles and side lengths are established.
If theta is an acute angle, the sine of theta is defined as the ratio of the opposite side length to the length of the hypotenuse ($\sin(\theta) = \text{Opposite} / \text{Hypotenuse}$).
Similarly, the cosine of theta is the ratio of the adjacent side to the hypotenuse ($\cos(\theta) = \text{Adjacent} / \text{Hypotenuse}$).
Finally, the tangent of theta is the ratio of the opposite side to the adjacent side ($\tan(\theta) = \text{Opposite} / \text{Adjacent}$).
This fundamental relationship can be memorized using the mnemonic SOHCAHTOA, which encapsulates all the required mathematical logic.
Understanding these ratios reveals a critical, non-linear relationship: the angle theta directly influences the corresponding lengths of the opposite and adjacent sides.
These trigonometric calculations are highly useful for modeling dynamic motion, particularly objects moving in circular paths, like pendulums or springs.

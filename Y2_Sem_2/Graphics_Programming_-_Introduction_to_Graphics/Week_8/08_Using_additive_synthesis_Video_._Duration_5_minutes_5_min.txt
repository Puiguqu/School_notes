The session provides a deep dive into generative techniques, specifically focusing on additive synthesis for creating complex wave patterns. Additive synthesis involves combining multiple simple waves—such as sine waves—to generate intricate visual and auditory effects. To begin, a basic wave is visualized by defining vertices using code, setting the range from $x=0$ to $x=\text{width}$. The initial visualization of a wave uses a formula like $x \cdot \sin(\text{coordinate}) \cdot (\text{height} / 4)$ to create a foundational wave structure. For movement, the calculation must incorporate time dependence, typically by adding a term like $+\text{frameCount}$ to the coordinate variable.

The concept of additive synthesis is demonstrated by layering waves, where a second wave (Wave 2) is added on top of the first (Wave 1). Wave 2 is shown to have a smaller amplitude and higher frequency, requiring separate scaling (e.g., dividing by 20) for distinct visualization. This combined process allows for the preservation of the large movements of Wave 1 while adding smaller, superimposed details from Wave 2. To add "magic," a third component, noise (Wave 3), is incorporated, which is itself treated as a wave function.

The noise function requires specific scaling to prevent overly random output; thus, values are often divided by a large constant (e.g., 10) and multiplied by $\text{frameCount}$ (e.g., $\text{frameCount}/100 \cdot 100$). Combining Wave 1, Wave 2, and Wave 3 demonstrates the final, richly textured composition, where the noise gives an organic, fluid quality.

However, cartesian coordinates have a positive y axis heading up and not down, while our screen coordinates have positive y values going down. For the moment, this difference is insignificant, what's important is that everything in the 2D Cartesian coordinate space, can be described in terms of two parameters, x and y, which represent movement on two axes or dimensions. There is however, a different way of thinking about the location of a point in 2D space, instead of using the x and the y coordinates, we can think of position in terms of polar coordinates. In the polar coordinate system, each point on a plane is determined by distance from the origin, and an angle from the polar axis. The distance from the origin is called the radial coordinate, radial distance or simply radius, and the angle is called the polar angle. The radial coordinate is often denoted by r, and the angle by theta. Angles in polar notation are generally expressed in either degrees or radians, with 2 pi radians being equal to 360 degrees. Why do we need this alternative coordinate system? Polar coordinates are most appropriate in any context, where the phenomenon being considered is inherently tied to the direction, and length from a center point. The Archimedean spiral, for example, is much simpler to express using polar forums. In addition, many physical systems such as those concerned with bodies moving around the central point, are simpler and more intuitive to model using polar coordinates. For example, it's easier to model the movement of planets around the sun with polar coordinates, than by using a Cartesian coordinate system. Okay, so now we know that we can describe everything with radial coordinates, using an angle and a radius. However in a P5 world, all the primitives take Cartesian coordinates, they don't take polar ones. If we wanted to draw an ellipse at a particular location, we would have to provide an x and a y coordinate. This is where the trigonometry we looked at earlier comes in handy, we saw that the sine of angle theta is equal to the opposite side, divided by the hypotenuse. In this case, the opposite side is effectively the Y coordinate of point x, y, and the hypotenuse is radius r. So sin theta, is equal to y over r. Now we don't know what y is, that's what we're trying to figure out. So we move that to the left of our equation, and we move all the values we know to the right. That means that y equals sin theta times r, which is the third equation in this list. So this is the formula for calculating the y coordinate when given the polar coordinates of a point. Similarly, for calculating the x coordinate, we will use the cosine of the angle along with the radius, the cosine is equal to the adjacent side, which in this case is x over the hypotenuse, which is equal to r. When we solve for x, we get cosine of theta times r.

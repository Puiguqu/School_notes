In radial space, an object drawn at angle zero degrees will appear in the same place as an object at 360 degrees, 720 degrees, and so on. That leads to a very useful property in the values that sine and cosine functions return. Have a look at the sine function plotted here. Notice how the output of the sine function is a smooth curve alternating between negative 1 and 1. This is known as oscillation. A bouncing ball, a pendulum, and a guitar string, all exhibit the same type of oscillating motion. We can now treat the sine function as a number generator, much like we did with the noise function in earlier lectures. This time however, the values returned will be from negative 1-1, and the shape of the graph will be the smooth sequence of nonlinear values. The wave that is formed by plotting the sine function has an amplitude and a period. Amplitude is the height of the wave, and in our picture world, it is the distance from the center of motion to either extreme. That distance here. The period is the amount of time it takes to complete one cycle of motion, two seconds. Period is linked to frequency. Frequency is the number of cycles in a unit of time. Say, the unit of time is one second. So in this diagram, the period is a quarter of a second and the frequency is four. In other words, we can fit four cycles in a second. Frequency is measured in hertz. So we would say that the sine wave has a frequency of four hertz. In this image, the waves have the same amplitude, but each of them has a different frequency. The top one has the lowest frequency or the smallest number of cycles per unit of time, while the bottom one has the highest frequency or the highest number of cycles per unit of time. Finally, phase in electronic signaling is the definition of the position of a point in time on the waveform cycle. Say, whole cycle is 360 degrees. A phase denotes where the object is in the cycle. Is it at 10 degrees, 90 degrees, 180 degrees?

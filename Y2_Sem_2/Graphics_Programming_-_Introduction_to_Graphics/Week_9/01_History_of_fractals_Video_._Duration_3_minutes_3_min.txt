This text introduces fractals, an advanced topic in procedural generation of graphics, and outlines a comprehensive graphics curriculum. A fractal is defined as a rough or fragmented geometric shape where smaller parts are approximately reduced-size copies of the whole structure. The term "fractal" was coined by Benoit Mandelbrot, who mathematically defined this natural phenomenon, drawing from the Latin word *fractus*, meaning broken or fractured. Historically, the concepts were known, but Mandelbrot's work established them as mathematical masterpieces. Fractals are ubiquitous in nature, observed in structures like clouds, mountains, river formations, and on the surface of the moon. They are also visible in biological systems, such as the leaves of plants, the branching patterns of trees, the spiral structure of seashells like the nautilus, and the network of blood vessels.

Nature's complexity often defies classical mathematical description, particularly for patterns like mountain ranges or tree growth. Mandelbrot revolutionized this field by introducing a new geometry model, demonstrating that mathematics could indeed describe these natural structures. The core principle enabling this is the concept of self-similarity. Using fractal geometry formulas allows for the detailed description of the natural world, suggesting an underlying order amidst apparent chaos. These advanced principles quickly moved into practical applications, completely transforming the creation of special effects in the film industry. They are now used everywhere complexity in textures, surfaces, or materials is needed.

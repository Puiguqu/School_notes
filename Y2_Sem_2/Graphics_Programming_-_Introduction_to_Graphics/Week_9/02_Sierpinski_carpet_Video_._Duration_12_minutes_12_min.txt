The Sierpinski carpet is a planar fractal, originally described by Waclaw Sierpinski in 1916. Its construction begins by taking a square and dividing it into nine congruent sub-squares, subsequently removing the central subsquare. This recursive process is then applied indefinitely to the remaining eight sub-squares. This fundamental technique of recursive subdivision and removal can extend to other shapes, such as the Sierpinski triangle, formed by dividing an equilateral triangle into four and removing the middle section. In three dimensions, a similar recursive construction based on tubes is known as the Menger sponge. To program the Sierpinski carpet, the initial size of the Canvas should be set as a power of three. This requirement ensures that the side length remains divisible by three when undergoing repeated division down to the pixel level.

The core of the programmatic approach relies on recursion, where a function calls itself to perform progressively smaller tasks. The recursion must have a defined end-condition, which in this case is reached when the side length is less than one pixel. Within the function, the side size is immediately divided by three, implementing the core geometric scaling. The drawing process involves setting up the canvas and using transformations like `translate` to move the drawing origin. The function then calls itself recursively for the eight non-removed positions (e.g., left, above-diagonally, right-above, etc.).

The logic demonstrates that the entire complex fractal structure is generated by simple repeated commands: moving to a new translated position, and then calling the function again. The system's complexity is immense, requiring a massive number of function calls, potentially reaching hundreds of thousands (e.g., around 300,000 calls). This high computational detail highlights how simple code, coupled with recursion, can generate intricate structures down to the pixel level.

Historically, these fractals found practical application in the 1990s by Nathan Cohen, a radio astronomer. He utilized fractal mathematics to design highly effective antennas with a snowflake-like shape. Fractal designs offer the technological breakthrough of making antennas significantly smaller while simultaneously enabling them to receive a much wider range of frequencies. This capability was crucial for wireless communication devices handling multiple services like Bluetooth and Wi-Fi. The Sierpinski carpet itself has been utilized in this manner, etched onto circuit boards. Ultimately, the widespread use of fractal designs suggests that these geometric patterns provide optimal solutions often modeled by natural selection.
